# Phase 6 — World Depth

**Prerequisite:** Phase 1 complete. Can run concurrently with Phases 3, 4, and 5.

**What this phase does:** Fills the historical, geographic, and cultural depth that makes Concordia feel like it existed before the game starts and will continue after it ends. The district designs are solid; this phase adds the wider world those districts exist within.

---

## Design Principles for This Phase

### The world should have existed without the player

Every major location in FNV was designed to feel like it predated the Courier's arrival. Novac has a history that doesn't begin when the player shows up. The Hub has ongoing commerce that would continue if the Vault Dweller had turned back. This principle scales to world-building: Inner Tepenia's pre-war cities, the Long Night War, the Falkland Treaty — these should feel like things that happened, fully and specifically, not things that were sketched in to give the player context.

The test: could you write a short story set in Fort McMurdo in 2701 that has nothing to do with the player character and feels like it's set in a real place? If yes, the world is developed enough. If no, it needs more work.

### History should produce friction, not just texture

World-building that exists only to be interesting is atmosphere. World-building that produces active tension in the present tense is design. The Long Night War should produce present-tense friction: survivors who disagree about what happened and why; districts whose current dynamics were shaped by what side they took; factions whose existence is a reaction to the war's aftermath. Pre-war city cultural sketches should produce things the player encounters — attitudes, prejudices, loyalties, foods, music, myths — not just facts about the past.

### Unanswered questions are assets

Not everything needs to be resolved. Some of what happened in the Long Night War should be contested or unknown — different factions have different histories of the same events. The Falkland Treaty's real content is a GDD decision (is there an in-world text, and does the player find it?), but the ambiguity around what was actually agreed can be a designed quality of the world rather than an incomplete design.

## 6.1 — Pre-War City Cultural Sketches

**Priority: High. These cities are referenced in backstories, questlines, and character histories.**

Five cities need cultural sketches: Fort McMurdo, Janbogo (additional depth beyond existing core facts), Belgrano, Neumayer, Mirny. The station-to-city mapping already exists; the cultural identity doesn't.

### What a cultural sketch contains

For each city:
- **Geographic and environmental character** — what does the physical environment impose? Antarctic cities built around different micro-climates, resource profiles, or geographic features will develop different cultures.
- **Historical origin** — what was this settlement before the Long Night War? A research station? A military base? A transit hub? The origin shapes the culture.
- **Population composition** — human/Doll ratio, dominant nationalities or cultural backgrounds (given the geopolitical history, some Antarctic settlements will be predominantly from specific former national cultures)
- **Economy and function** — what does this city produce or provide?
- **Social texture** — what does daily life look and feel like? What do people argue about? What do they take for granted?
- **Relationship to Concordia** — is this city allied with, in tension with, or indifferent to Concordia? Does it trade? Does it trade in forbidden things?
- **Long Night War legacy** — what happened to this city during the war? What did it lose? What shape did its survival take?
- **2-3 distinctive cultural markers** — specific things that make this city's culture recognizable: a food, a practice, an attitude, a myth

### Janbogo specifically

Janbogo has core facts but no cultural depth. Given its narrative significance (it's the district where the Veilkeepers operate, and it's associated with the Arcanet black archives), it deserves more development than the other four cities. Write the full cultural sketch plus the Subnet Nexus integration (from Phase 1, task 1.5, which should already be resolved).

### Fort McMurdo

The name suggests a connection to the real-world McMurdo Station — the largest Antarctic research station, built and operated primarily by the United States. In Inner Tepenia's world, the geopolitical context has changed dramatically, but the site's history as a center of Antarctic presence makes it significant. What is Fort McMurdo in 2786? A military city? A research city? A city that was both and is now something more complicated?

## 6.2 — Long Night War: Parameters

**Priority: High. Impacts character backstories, NPC dialogue, faction histories, ending slides.**

The Long Night War is referenced everywhere and defined precisely nowhere. "Iconic events" are documented but the parameters are undefined.

**Parameters needed:**
1. **Combatants** — who fought whom? This is the most important parameter and the most carefully avoided answer in the GDD. The reluctance may be intentional (the ambiguity is the point) or the question has simply never been forced. Either way, it needs an answer, even if that answer is "the official history says X fought Y, but the actual combatant roster was more complicated."

2. **Duration** — approximately when did it begin and end? Context: the robot personhood timeline runs 2318–2564; the Falkland Treaty is a post-war consequence. The Long Night War should fit in the timeline relative to these anchors.

3. **End condition** — what ended it? Military defeat? Resource exhaustion? A negotiated settlement (the Falkland Treaty)? The way a war ends shapes the peace more than anything else.

4. **Scale of destruction** — what was lost? The "Long Night" name suggests significant destruction of Antarctic infrastructure, possibly including light sources or energy systems. The pre-war city sketches (task 6.1) should be consistent with whatever scale is established here.

5. **Robot combatant status** — were Dolls combatants in the Long Night War? On which side(s)? This has enormous implications for the robot experience within the game world — robots who fought in a war have a different relationship to their own existence than robots who were non-combatant.

### The ambiguity question

Some of these parameters can be deliberately ambiguous — the war is far enough in the past that contested histories are credible. But the GDD needs to know the truth (even if the game's characters don't). The creator should have a settled answer to each parameter question, even if that answer is "officially unknown, with specific claimants making specific conflicting claims."

## 6.3 — Amundsen Tower: Destruction Specifics

**Priority: Medium-high. Referenced in multiple quest contexts; defines the world's largest single loss.**

What weapon was used? When specifically? What was lost in the destruction beyond the physical structure? Who witnessed it? The tower's destruction is the world's definitive catastrophic event — it should be as specifically designed as the event merits.

The Echoes of Amundsen faction (whether kept, redesigned, or cut per Blocking Decision 1) is organized around the loss of this tower. The tower's destruction specifics should be canonical before the faction's meaning is fully designed.

---

## 6.4 — Falkland Treaty: In-World Text

**Priority: Medium. Historical document; the research for it has been done.**

A draft in-world text. This doesn't need to be legally precise — it needs to feel like a real document that real parties signed under real pressure, with specific clauses that are legible to the game's current-day characters as either a triumph, a betrayal, or an inadequate compromise depending on their position.

The Veilkeepers (information control faction) have a specific relationship to the Treaty's content — some of what the Treaty established may be what they're protecting. The Treaty Scholars (minor group) are organized around studying it. These downstream uses give the Treaty text real GDD work to do beyond historical texture.

---

## 6.5 — War of Upper Earth: Complete the Record

**Priority: Medium. Currently ~60% documented.**

The remaining 40% needs to be identified and completed. Pull the existing document, mark what's TBD, and design the missing portions. The War of Upper Earth established the political and physical landscape that produced the Antarctic exodus — it's the backstory to the backstory, and its incompleteness leaves gaps in character backstories that were written assuming it existed.

---

## 6.6 — Historical Figures: In-Game Presence

**Priority: Medium-low. Atmospheric depth work.**

Several historically significant figures exist in the world history: Gyeong-ja Yun (the case that produced the 2318 robot personhood recognition), the figures involved in the Falkland Treaty negotiations, specific Long Night War figures. These should have a defined in-game presence — how does the player encounter them, even in a world 200+ years after their time? Terminals, archives, portraits, NPC dialogue references, faction mythology.

The Gyeong-ja Yun case specifically is listed in the quest layer (Phase 5 task 5.8) as something that needs in-game presence. The historical record here feeds that quest hook.
