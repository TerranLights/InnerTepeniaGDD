# Phase 3 — Characters

**Prerequisite:** Phase 1 complete (district names, stat system settled). Phase 2 begun (not necessarily complete; but faction identities need to be at least drafted, since some character personalities are shaped by their faction relationships).

**Parallel with:** Phase 4 (Perks). Both can be worked simultaneously once Phase 2 is underway.

**What this phase does:** Completes all named characters whose design is currently partial or absent. The goal is that every character in the GDD — scaffold characters, TBN characters, companions with gaps — ends this phase with enough design to hand to a writer who has never seen the GDD and produce voice-acted dialogue.

---

## Character Design Standard

A character is complete when a writer who has never met this character can:
- Write any scene they appear in with confidence
- Write their reaction to any world event
- Write their dialogue in any register (light, emotional, confrontational, curious)
- Know what they would never say

This requires: a personality description (not traits, but how they think and what they're organized around), a voice (how they speak, what their sentence rhythms are, what they avoid), a set of core convictions and at least one blind spot, a history that explains why they are the way they are, and a role in the fiction that means something beyond quest-giver.

The 44 district Quest NPCs are already written to this standard (see any of the District-Quest-NPCs files). Use them as the benchmark.

## 3.1 — Complete the Gap Characters: Ji-Eun Kim and Calethina

**Priority: Ji-Eun Kim is high and immediate; Calethina is high but deliberately deferred.**

### Ji-Eun Kim

Ji-Eun Kim has a profile and backstory but incomplete personality and an incomplete questline. Her questline is the first thread of the Calethina arc — it cannot be completed until Blocking Decision 3 (Calethina's passive link mechanic) is resolved.

**What to do now:** Write Ji-Eun Kim's personality and voice without completing the questline. She is a complete character who appears in contexts beyond just the Calethina questline — she exists in the world before the questline begins. Her personality can be written independently of how the questline resolves.

**What to defer:** The specific questline beats that depend on the passive link mechanic definition. These stay deferred until Decision 3 is resolved.

### Calethina

Calethina is the most important character in the game and the most intentionally incomplete. Her questline is deliberately deferred. Her backstory is not yet written. Her personality is not yet written.

**What to do in this phase:** Write Calethina's backstory. The questline can stay deferred; the backstory should not. Understanding who Calethina is and how she came to be singular should be written and settled before any more of the game's story documents are produced, because she is referenced in almost everything.

Her backstory precedes the questline. The archive-narrator direction is established and should be the starting constraint: she speaks to the player through a specific register. What made her that way? What happened during her transition from the distributed predecessor-system to singular Calethina? Who was she, in the early period of her singularity, before the Long Night War? The Pisces Dying Robot NPC has pre-war sensory memories of Calethina — this is a design constraint. What Calethina was like in the early 2630s should be consistent with what that NPC would have encountered.

**What to continue deferring:** The questline beats, the passive link mechanic specifics, the reconciliation of the five-step vs. personality-download designs.

## 3.2 — Scaffold Characters: Complete Personality and Voice

**Priority: High. These characters are blocking companion perk design and DLC planning.**

Five scaffold characters have backstory but no personality or voice. This is the minimum viable design gap — the backstory is the skeleton, the personality is the body.

The approach is the same for all five: read the backstory, ask what kind of person this backstory would produce, and write the personality from that position. The standard is the same as 3.1: a writer who has never met this character should be able to write them in any scene.

### Kendra Heinrich (DLC 1 companion)

Kendra is slotted for DLC 1 (South Pole). Her personality doesn't need to be calibrated to a main game district context — she's a DLC character with her own geographic context. However, her personality should be designed in Phase 3 even if the DLC questline is Phase 7 work, because she may appear as a named presence before the DLC (in references, in overheard conversations, etc.). Note: if the user decides DLC 1 is not a priority, Kendra's personality design can slide to Phase 7 as well. Discuss with creator.

### Meyzan Yocazhda

Meyzan's district is marked ⚠️ (tentative/conflicting). Before writing personality, resolve the district question. District shapes personality in this world — where someone is from and what they're adapted to is constitutive. Check all documents that reference Meyzan, determine which district reference is correct, canonize it, and then write the personality from that foundation.

### Michelle Stanton

Michelle has a complete backstory and a confirmed district. Her personality can be designed immediately. She should be prioritized.

### Salagéa Aparast

**Confirmed DLC 5 (Atlantic Coastal Region) — moves to Phase 7.** Not part of main game character completion work. Her personality and questline design begin in Phase 7 once the DLC's central conflict and thematic contribution are established.

### Vosora Lashár Tanslock

Vosora has a complete backstory and a confirmed district. Design alongside Michelle Stanton.

## 3.3 — TBN Characters: Names and Core Identities

**Priority: Medium-high. These characters have partial contextual development but no personality.**

Eleven TBN characters exist in the GDD — referenced in questlines, mentioned in other characters' histories, or appearing in early design passes with image references. They need names and core personalities.

The naming should be consistent with the character's district of origin and cultural context (Inner Tepenia's world has specific naming conventions tied to the geopolitical history documented in the world history reference).

**Characters with existing questlines (design personality around the questline):**
- TBN [FW-25 Pink Lucy] — Leo entertainer; questline written. Her personality should be deduced backward from the questline: what kind of person does this questline make sense for?
- TBN [IT-021 white shirt Fenny] — Taurus questline written. Same approach.
- TBN [TCY-06 red-dress Palmer City Elva] — Star War house established. Same approach.
- TBN [TCY-20 unimpressed bartender Miranda] — The Quiet Shift. Design personality that fits both the bartender context and The Quiet Shift's night-worker philosophy.
- TBN [TCY-25 smoldering darkness Rui] — Scorpio-adjacent. Design personality consistent with Scorpio's district culture.
- TBN [TCY-42 ravishing extravagant Lillian] — Legacy Star War house. Design alongside TCY-06.
- TBN [XT-03 Mei-Li] — The Found/Assembled. Strong thematic constraint: she is an Assembled Doll with a specific identity politics implication.
- TBN [XT-17 Charlene] — Aquarius. Design personality consistent with Aquarius's district culture.
- TBN [XT-21 Angelina] — Hub. The Hub is the most politically complex district; her personality should reflect that context.

**Characters with no established context (design from scratch):**
- TBN [FR-03 billiards Maria] — Everything TBD. The billiards detail is the only hook. What kind of person is she, why does she play billiards, what is her district?
- TBN [TCY-45 heavenly summertime Momo] — Everything TBD. Start from the name impression and build.

## 3.4 — Upper Earth Defectors: Motivation Framework and Exemplars

**Priority: Medium. Blocks Defectors questline (Phase 5).**

The Upper Earth Defectors folder exists and is empty. Before individual Defector NPCs can be designed, the motivational framework needs to exist: why do people defect from Upper Earth to Concordia, what kinds of people do this, what do they lose and what do they gain, how are they received in Concordia?

The framework document should establish 3-5 distinct defector archetypes (not individual characters, but types). Then 2-3 individual exemplar characters should be designed to fill the most important archetype slots — these become the named Defectors the player can actually meet.

The Defectors questline and the Upper Earth Defectors as a world-building presence are separate things. The framework enables both.
