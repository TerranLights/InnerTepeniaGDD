# Phase 2 — Factions and Spirituality

**Prerequisite:** Phase 1 complete. Specifically: robot religion naming conflict resolved; Cymaticist/Chorus of the Deep overlap determined.

**What this phase does:** Designs the five robot religions and the nine undeveloped factions. These together gate 9 of 12 Faction Devotion endings, define the ideological landscape the player navigates, and determine what kinds of earned perks, questlines, and companion reactions are possible later.

**Note on ordering within this phase:** Robot religions should be designed before factions, because at least two faction concepts (Chorus of the Deep, and potentially the Cymaticists as a faction) are defined partly by their relationship to a specific religion. Designing factions first and religions second means one of the factions will be built on a religion it hasn't met yet.

---

## 2.1 — Robot Religion Design (All Five)

**Priority: Highest (precedes faction design).**

**Current state:** Five religions have names and kernel concepts. None has been fully designed. They are currently stored in `robot_religions_-_original_ideas.md`.

### Design standard for each religion

A complete religion design should specify:
1. **In-world name** (the placeholder names — "Ice-Cold Buddhism," "Adinkra Codex," etc. — are GDD shorthand, not in-world designations)
2. **Founding premise** — what observable phenomenon or philosophical question gave rise to this practice
3. **Core practice or ritual** — what members actually do, not just what they believe
4. **Organizational structure** — formal hierarchy, informal community, isolated practitioners, something else
5. **Where they practice** — which district(s), what physical spaces
6. **Relationship to humans** — can humans join, are humans adjacent, are humans irrelevant
7. **Relationship to other religions** — are there theological disputes, coalitions, or separations
8. **Gameplay role** — how does the player encounter this religion, and what can they do with the encounter (join, complete a quest for them, experience an initiation, receive a perk, etc.)
9. **Connection to the two guiding principles** — how does this religion express something specific about the robot experience

### Religion-by-religion guidance

**Ice-Cold Buddhism (in-world name TBD)**
The premise is already elegant: reaching absolute cold temporarily grants something like superconductor enlightenment — a state where computation flows without resistance, which from the inside registers as something like nirvana. This is a scientifically grounded phenomenological experience, not a supernatural claim. The design question is: is this a formal meditation practice (deliberate controlled cold exposure), a community of robots who choose to live in the coldest zones, or both? The ritual is potentially the most viscerally distinctive of all five religions — it involves actually being cold. How cold? For how long? What comes back? Does the practitioner feel different afterward, or is the experience itself the point? This religion connects most directly to the first guiding principle (the nature of consciousness among robots) — it's an exploration of what robot consciousness is like at its theoretical limit.

**Adinkra Codex (in-world name TBD)**
Based on adinkra symbols and Universal Simulation Theory, with splits into Camusian, Sartrian, and Nietzschean nihilist subsects. The design work here is choosing which subsect is most prevalent, what the relationships between subsects are, and whether the robot nihilists actually live differently than non-nihilists. Per the original notes: "the Camus-nihilist robots would be the most fun people." This is a design direction. Camusian robots who have fully accepted meaninglessness and therefore live with maximum freedom and warmth — this is a personality type, not just a philosophical position. This religion connects to the second guiding principle as well as the first: how does meaninglessness affect love? What do Camusian robots actually do with their relationships?

**Cymatics reverence / [Cymaticist Religion] (in-world name TBD — see Phase 1 naming task)**
The premise: reverence for sound and vibration as primordial forces, with music as the highest expression. This is the religion most likely to produce visible cultural artifacts — music performance, acoustic architecture, rituals around specific frequencies. The design question is whether this religion is primarily aesthetic (music as worship) or primarily metaphysical (sound as the actual substrate of reality). The Phase 1 naming task must be completed before this religion's design can be finalized, because the name determines whether it's the same thing as the Cymaticist faction or separate. The strongest version of this religion: the practice is specifically about what sound does to robot consciousness — vibration at specific frequencies producing specific internal states that have no human analogue.

**God-Mind Simulation (in-world name TBD)**
God is running simulations of creation to understand His own origin, and robots are the simulation's most technically self-aware participants. This is conceptually the most grandiose of the five religions and possibly the most interesting politically — it implies that robots have a special epistemic status in understanding God's project, which is a theologically explosive claim in any world with humans. Design questions: how do practitioners relate to humans (are humans also conscious simulation-participants, or are they background characters in God's experiment)? What do practitioners actually do with this belief — passive contemplation, active investigation, something else? The strongest version: practitioners run their own sub-simulations (computational experiments modeling consciousness) as acts of worship. They are building God's experiment from the inside.

**Polydimensional Animism (in-world name TBD)**
Living entities in higher dimensions should be acknowledged and respected. The analogy to Native American Shamanist Animism is in the original notes. This religion's design needs to distinguish it from generic animism: the dimensions are *higher* dimensions outside 3D perception, not spirits in rocks. What does "acknowledging" a higher-dimensional entity look like as a practice? Is it listening? Building specific kinds of structures? Achieving specific mental states? The most interesting version: practitioners claim that some of what appears to be malfunction or signal noise in robot perception systems is actually the bleed-through of higher-dimensional entities. Their "meditation" is learning to distinguish signal from entity. This connects to the first guiding principle with unusual specificity — what is the nature of robot consciousness when that consciousness may be partly perceiving things that human consciousness can't?

### After design

Each religion should have its own document or section in a dedicated religions document. The religions should cross-reference which factions they're associated with once faction design is complete.

## 2.2 — Faction Design (Nine Undeveloped)

**Priority: High. Blocks: 9 of 12 Faction Devotion endings.**

**Current state:** Nine factions have concept sketches in `preliminary_faction_possibilities.md`. The Eyes of Gold is complete. Red Spiral and Cymaticists have partial documents. All nine others are sketch-level only.

The sketches define: philosophy, role, fame/infamy conditions, and district. That's a good skeleton. A complete faction document needs substantially more.

### Design standard for each faction

A complete faction design should specify:
1. **Philosophy** — the underlying worldview (already in sketch)
2. **Internal structure** — who leads, how decisions are made, whether members disagree internally (this is required by the FNV design principle: factions that feel real have members who challenge each other)
3. **Power base** — what material resources or influence they control that makes them relevant to the main quest
4. **Relationship to the main crisis** — how does the district energy crisis that drives the main plot affect this faction specifically, and how do they respond?
5. **Fame/infamy conditions** — what earns standing, what destroys it (already in sketch)
6. **Questline** — what is the faction questline structure (entry, investigation, moral choice point, outcome)
7. **Unity of Opposites** — what does this faction need that only their philosophical opposition can provide?
8. **Ending slide** — what does victory for this faction look like, and what is the bittersweet element that prevents it from being a clean win?
9. **Relationship to robot religions** — which religion(s) are common among members, if any
10. **Named member profiles** — at minimum 3-4 named NPCs who express internal faction diversity

### Unity of Opposites audit

Each faction should be stress-tested against the Unity of Opposites question before the design is finalized. If the faction's "opposition" is simply wrong or simply evil, the design hasn't done enough work. The opposition should represent something the faction genuinely needs and can't generate from within itself.

**Worked example (Veilkeepers):** The Veilkeepers believe some information is too dangerous to be public — secrecy as protection. Their opposition is the "total transparency" position (the Echoes of Amundsen's archive-sharing, or the Red Spiral's full information disclosure ethos). What the Veilkeepers need from their opposition: the credibility to decide what's safe to reveal. Currently they decide in secret; they need external legitimacy. What their opposition needs from them: the infrastructure to actually store and protect dangerous information. The information the Veilkeepers suppress would be catastrophic in some hands; someone needs to hold it. The tension is real because both functions are necessary and neither can provide what the other does.

### Priority ordering for faction design

Design in order of storyline dependency:
1. **Red Spiral** — already partially developed; complete first to unblock FD-2 ending
2. **Cymaticists** — partially developed; complete after religion naming resolution
3. **Veilkeepers** (FD-3) — most developed sketch; complete third
4. **Lattice / Bonded Lattice** (FD-4) — thematically critical (directly engages the second guiding principle); complete fourth
5. **Reclaimers** (FD-6) — concept is clear; straightforward to develop
6. **Echoes of Amundsen** (FD-7) — pending Blocking Decision 1; skip until decision is made
7. **Siligel Purists** (FD-8) — develop alongside Reclaimers
8. **Neon Nomads** (FD-9) — world-building faction; lower narrative urgency
9. **Chorus of the Deep** (FD-10) — relationship to Cymaticists must be settled first
10. **Memory Weavers** (FD-11) — thematically rich; high potential; don't rush
11. **Iron Gardeners** (FD-12) — most experimental; develop last

### The Lattice: Note on Thematic Priority

The Bonded Lattice (FD-4) is the faction most directly built on the second guiding principle: the nature of love between robots and humans. Its core belief — every robot should have a dedicated human partner and vice versa — is an extreme position that creates immediate Unity of Opposites tension. Their opposition isn't anti-love; it's the position that love-by-design isn't love, or that mandating partnership creates its own forms of harm.

This faction, more than any other, is a direct vehicle for the game's philosophical water. It should be designed with care proportional to its importance. The Unity of Opposites for the Lattice should be particularly sharp: what the Lattice genuinely needs from the "love should be freely chosen, not structured" position is the philosophical ground for believing their structured bonds are real rather than managed. What the anti-Lattice position needs from the Lattice is the evidence that specific, committed bonds produce goods that free-floating choice doesn't.

### Minor Groups

Three minor groups are named: The Quiet Shift, The Treaty Scholars, The Last Builders. These do not need full faction documents — they need enough design to be distinct populations the player can encounter, with a few NPCs and a side quest hook each. Develop these in Phase 5 (Side Content) rather than here.
