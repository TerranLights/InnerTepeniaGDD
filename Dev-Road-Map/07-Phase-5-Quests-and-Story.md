# Phase 5 — Quests and Story

**Prerequisite:** Phases 2 and 3 complete (factions designed, characters defined). Phase 4 substantially underway.

**What this phase does:** Completes all major questlines that are currently structured but unwritten, completes the main story's Act 3 implementation, designs the side content layer, and finalizes all endings. This is the largest single phase in terms of creative scope.

---

## Design Principles for This Phase

### Quest anatomy (every major quest must have)

Derived from FNV's documented design standard and adapted to Inner Tepenia's requirements:

1. **Acquisition method** — how does the player find this quest? NPC dialogue, environmental discovery, world-state trigger, prerequisite chain? This isn't a minor detail — hidden quests reward engaged players; telegraphed quests serve players who want direction. Most quests should have both a visible entry point and at least one non-obvious discovery route.

2. **At minimum three solution paths** — one combat, one skill-based non-combat, one social/dialogue. High-priority quests should target five or more. Never design a quest that can only be resolved one way unless the single-path design is the deliberate point (and this should be rare).

3. **Distributed skill checks** — not concentrated at a single gate. A player who fails the initial Speech check should find a different dialogue branch that serves the same purpose through a different stat. Investigation for information-gathering, Humanity for emotional context, Calculation for technical approaches, etc.

4. **A key decision point** — distinct from the quest's surface objective. The surface objective ("find the missing supply shipment") and the decision point ("who do you believe about where the shipment went, and who do you trust to manage the fallout?") are separate things. The decision point is where moral weight is loaded.

5. **World-state flags** — every major quest sets at least one world-state flag that can be read by other quests or the ending slides. Inner Tepenia's Consequence Web system already handles inter-district chains; this extends that logic to all major questlines.

6. **At least one misleading surface reading** — the Junktown principle. The obviously-correct choice should have consequences that complicate the initial read. Not every quest, but most. The player who acts on surface readings without investigating will get technically successful but messier outcomes.

7. **A bittersweet endpoint** — no clean resolutions. Every major quest should end with something lost even when something is gained. This is not grimdark — it is the Unity of Opposites principle applied to quest resolution.

### On companion questlines specifically

Companion quests should resolve a question the companion has about themselves, not a plot problem that needs solving. The plot may be the vehicle (investigating Villena Hiresvett's past might involve actual investigation of actual places) but the emotional destination is always: what does this companion need to understand about who they are? The player facilitates this; they do not control it.

The companion quest's moral choice point is specifically about the companion's identity or future direction. Equipment rewards are secondary. The ending should reflect what the companion chose about themselves, not just what the player chose for them.

## 5.1 — Calethina Questline ("Echoes of the Bridge")

**Priority: Highest. Deliberately deferred but must be designed in this phase.**

**Prerequisite within phase:** Blocking Decision 3 resolved (passive link mechanic defined). Calethina's backstory written (Phase 3).

The questline's design is constrained by what's already established:
- The archive-narrator direction (Calethina speaks in a specific register)
- The Ji-Eun Kim first thread (established; questline picks up here)
- The wrist-device download mechanic concept
- The five-step personality-download design

**What needs to happen:**

**Stage 1:** Reconcile the five-step personality-download design with the wrist-device mechanic. These may be the same system at different points (the wrist device delivers the five steps) or different systems (the wrist device is for something else, the five-step structure is the emotional arc of the questline). This is a design decision before the writing begins.

**Stage 2:** Write the questline beats. The archive-narrator direction suggests the questline is structured around recovering or reconstructing something — memories, records, fragments of who Calethina was before she was singular. The passive link mechanic (once defined) should be woven through each beat as the medium through which the player experiences Calethina's perspective.

**Stage 3:** Design the questline's moral choice point. This questline, more than any other, engages the first guiding principle directly — what is the nature of consciousness among robots, specifically this robot. The choice point should not be about helping or harming Calethina. It should be about what the player chooses to understand about her, and whether they act on that understanding.

**Stage 4:** Write the questline's ending. Per the bittersweet-only principle: the Calethina questline cannot end with a clean revelation. Whatever is recovered is partial. Whatever is understood is incomplete. The value is in the incompleteness being specific and true rather than merely absent.

## 5.2 — Planetary Split Brain Questline

**Priority: High. Blocks: specific faction ending paths; Act 3 complexity.**

**Current state:** Premise established; structure undefined.

The Planetary Split Brain is one of the most philosophically distinctive questlines in the design — it engages with questions about distributed vs. unified consciousness in a context that is unique to Inner Tepenia. It should be designed with the full weight of the first guiding principle: this questline is about the nature of consciousness among robots at a systemic, not personal, level.

**What needs to happen:**

1. Define the split brain scenario — what is the player actually encountering? An AI system that has diverged into two distinct personalities/decision-making processes? A distributed intelligence whose segments disagree? The exact premise determines everything else.

2. Design the questline beats — how does the player discover the situation, what is at stake, what are the possible resolutions?

3. Identify the faction connections — which factions have stakes in the resolution? (The Veilkeepers, the Cymaticists, potentially the Calethina-adjacent research community all seem likely candidates.)

4. Write the decision point — the decision should not be "which half do you choose?" The more interesting decision is "what framework do you apply to a consciousness that can't be whole?" This engages the philosophical water directly.

5. Write the bittersweet endings — any resolution has costs. Reunification changes both halves. Separation requires maintaining a boundary. Destruction ends the suffering but also ends the consciousness. No clean outcomes.

## 5.3 — Act 3 Climax: Full Implementation

**Priority: High. Blocks: Final endings; Act 3 Act-break writing.**

**Current state:** Structure exists; specific beats are TBD.

The Act 3 structure and the ideas/possibilities document exist. The implementation beats do not. This task is writing the specific scene-by-scene structure of Act 3's climax — not dialogue, but the precise sequence of what happens, in what order, with what player choices at each beat.

**Design constraints:**
- All major faction questlines must be resolvable in the Act 3 structure
- The Consequence Web's Act 3 state profiles (already written) should be honored
- The climax must support the bittersweet-only ending principle at the macro level

**Approach:** Start from the Major Stabilization Paths document. Each stabilization path is a different version of Act 3. Map the specific beats for each path — what the player sees, does, and chooses, and how those choices interact with the faction state set during Act 2. Once all paths are mapped, identify shared beats (moments that appear in all paths) and divergent beats (moments that are path-specific). The shared beats are the structural skeleton; the divergent beats are where the fiction justifies why Act 3 feels different per playthrough.

## 5.4 — Major Stabilization Paths: Quest Chain Detail

**Priority: High, linked to 5.3.**

The framework document exists. The quest chains within each path do not have their beats written.

For each stabilization path: write the 3-5 specific quest stages the player navigates to reach the Act 3 state that path produces. Each stage needs the same anatomy as any major quest (acquisition, investigation, decision point, world-state flags, outcome). The Consequence Web chains already tell us what world states change — this task writes the player-facing experience of causing those changes.

## 5.6 — Faction Questlines: Completion

**Priority: High. Blocked by Phase 2 (faction design) — begins as Phase 2 completes each faction.**

Each fully designed faction needs:
1. An entry quest (how the player joins or gets involved with the faction)
2. An investigation/mid quest (what the player does in service of the faction's goals)
3. A moral choice point quest (the point at which faction loyalty and player values are tested)
4. A Faction Devotion ending unlock (what the player does to max faction standing and trigger the FD ending)

This work proceeds in parallel with Phase 2 — as each faction is designed, its questline can begin. The questlines don't all need to be written before Phase 5 can complete; they can be rolled in as each faction is finalized in Phase 2.

## 5.7 — Endings: Completion

**Priority: High. Blocks: GDD can't be called "near-complete" without full endings.**

**Current state:**
- All 6 ending categories: frameworks complete
- 3 of 12 Faction Devotion endings: ✅ (Eyes of Gold, Red Spiral partial)
- 9 of 12 Faction Devotion endings: ⚠️ or ❌ (depend on Phase 2 faction design)
- Hidden Paths: structure done; narrative content TBD
- District Idolized Endings: ✅

**What needs to happen:**

After Phase 2 finalizes each faction, write the specific Faction Devotion ending for that faction. The ending slide format is established. The bittersweet constraint is established. What needs to be written: the specific narrative content of what victory for this faction means — what changes, what costs, what the world looks like in the aftermath, and what the player's role in that future is.

For Hidden Paths: write the narrative content for all four paths and nine resolutions. The structure exists; the prose doesn't.

For the Courier-equivalent question (the player character's personal ending): FNV's analysis flagged this as a gap. The player character in Inner Tepenia — the protagonist Doll — should have a set of personal ending slides that reflect their specific journey, not just which faction they backed. Design these personal ending variables: what did the protagonist resolve about themselves? This is a direct expression of the first guiding principle.

## 5.8 — Side Content Layer

**Priority: Medium. The game is playable without side content; this is density and reward work.**

**Minor faction groups (The Quiet Shift, Treaty Scholars, Last Builders):** Each needs a few NPCs and one side quest hook. 3 mini-quests, roughly the scope of a minor FNV side quest.

**Palmer City and pre-war lore:** The Gyeong-ja Yun case's in-game presence needs to be designed. What can the player find? What does finding it do? This bridges into Phase 6 (World Depth) but the quest hooks are Phase 5 work.

**Named evacuee characters:** Which specific evacuees are relevant to the player's experience? 1-2 named evacuee NPCs with specific personal histories that connect to the game's themes. Their presence as individuals (not just the "evacuees as category") is Phase 5 work.
