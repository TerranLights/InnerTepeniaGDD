# Concordia — City Logistics Reference

## Basis: Concordia Station, Antarctica

The fictional Concordia is a "city-fication" of the real Concordia Station — a joint French-Italian research station located at Dome C on the Antarctic Plateau.

**Real Concordia Station key facts:**
- Coordinates: 75°06'S, 123°21'E
- Altitude: ~3,233 meters above sea level (high-altitude effects are significant)
- Distance from coast: ~1,700 km (one of the most inland points on Earth)
- Summer population (real): ~60 people; winter (real): ~13 people
- Temperature range: −25°C in summer to below −80°C in winter (average winter around −50°C)
- Annual snowfall: minimal (Concordia is technically a polar desert — very dry)
- Polar night: approximately 3 months of complete darkness in winter
- Midnight sun: approximately 3 months of 24-hour daylight in summer

**What "city-fication" means for Inner Tepenia:**
The fictional Concordia is a full city with 13 districts and a mixed human-robot population numbering in the tens of thousands (exact figure TBD). It grew from the research station through some combination of planned expansion, refugee influx, and political circumstance related to the Falkland Treaty and the War of Upper Earth. The dome-and-tunnel structure of the city reflects what was physically required to make permanent habitation viable at this location.

## Physical Structure

Concordia is an enclosed city. Almost nothing happens outdoors that doesn't have to. The Antarctic environment — cold, altitude, radiation exposure during polar summer, complete darkness during polar winter — makes outdoor exposure a managed risk rather than a normal condition of life.

**General layout:**
- Radial structure centered on Janbogo Central Hub (Axis Mundi)
- Districts connected by three major surface/tunnel highways: Hwy 110, Hwy 37, and Hwy 183
- Undergrid: the underground transit and maintenance network connecting all districts below ground
- Inter-district travel: transit tubes, Kharkovchanka shuttles (surface), or on-foot through the Undergrid

**Dome structure:**
Most districts are contained under domes — pressurized, temperature-controlled enclosures that maintain habitable conditions. Domes vary in size and condition by district. Outer districts (particularly the Frostlands/Sagittarius) have partially completed or damaged domes that require ongoing maintenance.

**Verticality:**
Many districts have multiple levels: upper dome levels, ground level, and Undergrid levels below. The Undergrid is not just transit — it is maintenance infrastructure, storage, and in some areas, habitation.

## Food Production

Concordia is self-sufficient in food production by necessity. The distance from the coast and the transport limitations of the Antarctic environment make reliable external food supply impossible. Everything consumed in the city must be produced in the city or brought in at significant cost and risk.

### Hydroponics (Primary System)

The backbone of Concordia's human food supply. The Coastal Cut district contains **vast hydroponic arcologies** — multi-story growing towers using LED lighting, controlled temperature, and nutrient solutions to grow food crops without soil or natural sunlight. Frontier hydroponic outposts extend the system to Sagittarius.

**Primary crops:**
- **Leafy greens** (lettuce, spinach, kale) — fast-growing, high-yield, essential for vitamins
- **Root vegetables** (potatoes, carrots, beets) — calorie-dense, storable, versatile
- **Legumes** (soy, lentils, chickpeas) — protein source, nitrogen-fixing (good for system health)
- **Grains** (wheat, rice) — calorie staples; space-intensive but essential
- **Mushrooms** — can be grown on organic waste, no light required, fast-growing, nutritious; produced throughout the city not just in dedicated arcologies
- **Tomatoes, peppers, herbs** — quality-of-life crops; limited but present

**System vulnerabilities:**
Hydroponics is entirely dependent on controlled light (LED), controlled temperature, and nutrient solution supply. The failing energy grid directly threatens food production. If power to the hydroponic arcologies fails for extended periods, Concordia faces starvation. This is not theoretical — it is the background pressure that makes the Aries energy crisis existential rather than merely inconvenient.

### Algae and Microalgae

Grown in bioreactor tanks throughout the city, including in the Coastal Cut district where "glowing algae lights" provide both illumination and food production in the same system. Spirulina and chlorella are the primary varieties — extremely nutrient-dense, requiring minimal space and energy relative to their output.

Algae farming is the city's most efficient food production method per square meter and per watt. It also provides a visible aesthetic component to several districts — the blue-green glow of algae bioreactors is a distinctive visual element of Concordia.

### Livestock

The primary protein source for Concordia's human population comes from four herded species — selected because each is specifically adapted to the conditions that make most large livestock impractical here. Standard cattle are absent at any meaningful scale: the feed demands, the space requirements, and the absence of any high-altitude adaptation make them untenable. What replaced them is better suited to Concordia than cattle would have been even in a temperate city.

**Primary herd animals:**

- **Yaks** — The best overall fit for Concordia's specific conditions. Yaks evolved for the Tibetan Plateau, which sits at comparable altitude to Concordia's 3,233m. They are cold-tolerant, high-altitude adapted, and low-maintenance relative to their output. A single yak provides meat, milk (richer in fat than cow's milk, valuable in a calorie-stressed city), and yak wool — one of the warmest natural fibers available. Yaks are the Coastal Cut's primary large animal.

- **Alpacas** — High-altitude adapted (Andes, similar elevation to Concordia). Lower-maintenance than yaks, easy to herd in enclosed spaces, and their fleece is lightweight, exceptionally warm, and water-resistant — properties that matter in a city where a torn dome panel is a cold-injury event. Alpacas also provide meat. They are the Coastal Cut's secondary large herd animal and have a secondary population in the Frostlands.

- **Sheep** — Not altitude-adapted in the same specific way, but hardy enough for enclosed heated environments and multi-resource: wool, milk, and meat from one animal. Wool production complements the alpaca fleece supply and gives the city two independent fiber streams. The Coastal Cut holds the largest sheep population.

- **Reindeer** — Cold-adapted rather than altitude-adapted, and the only one of the four large animals with a significant Frostlands presence. Reindeer can tolerate temperatures far below what requires full climate control, making them the most practical large animal for the Frostlands' partially damaged and semi-open dome sections. They are herded through the outer district's corridors and partially exposed paddocks — areas that would stress any other large animal and barely register for reindeer. They provide meat, milk, and hide; reindeer hide is among the warmest natural insulating materials known.

**Supporting species:**

- **Pigs** — Not primary, but not absent. Pigs eat organic waste — food scraps, processing byproducts, agricultural waste — and convert it into protein. They are the city's biological waste processor as much as they are a food source. Every district with significant food preparation produces waste that can feed pigs; small pig operations are distributed throughout.

- **Chickens** — Compact, egg-productive, and consistent. Chickens do not provide the bulk protein of the herded animals but they provide eggs daily and require minimal space and feed per calorie output. They are found in small operations across many districts, not concentrated in any single zone.

- **Rabbits** — Very efficient feed-to-protein conversion, fast reproduction, minimal space. A secondary protein source that can be maintained in small facilities wherever space permits.

- **Insects** — Mealworms, crickets, black soldier flies. Insect farming requires almost no space or energy, can be fed on organic waste, and produces extremely dense protein per gram. Cultural acceptance varies by district and population segment, but the practical case for insect protein in a resource-constrained Antarctic city is not marginal — it is substantial.

**Geographic placement:**

*Coastal Cut (primary):* The large enclosed livestock paddocks are integrated into and adjacent to the hydroponic arcology complex. The relationship is deliberate: inedible hydroponic biomass (stems, roots, crop waste, processing byproducts) becomes animal feed; animal waste is processed into the nutrient solution that feeds the hydroponics. Nothing in the Coastal Cut agricultural system is discarded. The paddocks are fully enclosed and climate-controlled, with dedicated movement corridors for herding between feeding areas, grazing areas, and processing facilities. Yaks, alpacas, and sheep are the primary animals here.

*Frostlands / Sagittarius (secondary):* The outer district's partially open dome sections, which are a liability for human habitation, become an asset for the reindeer. Semi-exposed paddocks requiring minimal heating house the city's reindeer herds. This makes the Frostlands not just a frontier zone and exploration staging point but a pastoral one — large cold-adapted animals moving through the outer edges of the city's structure, herded by people who know the district's geography intimately. Some alpacas are also kept here. The Frostlands' pastoral character is a significant part of its visual identity and distinguishes it from the more industrial and technical inner districts.

### Aquaculture

Despite being ~1,700 km from the coast, Concordia operates indoor aquaculture systems. Fish farming does not require proximity to the ocean — it requires water, controlled temperature, and appropriate feed.

**Species farmed:**
- **Tilapia** — Extremely hardy, fast-growing, tolerates varying water conditions; a staple of indoor aquaculture worldwide
- **Catfish** — Hardy, bottom-feeding, efficient conversion
- **Trout** — Higher quality, higher demand, more resource-intensive; likely a premium product

**Antarctic fish:** The real Concordia Station is too far inland for coastal fishing, and the fictional Concordia is similarly positioned. However, the city likely receives limited imports of Antarctic seafood (toothfish/Antarctic sea bass, krill, icefish) via highway trade with coastal Antarctic settlements or the Tepenian Federation. These would be relatively expensive and intermittent.

### External Food Supply

The Port of Concordia (small trucking docks, Coastal Cut district) and the highway network provide limited external supply lines. Janbogo is mentioned as a source of supplies ("supplies from Janbogo are delayed again"). This suggests regular but unreliable import of food commodities that cannot be efficiently produced locally — specific flavors, luxury items, variety foods.

External supply is supplemental, not essential. The city cannot survive on imports alone and does not depend on them for baseline nutrition.

### The Siligel System (Robot Food)

Siligel is a synthesized food source for the robot population. It is produced from mined precursor materials — not found ready-made, and not derived from the same agricultural system as human food. Siligel is **lethal to humans**.

Siligel production is centered in the Mountain Cut Industrial Zone, where siligel synthesis facilities and precursor material quarries are located. The Siligel Purists faction is concerned with quality control in this system. Illegal siligel refineries operate in the Black Market district.

The human and robot food systems are entirely separate. This has significant logistics implications: the city must maintain two parallel food supply chains, both dependent on energy, both with distinct vulnerabilities.

## Water Supply

Antarctica is the world's largest freshwater reservoir — almost all of it locked in ice. For Concordia, water supply is not a scarcity problem under normal conditions. It is an energy problem.

**Sources:**
- **Ice melting** — The primary source. Surface ice and snow are collected and melted using waste heat from the city's power systems (particularly geothermal). The failing grid makes this increasingly expensive in energy terms.
- **Subglacial water** — Dome C is near the Antarctic Plateau's subglacial lake system. It is plausible that Concordia has access to subglacial freshwater sources through deep drilling, which would provide water independently of ice melting. TBD in game lore.
- **Water recycling** — At this altitude and in this environment, all wastewater is recycled. Concordia's water system is closed-loop by design. The city cannot afford water waste.

**The Coastal Cut connection:** The district's name and its "coastal" descriptor are slightly puzzling given Concordia's inland location. This may refer to an internal body of water — a reservoir, a subglacial lake access point, or a large water processing facility — rather than an ocean coast. The "humid, misty, greenhouse-like" atmosphere and "sounds of water pumps" suggest significant water processing activity.

## Energy

The energy system is the central crisis of the game. See main story files for the full crisis design. For logistics reference:

**Primary sources:**
- **Geothermal** — The TBN-district and Power Core contains massive geothermal pipes and reactor cores. The Antarctic Plateau has geothermal heat access through deep drilling. This is the city's primary heating and power source.
- **Nuclear** — Present in the power core system. "Glowing reactor cores" referenced in the district description.
- **Wind** — The Antarctic Plateau experiences extreme katabatic winds. Wind power generation is likely present on the city's exterior, though the enclosed dome structure limits integration with interior systems.

**The crisis:** The central grid is failing. Every city system that depends on power — which is essentially all of them — is affected. The hydroponics need light and heat. The water system needs heat for ice melting. Climate control needs power. The Undergrid transit system needs power. The energy crisis is not just an inconvenience — it is a slow civilizational failure.

**The Lattice alternative:** A decentralized parallel power network exists (the Lattice, tended by the Bonded Lattice faction). Its cross-district coverage is growing but not yet sufficient to replace the central grid.

## Internal Transportation

**Transit tubes:** Fast, enclosed transit between major points within and between districts. High energy cost. Likely reduced in frequency and reliability due to the energy crisis.

**Kharkovchanka shuttles:** The Kharkovchanka is a real Soviet-era Antarctic tracked vehicle — a massive, enclosed, heated cabin on a tracked chassis designed for extreme Antarctic conditions. In Concordia, these serve as inter-district heavy transport and surface travel between highway junctions. They are slow, reliable, and able to operate even in conditions that would disable lighter vehicles. They are a distinctly Antarctic form of transport.

**On-foot / Undergrid:** The underground network allows pedestrian movement between districts without surface exposure. Travel time is significant and the environment is hazardous in sections, but it is always available regardless of power availability (though lighting depends on power).

**Within-district movement:** Largely on-foot or by smaller electric vehicles (scooters, cargo carts). Cycling is not referenced but plausible in the larger dome spaces.

## External Connections

Concordia is isolated but not completely cut off.

**Highway network:** Three major highways (Hwy 110, Hwy 37, Hwy 183) connect Concordia to other Antarctic settlements and the broader Tepenian Federation. Surface travel on the Antarctic Plateau is dangerous and slow — primarily via Kharkovchanka convoy with appropriate support. Highway maintenance is critical and is partly the responsibility of the Neon Nomads (highway clans who live on the roads).

**Port of Concordia:** Small trucking docks in the Coastal Cut district. This is the primary terminus for highway trade — goods arriving by surface convoy from other settlements. "Port" is somewhat misleading given the inland location; this is a highway freight terminal, not a maritime port.

**Janbogo:** Another city (off-limits to the player, mentioned in lore). Regular supply convoys from Janbogo are referenced. Refugees from Janbogo have also arrived at Concordia. The relationship between the two cities is significant in the game's political landscape.

**Air transport:** Likely extremely limited. High altitude + extreme weather + energy costs make air transport impractical for routine supply. Possibly used for emergencies or high-value, low-mass cargo.

**Off-world:** The Space Elevator that once connected Antarctica to Upper Earth was destroyed. There is no off-world supply or communication. The Echoes of Amundsen maintain signal towers attempting to contact the off-world evacuees.

## Healthcare and Social Services

**Medical:**
Concordia's population faces specific health challenges from its environment:
- **Cold injury** — frostbite, hypothermia (primarily affecting people who spend time in damaged or exterior areas)
- **Altitude effects** — at 3,233m, altitude sickness affects newcomers; long-term residents adapt but remain at elevated risk for certain cardiovascular issues
- **UV deprivation** — during polar night (3 months of darkness), vitamin D deficiency is a significant health concern for the human population; UV lamp therapy and supplementation are standard
- **Polar night depression** — the psychological effects of 3 months of darkness on human (and possibly robot) populations are documented and significant; Scorpio's therapeutic community addresses this among many other things
- **Grid failure effects** — as heating reliability decreases, cold-related injury and illness increase throughout the city

**Psychological/Therapeutic:**
Scorpio's therapeutic community is a major city institution. The psychological cost of living in extreme isolation, under resource pressure, in an ongoing slow-motion crisis, is substantial. Scorpio's services are not optional infrastructure — they are load-bearing to the city's social cohesion.

**Education:**
Schools and creches are referenced in the Bonded Habitation Rings. A University of Concordia exists in the Research district.

## Climate Control

Everything inside the city's domes is climate-controlled. Temperature, air quality, humidity, and lighting are all managed systems.

**Heating:** Primarily geothermal, supplemented by the Coastal Cut's heating plants. As the grid fails, heating reliability decreases — outer districts with damaged domes are most vulnerable.

**Lighting:** LED arrays simulate day/night cycles within the domes. During polar night, artificial lighting is the only source of light for the entire city. Algae bioreactors provide supplemental ambient light in some districts.

**Air quality:** CO2 scrubbing and oxygen production from the hydroponic systems and algae bioreactors. The relationship between food production and air quality is functional — the plants that feed the city also breathe for it.

**The failure cascade:** Grid failure → heating failure + lighting failure → hydroponic failure + water processing failure → food shortage + water shortage. The energy crisis is an everything crisis, and the reason it is existential is that every system depends on every other system.

## Commercial Activity and Businesses

Concordia's economy is district-based, with each district specializing according to its resources and culture.

**What businesses exist:**

*Food and consumption:*
- Hydroponic product stalls and markets (Coastal Cut)
- Meat and dairy markets (Coastal Cut primary; Frostlands for reindeer products specifically)
- Restaurants and food service (all districts, quality varies dramatically by district resource access)
- Siligel distributors (robot-facing, in all districts)
- Black market food operations (illegal siligel refineries, contraband food imports)
- Insect protein vendors (some cultural resistance but widespread practical acceptance)

*Fiber and textiles:*
- Yak wool, alpaca fleece, and sheep wool are produced in quantity and processed into insulating clothing — a survival necessity in a city where a dome breach is always possible. Textile production and clothing trade are significant industries, centered in the Coastal Cut and distributed outward.
- Reindeer hide processing (Frostlands) — hide-based insulating garments and gear for people who work in exposed areas or travel the highways.

*Technology and equipment:*
- Robot component shops (Mountain Cut primarily)
- Repair services (widespread — maintenance is survival)
- Weapons dealers (legal and illegal; the Eyes of Gold training grounds have legitimate weapons suppliers nearby)
- Arcanet equipment and services (Gemini/Janbogo Subnet area)
- Nanotech implants (rare, premium, associated with Ji-Eun Kim's work and Eyes of Gold)

*Services:*
- Transit services (Kharkovchanka hire, Undergrid guides)
- Information brokerage (Gemini, Eyes of Gold adjacent)
- Therapeutic and medical services (Scorpio district primarily)
- Arts and cultural services (Cymatic Resonance district — performances, resonance tech, music clubs)
- Memory archiving and editing (Memory Weavers adjacent, some commercial crossover)
- Legal and diplomatic services (Libra district)

*Trade and logistics:*
- Highway freight (Neon Nomads, independent operators)
- Black market (extensive, centered in the Black Market district)
- Arcanet data trade (Gemini Truth Markets)

**Currency/economy:**
TBD in game lore. The city likely uses some combination of resource credit (managed by Libra's resource allocation system) and informal exchange. The black market and Pisces district suggest a parallel informal economy alongside whatever official system exists.

## The Dual Economy (Human and Robot)

Concordia's mixed population creates a genuinely dual economy in some respects:

**What is shared:** Most services, entertainment, cultural products, and information are used by both humans and robots. Districts are mixed. The transit system, the governance structure, and most commercial activity serve both populations.

**What is separate:** The food systems are completely separate (human food vs. Siligel). Robot maintenance and repair services are specialist industries. Robot religious practice and human religious practice exist in parallel.

**The Lattice/Bonded Lattice dynamic (see faction file):** The faction advocates for robot-human pair bonding — the idea that emotional bonds between humans and robots should be structurally supported by the city's resource allocation. The Bonded Habitation Rings are a physical expression of this philosophy in the city's built environment. The economics of the paired household (resource allocation, heating credits, Siligel vs. food budgeting) are a distinct area of city logistics.

## The Polar Calendar

Concordia's life is shaped by Antarctica's extreme annual light cycle in ways that no temperate city experiences.

**Polar night (~3 months, approximately April–July in the Southern Hemisphere winter):**
- Complete darkness. No natural light reaches the surface.
- All lighting is artificial, consuming significant power.
- Psychological effects on the human population are substantial and documented.
- Outdoor activity essentially ceases for non-essential purposes.
- The Eternal Winter Collective / Frost Ascetics treat this period as spiritually significant.
- The city's energy grid is under maximum stress during polar night (heating + full artificial lighting simultaneously).

**Polar day / midnight sun (~3 months, approximately November–January):**
- 24-hour daylight. Sleep cycles require active management (blackout systems in habitation areas).
- Solar exposure on dome exteriors is significant; some energy recapture may be possible.
- The psychological lift for the human population is real and notable.
- Outdoor activity is theoretically possible but still limited by cold.

**Transition periods:**
The in-between months (increasing/decreasing light) are the most variably active periods for surface travel and external highway operations. The Neon Nomads and highway trade peak during these windows.

## Key Vulnerabilities (Summary)

1. **Energy** — the central crisis; everything cascades from here
2. **Food supply** — entirely dependent on controlled environments and therefore on energy
3. **Water** — energy-dependent ice melting; recycling mitigates but does not eliminate
4. **Climate control** — domes require active management; failure = exposure to -50°C+ environments
5. **External supply chain** — the Janbogo highway is mentioned as unreliable; if it closes, specific imports become unavailable
6. **Mental health** — polar isolation + resource crisis + polar night creates compounding psychological pressure on the human population
7. **Siligel supply integrity** — a separate vulnerability specific to the robot population; grid failure affects Siligel production as it affects everything else
8. **Dome structural integrity** — particularly in outer districts; storm damage and deferred maintenance create ongoing vulnerability
