# City-Origin Faction Possibilities — Grounded Drafts

**Purpose:** Organic community groupings that emerge from the specific cultural, national, and historical characters of Tepenian cities — developed on their own terms, independently of Concordia's district politics or game mechanics. These are real communities of real people with lives, debates, and internal tensions that exist whether or not the player ever encounters them.

**Method:** Each faction here is derived from a specific city's actual cultural-national character, its specific history, and the specific psychological reality of what it means to have come from that place. Where a city is destroyed or ruined, the fact of destruction is part of the community's character but not the whole of it — these people had full lives before the war, and that prior fullness is what shapes them.

**What this document is NOT:** A list of factions that want things from Concordia. That comes later. This document is about who these people are.

## The Continuity Schism — Mirny Communities

**Origin cities:** Mirny (primary); Framheim (secondary, different angle)

Mirny Station was never abandoned for a single day in its first century of existence. This was not luck — it was a collective decision, renewed every winter, every crisis, every near-miss. The unbroken occupation was the city's identity in a way that went beyond pride: it was the proof that something could be sustained against everything the continent could throw at it.

Then the Long Night War ended that.

The Mirny refugee community has been living with a philosophical fracture ever since, and it runs deeper than grief. The question is not "how do we recover?" — it's "what are we now?" Specifically:

**The Continuants** hold that the unbroken line did not end when the last person left the physical station. The line is the community, not the location. Wherever the Mirny people are, the century of continuity continues — it has simply moved. They maintain the same scheduling traditions, the same shift structures applied to whatever work they do in their new context, the same winter observances. They would argue that "Mirny" is not a place but a practice, and that abandoning the practice would be the real end.

**The Rupturists** — who are a minority but a vocal one — argue that the Continuants are engaged in a kind of self-deception that dishonors what Mirny actually was. Mirny was a physical location, continuously occupied. What exists now is a community of people who used to live there. Calling that "Mirny" is like calling a photograph a person. They believe the honest thing is to acknowledge that Mirny ended, grieve it properly, and build something new that doesn't have to pretend to be the old thing.

The internal tension between these two positions generates constant low-level friction. The Continuants find the Rupturists cold and defeatist. The Rupturists find the Continuants unwilling to reckon with reality. Both sides contain people in genuine good faith. Neither side has the argument fully won.

**What they argue about:** Whether maintaining the old schedules is preservation or performance. Whether new members of the community (non-Mirny people who've joined over the years) are "part of Mirny" or guests. Whether there can ever be a "return" to a station that no longer exists, and whether wanting one is healthy or pathological.

**Framheim's angle:** Framheim was built from historical records — it was, by definition, a city resurrected from archives. Its community has a related but different question: they don't argue about whether their continuity broke (it did, definitively, because Framheim didn't exist before the expedition rebuilt it). They argue about whether reconstruction from archives is the same as authentic continuation, or whether it's a very sophisticated kind of pretending. This makes Framheim and Mirny communities natural conversation partners who end up at opposite ends of the same question.

## The Burning Witness — Zukelli Communities

**Origin cities:** Zukelli (exclusively)

Zukelli was not a failing city. It was not a city in decline, or a city people were already leaving, or a city that had seen better days. It was at 72.5% human retention — near peak — when it was destroyed. The survivors did not evacuate from a dying city. They fled a living one.

This specific fact shapes the Zukelli community in ways that other destroyed-city communities don't share. There is no softening narrative available. There is no "well, things were already changing" or "people had already started to leave." The city was full. Then it wasn't.

The other fact that shapes them: Janbogo is 10km away across the water. Janbogo survived.

The Zukelli community is not unified in how it holds this. It has at least three distinct orientations:

**The Witnesses** take the position that what happened to Zukelli must be remembered exactly as it was, without comfort, without narrative smoothing, without the gradual drift of collective memory toward a version of the story that's easier to live with. They are not primarily angry — they are vigilant. Memory, for them, is a moral obligation. They are the ones who write things down, who correct each other's recollections when they soften, who get tense when someone phrases the destruction in the passive voice. "The city was destroyed" is not acceptable. Something destroyed it. That specificity matters.

**The Separatists** — not from Janbogo, but from the wider Tepenian refugee community in general — have developed a quiet but real cultural insularity. Not hostility, exactly. More like: they find that the company of people who didn't lose what they lost requires a constant low-level performance of okayness that they find exhausting. Within their own community they don't have to explain. They don't have to answer "how are you holding up?" with anything except the truth. They tend to socialize almost exclusively within the Zukelli community, which outsiders sometimes read as clannishness but which is more accurately described as the social cost-benefit calculation of people who are tired.

**The Integrationists** have reached, or are working toward, a position where Zukelli is part of their identity but not the whole of it. They have Tepenian friends, Concordia friends, friends from other refugee communities. They don't hide being from Zukelli — it comes up, it matters — but they don't organize their entire social life around it. The Witnesses sometimes regard them as having moved on too quickly. The Integrationists sometimes regard the Witnesses as having built a community around keeping a wound open.

**What they argue about:** Whether proximity to Janbogo culture is a betrayal of Zukelli identity or simply a practical reality. Whether the children born after the destruction are "from Zukelli" or just Tepenian. Whether the effort to document and preserve the city's memory is for the dead or for the living — and whether those are the same project. Whether anger at Janbogo (for surviving, for being close, for being okay when Zukelli wasn't) is legitimate grief or a dead end.

**What they don't argue about:** That it was full when it burned.

## The Founding Argument — Palmer City and Cape Adare Communities

**Origin cities:** Palmer City (first Tepenian city, established 2564); Cape Adare (first permanent Antarctic habitation, 1899/pre-exile)

These two communities share a specific kind of identity claim: the claim to the founding of something. But they're claiming different foundings, and they have different relationships to what "founding" means.

**Palmer City** was the first Tepenian city — where the exiled robots first set foot in 2564 and said "here." Its community holds this the way that families hold genealogies: not as power but as precedence. They were first in the sense that matters to them — first in the context that actually is Tepenia. Pre-exile Antarctic history is, in their view, a different story.

**Cape Adare** was where the first humans permanently wintered on the continent in 1899, and its community holds the deeper historical clock. The Tepenian story is one chapter; the human-Antarctic story begins centuries earlier and Cape Adare is where it starts. From their view, Palmer City's "first" is a parochial claim that ignores the longer history they are part of.

This is not a war. It's more like a long-running family argument about which branch of the tree gets to write the family history. Both communities regard the other with a mixture of respect and mild competitive irritation. They collaborate on historical preservation projects and disagree about framing. Scholars from both communities publish work that is read and argued with by scholars from the other.

What makes this genuinely interesting is that both communities are, in their own ways, conservative in a philosophical sense — oriented toward the past, toward record-keeping, toward the maintenance of founding identity. They share values and methods while disagreeing about the object of those values. A Palmer City archivist and a Cape Adare archivist have more in common with each other than with most of their fellow Tepenians, and both of them know it, and neither of them quite wants to admit it because it would soften an argument they both find useful.

**What they argue about:** Which date is the real anniversary of Antarctic civilization. Whether the pre-exile history "counts" as Tepenian history or is simply Earth history that happened on the continent. Whether founding precedence confers any kind of moral authority, or whether it's simply a historical fact with no normative weight. (Palmer City says: no authority, it's just true. Cape Adare says: no authority, it's just older and therefore truer in a different way. This distinction matters enormously to both parties and is nearly invisible to everyone else.)

## The Three Schools of Endurance — Nordic/Australian Communities

**Origin cities:** Troll (Norwegian), Aboa (Finnish), Mawson/Davis (Australian), Framheim (Norwegian/Amundsen heritage)

These communities share an overlapping philosophical tradition around endurance, self-reliance, and understated competence. They recognize each other across cultural difference — there's immediate mutual respect — and they also, on closer acquaintance, disagree about almost everything below the surface.

**The Norwegian position (Troll, Framheim):** Endurance is a communal practice. The Norwegian tradition of jantelov — the cultural pressure not to present yourself as better than anyone else — means that the Norwegian communities absorb hardship collectively, without individual heroics. The person who endures hardship without complaint does so because the complaint would be a burden on the community, not because suffering in silence is noble. The self-reliance is ultimately in service of not requiring the community to care for you excessively. Framheim adds a specific layer: they rebuilt their city from historical records, and there's a Norwegian quality to having done that without drama — they simply decided it needed to be done and did it.

**The Finnish position (Aboa):** Sisu is not quite the same as Norwegian endurance, and the Finnish community would want this acknowledged. Sisu is not community-oriented in its primary expression — it is individual, internal, and functions even in the absence of community support. The Finnish tradition would not use "we endured" as its primary frame; it would say "I endured, and so did you, independently." The sauna culture is communal in practice but the philosophy underneath is individual resilience that then comes together. The Finnish community finds the Norwegian emphasis on collective endurance slightly suffocating, and the Norwegian community finds the Finnish emphasis on individual resilience slightly cold.

**The Australian position (Mawson, Davis):** Mateship is neither the Norwegian communal practice nor the Finnish individual endurance — it is lateral solidarity between equals under pressure. The Australian framework doesn't ask anyone to suppress themselves for the group (Norwegian) or endure independently (Finnish); it says: you go through the hard thing, and I go through the hard thing beside you, and we're both changed by it together. Saint Douglas Mawson didn't survive because of community support structures or because of pure individual will — he survived with companions, losing them, and the Mawson community holds that specific quality: endurance-with-witness.

All three schools have enormous respect for each other. They would share resources in a crisis without question. They would also, in a long conversation after the crisis was over, disagree quite specifically about what the experience had meant and what it said about how people should live.

**What they argue about:** Whether endurance is primarily individual or communal. Whether talking about endurance diminishes it (the Norwegian and Finnish positions lean yes; the Australian position is more comfortable with storytelling around it). Whether Framheim's reconstruction counts as genuine endurance or as something more like historical cosplay (the Framheim community finds this an unfair characterization; the Mawson community says it without malice). Whether sisu can be learned or is culturally transmitted in a way that outsiders can't fully access.

## The Methodologists — German and British Scientific Communities

**Origin cities:** Neumayer (German, precision engineering), Rothera (British, empirical research), Halley (British, atmospheric science)

These communities share scientific identity and systematic worldview and have very different ideas about what scientific rigor actually means.

**The German tradition (Neumayer):** The Ausbildung model — structured apprenticeship leading to recognized mastery in a specific domain — produces people who believe that expertise requires depth before breadth. You learn one thing correctly before you attempt to synthesize. The Neumayer community's scientific culture prizes meticulous documentation, reproducible procedure, and the specific authority that comes from having mastered a craft through structured training. They are skeptical of interdisciplinary work that hasn't fully honored the disciplines it's drawing from.

**The British tradition (Rothera, Halley):** British empiricism works differently — it accumulates observations and is comfortable with provisional conclusions, updating incrementally as evidence accrues. The British scientific communities are more comfortable with "we don't know yet" than the German community tends to be, and more comfortable with interdisciplinary synthesis, because British scientific culture has always valued the generalist naturalist tradition alongside the specialist. They find the German insistence on disciplinary depth occasionally over-rigid. The German community finds the British comfort with provisional conclusions occasionally careless.

**Halley's specific inflection:** Halley was famous for atmospheric science and for living on a floating ice shelf. The city always knew it could move. This produces a specific British-scientific variant that has incorporated impermanence into its methodology — Halley scientists are unusually comfortable designing studies that account for the possibility that their measurement location will change. They have a philosophical sophistication about the relationship between the observer's position and the observation that distinguishes them from Rothera's more stable-ground empiricism.

**What they argue about:** Whether depth of mastery or breadth of synthesis produces better scientific understanding. Whether a provisional conclusion that turns out to be correct is better or worse than a certain conclusion that takes twice as long to reach. Whether Halley's "impermanence methodology" is a genuine scientific contribution or a local adaptation that shouldn't be generalized. Whether the Ausbildung model produces scientists or technicians — a question the Neumayer community considers insulting and the Rothera community considers fair.

## The Tertulia Communities — South American and Spanish Communities

**Origin cities:** Juan Carlos I (Spanish), Belgrano (Argentine), Esperanza (South American primary), Marambio (Argentine military)

These communities share a tradition of the communal gathering — the social event that is neither purely recreational nor purely functional but operates in the space between, where conversation is itself the activity and the quality of conversation is how you assess whether it was a good evening.

But they express this tradition differently:

**The Spanish tradition (Juan Carlos I):** The tertulia is the form. A tertulia is not an open gathering — it has a host, a guest list, an implied topic or sensibility that organizes it, and a quality of intellectual expectation. Not everyone is invited to every tertulia, and this is not considered rude — it is considered appropriate. The Spanish community's gatherings have a specific formality-within-informality: they last many hours, begin late, are organized around argument rather than consensus, and the argument is the point. You are not trying to win. You are trying to make the argument more interesting.

**The Argentine tradition (Belgrano, Esperanza, Marambio):** The asado is the form. An asado is formally about the food — specifically the fire and the meat or synthetic equivalent — and informally about everything else. The host tends the fire; the guests tend the conversation; both are ongoing and neither is finished until everyone is ready. Argentine gatherings are more egalitarian in their social structure than the Spanish tertulia: anyone can say anything, you don't need credentials to have an opinion, and the quality of your contribution matters less than the genuineness of your presence. You are not trying to make the argument interesting. You are trying to be really there.

**The Marambio inflection:** Marambio was a military airstrip, and its Argentine community has the asado tradition with a specific operational overlay. The gathering happens after the work — the shared meal is explicitly the reward for the shift completed, the job done right. This gives Marambio's version of the Argentine communal tradition a slightly different weight: presence matters, but earned presence matters more.

**What they argue about:** Whether the tertulia format or the asado format better serves the actual purpose of communal gathering. (The Spanish community considers this a silly question; the Argentine community considers it somewhat revealing about the Spanish community's values.) Whether intellectual formality enriches or gatekeeps. Whether the emphasis on argument (Spanish) or presence (Argentine) produces better outcomes for the community. Belgrano's specific archival mission sometimes creates tension: when preservation of the past becomes the primary topic of every gathering, some community members start to feel that the gathering has become a memorial rather than a living community event.

## The Archivists' Debate — Cities of Memory

**Origin cities:** Belgrano (archival mission), Palmer City (founding records), Cape Adare (deepest historical record), Framheim (built from archives), Scott (martyr-city, death-and-dignity tradition)

These communities share an orientation toward the past — toward preservation, documentation, and the weight of what came before — but they disagree profoundly about what the archive is FOR.

**Belgrano's position:** The archive is a rescue operation. Salagéa Aparast moves through the world collecting datashard testimony because if she doesn't, those testimonies die with the people who hold them. For the Belgrano community, archiving is emergency work — the building is on fire and you are carrying what you can. Speed and completeness are the values. The archive is for the future: future Tepenians need to know what was here.

**Palmer City's position:** The archive is a claim. The founding city's records are not just historical documentation — they are evidence for a position about what Tepenia is and who has the right to define it. Palmer City refugees who engage with archival work are often doing so with a specific audience in mind: they want the founding record to be legible to future decision-makers. The archive is advocacy with dates attached.

**Cape Adare's position:** The archive is continuity. The deepest historical clock in Tepenian Antarctic life belongs to Cape Adare — the first wintering, the first permanent habitation. Cape Adare archivists are less interested in advocacy or rescue than in the simple maintenance of an unbroken record. They would argue that Belgrano's urgency, while understandable, produces incomplete records because urgency doesn't allow for cross-referencing. They would argue that Palmer City's advocacy purpose subtly distorts what gets preserved and what doesn't.

**Framheim's position:** The archive is a blueprint. Framheim was built from historical records. For the Framheim community, an archive is not just a record of what was — it is a potential instruction manual for what could be reconstructed. This gives Framheim archivists a very specific relationship with completeness: they want the archive to contain enough information that something could be rebuilt from it. Other archival communities find this simultaneously the most practical and most haunting framing — it implies that everything is potentially already over, and the archive is for whoever comes after.

**Scott's position:** The archive is a testimony. Named after a martyr who died with dignity and wrote about it, the Scott community approaches preservation through the specific lens of the last statement — what the person chose to say when they knew they were running out of time. The Archive of Final Confessions is, in some ways, the most Scott-like institution in Concordia. Scott archivists are most interested in endpoint documents: what was said at the end, what was preserved under maximum pressure. They consider this the truest form of archiving because it strips away everything except what the person needed to say.

**What they argue about:** Whether urgency or rigor produces better archives. Whether an archive with an advocacy purpose is compromised or simply honest about its values. Whether Framheim's "blueprint" framing is practical wisdom or a kind of civilizational anxiety. Whether Scott's focus on endpoints fetishizes death rather than honoring life. Whether an archive should be for future historians or for the people whose stories it contains.

## The Question of the Unbroken Name — Destroyed City Internal Politics

**Origin cities:** All five destroyed/ruined communities — Zukelli, Soyuz, Belgrano, Palmer City, Casey — each face this question independently

Every destroyed-city community eventually confronts the same question, on their own timeline, in their own way: when does a person stop being "from Zukelli" (or Soyuz, or Belgrano, or Palmer City, or Casey) and become simply Tepenian?

This is not a question with an obvious answer. It is not even, on examination, a single question — it is several:

**The identity question:** Is your city of origin a permanent fact about you, or is it a diminishing weight that you can and perhaps should set down? The people who hold it as permanent are not wrong that it shaped them. The people who want to set it down are not wrong that carrying it indefinitely has costs.

**The community question:** If individuals from a destroyed city begin to identify primarily as Tepenian, does this dissolve the community? And if the community dissolves, is that a loss to be grieved or a natural conclusion to be honored? Some destroyed-city communities have a specific fear that their city will be "erased twice" — once by the war, and once by the gradual drift of their survivors into the wider Tepenian community.

**The generational question:** The children born after the destruction — and especially the grandchildren — have no personal memory of the city. They know it through stories and preserved materials. Are they "from" the destroyed city? Are they obligated to carry the identity? Do they have the right to decline it? Different communities answer this differently. Zukelli communities tend toward: the children are from Zukelli, full stop, because the community's continuity is the city's continuity. Soyuz communities tend toward: the children should know where they came from, but the choice of whether to carry it is theirs.

**The anniversary question:** How long do you hold the anniversary? Every destroyed-city community maintains some form of observance around the date of destruction. But the observances change over generations — they attenuate, formalize, or sometimes intensify. The communities that intensify their anniversaries over time are sometimes criticized (by outsiders and occasionally by insiders) for cultivating grief rather than processing it. The communities that let the anniversary formalize into something more ceremonial and less raw are sometimes criticized for moving on. Neither criticism is entirely fair.

Each of the five destroyed-city communities is living through this question on its own timeline, and they are at different points in the negotiation. Belgrano is perhaps furthest along — its archival mission gives the community purpose that is forward-oriented even while it is backward-facing. Palmer City is perhaps most stuck — the founding-site identity makes the city difficult to set down even partially, because setting it down feels like abandoning a claim that nobody else will make if Palmer City doesn't. Casey is perhaps most conflicted, because the destruction was unexpected (Casey was not a primary target) and the community has not fully resolved whether to integrate that surprise into its identity or to treat it as an arbitrary wound with no meaning.

## The Crossroads People — Cities of Intersection

**Origin cities:** Sayowa (dual-approach gateway), Esperanza (Peninsula gateway), Janbogo (metropolis, but crossroads in a different sense)

These communities share a specific quality: their identity, before the war, was organized around being the place where different things met rather than around being a thing itself.

**Sayowa's version:** The smallest of the three, and the most purely crossroads-identified. Sayowa sat at the intersection of the Atlantic and Pacific cultural corridors. Its residents navigated two different cultural streams simultaneously as a daily reality. The specific cognitive flexibility this produces — the ability to hold two frameworks without having to choose between them — is Sayowa's most distinctive cultural export. Sayowa people tend to be extremely comfortable with contradiction, extremely skeptical of people who insist there is one right answer, and occasionally insufferable about it.

**Esperanza's version:** The Peninsula gateway. Everyone came through Esperanza on their way to or from somewhere. The city's crossroads character was temporal as well as spatial — it was a city of arrivals and departures, and its residents developed a specific quality of provisional intimacy: the ability to form genuine connection quickly, because connection that waits for permanence never forms in a transit city. Esperanza people are often described as immediately warm but difficult to know deeply, which is partially a misreading — they form deep connections, but they form them faster than most people and are then surprised when the other person hasn't kept up.

**Janbogo's version:** Janbogo's crossroads character was less about being between cultures than about being the place where everything happened. The largest city, the most powerful node, the place everything ran through. Janbogo people have a specific quality of cosmopolitan centrality — they are not between things, they are the thing everything converges on. This produces a cultural confidence that can read as arrogance and is occasionally arrogance, but is more accurately described as the worldview of people who grew up in a city where being from here meant something specific and large.

The tension between Sayowa and Janbogo as crossroads communities is interesting because they represent opposite versions of the same quality. Sayowa's crossroads identity is about navigating between larger things — being the hinge. Janbogo's crossroads identity is about being the center that everything rotates around. The Sayowa community finds Janbogo's version of cosmopolitanism slightly mistaken — centrality is not the same as openness. The Janbogo community finds Sayowa's version slightly precious — a small city that made a virtue of its smallness.

**What they argue about:** Whether genuinely cosmopolitan identity requires having been at the center or requires having navigated the margins. Whether the crossroads identity is a stable thing to build a community around or whether it eventually requires choosing a home. Whether Esperanza's provisional-intimacy culture is warmth or avoidance of depth (Esperanza people find this question somewhat insulting). Whether Janbogo's survival — while Zukelli burned, while Palmer City crumbled — is a fact about its character or simply a fact about its size.

## The Saint Cities — Communities Named After the Dead

**Origin cities:** Mawson (Saint Douglas Mawson), Scott (Saint Robert Falcon Scott), Framheim (Amundsen's heritage, though Amundsen himself survived)

These cities carry the names of specific historical figures, and in each case the figure's story has become part of the city's identity in ways that are not always straightforward.

**Mawson:** Saint Douglas survived. He lost his companions. He walked back to base alone, or nearly alone, across impossible distance, in impossible condition. The Mawson community carries this as a foundation story, but it is an ambivalent one — Saint Douglas didn't survive through sheer will, he survived partly through luck and partly through the deaths of others. The Mawson community's relationship with endurance is therefore not uncomplicated triumph. It is endurance-with-cost, survival-with-grief. The Saint is not a pure hero; he is a witness to loss who also happened to live.

**Scott:** Saint Robert died. He wrote about it with extraordinary clarity and without self-pity. "We shall stick it out to the end, but we are getting weaker" is the city's founding testimony, and it is a testimony of dignity in failure rather than triumph. The Scott community has inherited this as their primary orientation toward mortality: it is not the worst thing to die, if you die having done the thing correctly, having written it down, having not blamed the wrong people. This produces a community that is, in some ways, more comfortable with failure than most — not because they court it, but because their founding figure demonstrated that failure endured with integrity is not shameful.

**Framheim:** Amundsen won. He reached the Pole first, he came back, he did it without the drama that killed Scott. The Amundsen heritage that Framheim carries is therefore a heritage of competence and preparation — of having done the unglamorous work correctly so that the dramatic moment, when it came, wasn't dramatic at all. The Framheim community doesn't romanticize endurance or death; they romanticize preparation and execution. Doing the thing right so that it doesn't become a story of suffering is the ideal.

**What they argue about:** Whether the founding figure's story is a model to emulate, a story to honor, or a fact about origin that has no special normative weight. Whether Scott's dignity-in-death tradition makes the Scott community unusually wise about mortality or unusually comfortable with failure in ways that might not always serve them. Whether Amundsen's unglamorous competence is more admirable than Mawson's extraordinary survival or Scott's extraordinary death — and whether "admirable" is even the right lens for any of these.

The three communities have a specific triangulated relationship: they recognize each other as "the cities named after the expedition era" and share a historical consciousness that most of Tepenia doesn't bother with. They also disagree, sometimes sharply, about what lesson the expedition era is supposed to teach.

## The McMurdo Problem — Communities of Lost Function

**Origin cities:** Fort McMurdo (primary); Esperanza (secondary)

Fort McMurdo was the de facto capital of real-world Antarctica. Everything went through it. For the first decades of Tepenian settlement, McMurdo's logistical heritage was the backbone of how the continent operated. Its residents knew this. Their identity was not just "we are from McMurdo" but "we are the people who kept everything running."

That function is gone.

The McMurdo refugee community carries a specific loss that is not the loss of a city but the loss of a role. The city still exists in memory. What doesn't exist anymore is the position it held — the logistical center, the place everything ran through. Other cities have absorbed those functions. The McMurdo community's expertise and institutional knowledge are real and valuable, but they are no longer uniquely necessary in the way they once were.

This produces a community organized around a kind of quiet professional grief alongside the geographic displacement grief. McMurdo people tend to find each other through work — through shared expertise in logistics, supply chain, Antarctic operational history — and the community is therefore more work-organized than most refugee communities, which tend to organize around neighborhood or national character.

The internal tension is between:

**The Institutionalists**, who believe the McMurdo tradition lives on as long as the knowledge is being applied somewhere, by someone who knows where it came from — who believe that function, not place, is what they are preserving.

**The Specificists**, who feel that decontextualized McMurdo knowledge is just logistics expertise, and that what they've actually lost is the specific thing that made it McMurdo — the scale, the centrality, the position in the network. They are not bitter about this, exactly. But they are precise about it.

Esperanza has a version of this. The Peninsula gateway function is gone. Esperanza was the arrival point, the city of first landing, the place where "hope" was not just a name but a function. Refugees no longer arrive through Esperanza. The Esperanza community's loss is therefore also partly a loss of function — the city of arrivals no longer receives anyone.
