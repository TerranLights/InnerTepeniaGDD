# Doll Visual Design Canon
## Canonical reference for the appearance, construction, and physical aging of Dolls

---

## Exterior Appearance

Dolls look like people. From the outside, a Doll is indistinguishable from a human at a glance — synthetic skin, hair, human proportions, regular clothing. They do not have visible mechanical components, chassis panels, or exposed metalwork under normal circumstances.

**The one universal external tell: luminescent irises.**

The iris of every Doll emits a soft internal luminescence. The glow is in the iris itself — not the sclera, not the whole eyeball. It reads as a vivid, internally lit iris rather than a glowing eye. Iris colors vary freely by individual.

This is the only feature that visually marks a Doll as a Doll.

---

## Iris Color and Emotional Shifting

Iris color is not fixed. It shifts in response to emotional state — a Doll in distress, in joy, in focused concentration, or in anger will show a corresponding shift in iris hue, saturation, or luminescence intensity. The shift is subtle and requires familiarity to read accurately, but it is consistent and involuntary.

**Who knows about this:** Common knowledge among humans who spend significant time in mixed communities (such as Concordia residents of any generation). Not known to humans from isolated or segregated contexts — Upper Earth defectors, frontier settlers, and others with limited Doll contact may not be aware of it. Reading a Doll's emotional state through their irises is a social skill, not a biological one, and its absence in a character is itself information about their background.

Dolls are generally aware that their irises are readable. How self-conscious any given Doll is about this varies by personality.

---

## Build Type Differences

A care model and a structural maintenance model look like two different people — not like two different machines. The difference is in body type and proportions, which are selected by the manufacturer for the intended function. A heavy-duty maintenance Doll (such as Michelle Stanton or Griselda Chávez) is built with a physically robust body type suited to that work. A diplomatic protocol model has different proportions entirely.

**The differences between build types are otherwise internal:**
- Internal fortification (structural integrity of the frame beneath the skin)
- Gel brain architecture (processing configuration for the intended function)
- Internal connectors and component layout (specific to function)
- Battery capacity and output (heavier work requires more power)

None of these internal differences are visible from the outside. A Doll wearing clothing looks exactly like a person of the corresponding body type.

---

## Damage, Repair, and Wear

Physical damage and its repair are visible on the exterior as they would be on a person: scarring, graft lines where replacement skin doesn't quite match the surrounding tone or texture, pigmentation irregularities from cold or chemical exposure. There is no visible metalwork, no exposed "chassis" — the underlying structure is internal and enclosed.

A Doll who has had a limb replaced will have a limb with replacement synthetic skin — possibly a slightly different texture at the join line, or a minor tone difference if the replacement wasn't precisely matched.

Long-term wear from specific working conditions accumulates in the skin: cold-exposure damage reads as altered pigmentation or texture in affected areas; chemical exposure leaves specific marks; repeated physical stress in specific areas produces callusing or micro-scarring in the synthetic skin.

---

## Aging and Death

Dolls are not immortal. They live significantly longer than humans, but they age and they die.

**External aging:** Older synthetic skin has a different quality from new — it accumulates its years in texture and surface response rather than in visible deterioration. Very old Dolls do not look elderly in the human sense; they look like a person whose skin carries something specific that newer skin doesn't.

**Internal aging and death:** The components that constitute a Doll's self degrade over time:
- **Gel brain:** Can wear out. Degradation affects memory, processing, and eventually personality coherence.
- **Battery (the "heart"):** Can become irreplaceable and burn out. Battery failure is typically terminal.
- **Central personality module:** Can become corrupted beyond recovery. Corruption past a threshold is effectively the death of the self — the Doll may still be physically functional while the personality is gone, which is its own category of horror.

From a Doll's perspective, any of these constitutes death — a total cessation of the self that cannot be revived from. Parts can be maintained and replaced, which is why Dolls live as long as they do. But no maintenance regimen is permanent. A Doll who cannot afford maintenance, who has lost access to compatible replacement parts, or whose core systems have degraded past repair will die.

A Doll near death shows it primarily through the iris: the luminescence becomes less stable, less consistent, dimmer. The iris-shift emotional response slows or becomes erratic. This is the most visible external sign of terminal decline.

## Manufacturing Eras

**En.Ki.Du (built 2083, U.R.U.K.):** The first sentient robot and the singular exception to the human-appearance standard. Noticeably robotic in construction, with humanlike features but clearly mechanical exterior. He is the prototype from which all subsequent Doll manufacturing diverged. No Doll built after him shares his exterior aesthetic.

**Post-En.Ki.Du standard (2083 onward):** All manufactured Dolls have full synthetic skin and look human from the outside. This standard has been consistent across the entire history of Doll manufacturing. There is no era in which "early" Dolls look robotic — only En.Ki.Du.

**Era differences in skin quality:** Early manufacturing (circa 2500s) produced synthetic skin that is fully human-looking but has less dynamic environmental response than modern skin. Contemporary synthetic skin micro-adjusts to temperature, humidity, and ambient conditions. Older skin does not, or does so more slowly. On a Doll of Mareen's age (built early 2500s), this reads as a specific texture quality — not wrong, not uncanny, but carrying its years in a way that is perceptible to someone who knows what to look for.

**Early-era irises:** Dolls built in the early manufacturing period (through approximately the late 2500s) tend to have more complex iris patterns — deeper color range, more layered shifting response. Early manufacturing emphasized the iris as a primary sensory array more heavily than later designs, which distributed sensory function more broadly. Older Dolls' irises are often more vivid and more readable than those of newer models.

## Note on Existing NPC Profile Documents

The NPC profiles in `District-Quest-NPCs/` were written before this canon was established and contain physical description language that is inconsistent with it — specifically, references to "chassis," "alloy," "panels," and visible mechanical repair work. These descriptions should be understood as referring to internal structure (not visible externally) and will be updated to use appropriate synthetic skin equivalents.
