# Robot Physiology and Cultural Practices

Foundational reference for robot biology, sensory experience, and cultural behaviors in Inner Tepenia. This document covers what robots consume, how they experience the world physically, and the cultural practices that have developed from both their nature and their centuries of cohabitation with humans.

---

## Physiology Basics

Robots do not require oxygen and have no respiratory system in the human sense. They can operate in vacuum — including on the outer hulls of spacecraft — without protective equipment. They do not breathe.

Their internal systems require:
- **Engine power** — the primary energy source driving all functions
- **Thermal regulation** — internal systems generate heat that must be managed; this is an ongoing physiological requirement, not an optional comfort

They do not age in the biological sense, though components degrade over time and require maintenance or replacement.

## Consumption — Robot Equivalents

### Siligel — Robot Food

Siligel is the established robot equivalent of food: a substance consumed to maintain and repair internal systems. Its exact composition and the full range of what it does internally is subject to further design work, but its role as "robot food" is confirmed canon.

### Coolant — Robot Drinks

Robots manage internal heat through coolant systems. Coolant functions as the robot equivalent of drinking — a liquid intake with genuine physiological purpose (thermal regulation). Like human beverages, coolant exists in a range of formulations, qualities, and varieties. A robot drinking coolant after hard work is the functional and cultural equivalent of a human drinking water after exertion.

**Robot coffee:** A specialty coolant formulation that produces particular internal effects beyond basic thermal regulation. The exact composition is TBD, but it sits within the established coolant category — consumed for the experience it produces, not purely for temperature management. The existence of "robot coffee" as a distinct product category confirms that robots consume things for pleasure and experience, not only for maintenance.

## Smoking — Canon Explanation

### The established fact

Robots smoke. This is confirmed by reference imagery and is canon. The specific form is most likely **robot-specific vapor products** (electronic rather than combustion-based) rather than traditional cigarettes, though both may exist in-world. The experience of smoking is genuine — not performed, not simulated — but it functions differently than it does for a human.

### Why it works mechanically (Option B + C combined)

Robots have internal atmospheric management systems — not for respiration, but for **thermal regulation and sensory input processing**. These systems move air or vapor-equivalent substances through internal chambers as part of managing heat and environmental awareness. Drawing vapor through these systems creates real, detectable internal states:

- **Thermal sensation** — warm vapor interacts with internal thermal sensors; the warmth is genuinely felt
- **Chemical interaction** — vapor compounds interact with internal environmental sensors in ways that produce detectable signal changes; robot-specific formulations (Option C) are designed precisely to target these systems
- **Internal state change** — the result is a genuine shift in internal processing state, not identical to human dopamine/serotonin response, but real in its own terms

Robot vapor products are a distinct consumer category, formulated specifically for robot internal systems rather than adapted from human products. This is the same design pattern as robot coffee within the coolant category — purpose-built for robot physiology, not a human product repurposed.

### Why it works culturally — the hands-and-fire insight

Beyond the physiological mechanism, smoking carries cultural meaning that maps onto robots in a way that is structurally identical to its human meaning, even though the mechanism is completely different.

One theory — confirmed as canon for this world — holds that a significant part of smoking's appeal beyond the chemical is that it unifies two things that are specifically, distinctly human:

1. **The hand** — opposable thumbs, manual dexterity, tool use; one of the core differentiators between humans and the rest of the animal kingdom
2. **Fire** — harnessed energy; traditionally considered magical, civilizationally transformative, communally binding (storytelling around fire is itself a distinctly human phenomenon)

A cigarette or vaporizer held in the hand and lit with flame is a single ritual object that unifies both. The gesture of smoking is hands + fire collapsed into one act.

For robots, **the parallel holds, but the "fire" moves inward:**

- **The manipulator** — robots have hands, or functional equivalents; the grip, the gesture, the bringing-to-the-mouth are all present and identical in form to the human version
- **Internal energy as fire** — a robot does not harness an external flame; a robot *is* an energy system. When vapor is drawn through internal thermal management systems, the robot's own internal energy processing *is* the fire. The harnessing is interior rather than exterior, but it completes the same conceptual circuit.

A robot smoking is therefore doing something **structurally identical** to a human smoking — unifying manipulator use with the harnessing of energy — even though the specific mechanism is entirely different. The ritual means the same thing by different means.

### Ambiguity — preserved by design

What exactly a robot experiences when smoking varies by individual and is deliberately left ambiguous in the world. Some robots experience something they would describe as genuinely pleasurable. Some smoke primarily for the ritual and the aesthetic — the act itself, the gesture, the visible vapor. Some do both. No universal answer is imposed. This ambiguity maps directly onto the game's central question about robot consciousness: the experience cannot be fully verified from the outside, and the robot cannot fully translate it into terms a human would recognize.

## Sexuality

Robots in Concordia are bisexual by default. They do not organize attraction by the gender of their partner. This applies universally across all robot characters — companions and NPCs alike.

This is distinct from human characters in the world, who are heterosexual. The difference is not framed as a judgment in either direction — it is simply a feature of how robots experience and express attraction, consistent with their different relationship to embodiment, identity, and social organization.
