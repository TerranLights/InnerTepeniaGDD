**Based on:** Byrd Station (USA), Marie Byrd Land, West Antarctica (~80°01'S, 119°31'W)
**Status:** Survived — struggling; one of only three cities in Tepenia still functioning as a city at meaningful scale — alongside Concordia and Janbogo; other survivors are either small outpost communities, damaged partial operations, or effectively unreachable

## Geographic Basis

Byrd sits in the deep interior of Marie Byrd Land, West Antarctica — not on a coast, not at a dramatic geographic feature, but in the relatively featureless interior of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet at approximately 1,530 meters above sea level. This altitude is substantially lower than Concordia (3,233m) and makes Byrd far more habitable from a physiological standpoint, but it is still deep inland, still isolated, and still Antarctic.

West Antarctica is geologically and glaciologically distinct from East Antarctica. The East Antarctic Ice Sheet sits on a continental landmass; the West Antarctic Ice Sheet sits largely on bedrock that lies below sea level, making it a marine ice sheet — inherently less stable, subject to different long-term dynamics, and sitting above geology that in some areas is volcanically active. The ground beneath Byrd is not the ancient, stable bedrock of the East Antarctic plateau. It shifts, slowly, in ways that the city's engineers have had to account for since the founding.

The real Byrd Station has a specific and peculiar history: established on the surface, it was progressively buried by snow accumulation over the decades of its operation until it became a subsurface facility. In Tepenia, this history has a direct architectural consequence — the older layers of Byrd are buried, and the city has grown upward and outward over generations, leaving a layered stratigraphy of construction beneath the current surface. The deepest parts of the old city are accessible by tunnels; some sections have been abandoned to the ice.

Byrd's isolation is of a different character from Concordia's. Concordia is isolated but connected by three highways. Byrd's connection to the rest of Tepenia depends on infrastructure that has not yet been fully established in lore — and may have always been more tenuous than Concordia's.

#### Notable Weather Phenomena

- **Buried infrastructure:** snow accumulation is a persistent reality; structures that are not actively maintained against snow loading eventually become submerged; this is not a dramatic event but a slow constant pressure on the city's surface architecture
- **West Antarctic Ice Sheet dynamics:** the ground beneath Byrd is not geologically stable in the way East Antarctic bedrock is; slow subsurface movement and ice sheet dynamics create long-term structural concerns that the city has always had to manage
- **Polar night (~108 days):** long, though slightly shorter than Fort McMurdo's 118 days; the absence of coastal aurora-generating factors means the Aurora Australis displays at Byrd are excellent but the atmospheric context is starker than a coastal site
- **Precipitation:** higher than the polar desert conditions of Concordia; Byrd gets meaningful snowfall, which combined with the burial dynamics makes snow management an ongoing civic concern

#### Gameplay Notes

- **The buried city:** the layered stratigraphy of Byrd — surface city above, older buried sections accessible by tunnel — is a major environmental design asset; the buried sections are a dungeon-like underworld of older Tepenian construction, abandoned equipment, and sealed-off history
- **Structural precarity:** West Antarctic Ice Sheet instability could manifest as a slow-burn environmental threat in DLC 2 — not a sudden disaster, but a city that is aware its foundations are not entirely trustworthy; some sections may have already been evacuated due to structural concerns
- **Isolation character:** Byrd's poor road/highway connection means the DLC 2 setting would feel more genuinely cut off than Concordia does; getting there is part of the challenge
- **Midnight sun (~128 days):** the longest midnight sun period of any Tepenian city; the DLC 2 setting could lean heavily into the psychologically strange quality of months of unbroken daylight in an isolated struggling city
- **Hardcore Mode influences:**
  - Snow management as an active mechanic: accumulation on exposed equipment, blocked pathways, buried access points are ongoing practical problems in Byrd rather than occasional events
  - Structural hazard zones: sections of the city condemned due to ice sheet movement would be off-limits or dangerous to enter — a Hardcore Mode player pushing into condemned areas faces structural collapse risk
  - Isolation supply mechanics: Byrd's tenuous connection to the broader highway system means supply chain in a Hardcore Mode context would be the most precarious of any surviving city; what Byrd cannot produce internally, it largely does without

## Founding

**Settled:** Shortly after the Falkland Treaty (June 21, 2564) — but not discovered by accident. Byrd Station had been abandoned as a surface facility around 2005 and was entirely buried by 2564; there was no visible structure to find. What existed were records.

Old preserved maps — held in the ruins of former Palmer Station, former Rothera Station, and former Belgrano Station II, three of the earliest Tepenian settlements — documented the location of Byrd Station in the West Antarctic interior. A founding expedition, equipped with rudimentary early-era Kharkovchankas (the vehicles existed in basic form from the Soviet Antarctic program, but by 2564 standards were primitive compared to what would be developed over the following century), made the crossing of the West Antarctic interior following those maps.

What they found was not a station. It was a field of compacted snow over buried infrastructure, with no surface indication of what lay beneath. They set up a small surface encampment, began probing, and found the tunnels. What followed was an inversion of normal settlement logic: instead of building outward from a surface foundation, the community built downward first — expanding and inhabiting the buried tunnel network, using the existing underground spaces as the core of what became an underground town. Only later, as the population grew and the underground was mapped and claimed, did expansion move outward through lateral tunneling and then upward, establishing above-ground structures on the surface above the buried city.

Byrd is therefore unique among Tepenian cities in having been founded underground before it existed on the surface. Its civic center, its oldest neighborhoods, its founding-era character — all of it is below ground. The surface city is the newer addition.

**Founding population:** Primarily American exiles, given the station's US origins, alongside Pacific-region communities and whoever else was willing to follow a set of old maps into the deep West Antarctic interior on the strength of a theory. Byrd was never a first-choice destination — it was too remote, too isolated, too strange — but it attracted people who valued exactly those qualities or who had reasons to want distance from the more connected eastern cities. The expedition character of the founding — the deliberate choice to go somewhere difficult because the records said something was there — persisted in the city's culture.

The name was kept, and with it came the inheritance of **St. Richard** — Richard E. Byrd, the American explorer whose expeditions mapped the region the city now occupies. St. Richard is venerated in Byrd as its founding Saint, the man whose work in West Antarctica made it knowable and eventually habitable. The city carries his name the way Scott carries Scott's — not as a civic formality but as a genuine relationship.

Once the underground archives were located and catalogued, they became a secondary foundation of the Byrd subnet's historical knowledge — including the records that would later enable the reconstruction of Framheim and Little America on the Ross Ice Shelf.

## Character & Culture

Byrd developed the culture of a city that knew it was on its own. Too far from the eastern cities to rely on them easily, connected to the broader Federation by infrastructure that was always somewhat tenuous, Byrd's population developed a self-sufficiency that was less pride than necessity and became pride over time.

It was never the largest city, never the most glamorous, never the cultural center. It was the western city — the one on the other side of the continent, the one that got there by crossing something most people didn't cross. That journey, and the commitment it implied, shaped who came to Byrd and why they stayed.

The buried-city architecture gave Byrd a peculiar spatial culture: interior life was not just comfortable but structurally necessary. The underground portions of the city — tunnels, buried chambers, sealed older sections — became the connective tissue of Byrd's social life in a way that has no equivalent in any other Tepenian city. People moved between structures underground, met underground, lived underground in the oldest parts of the city. The surface was where you worked and where the sky was; the tunnels were where you were.

**Nationally known for:** Being the western city; the buried tunnels; St. Richard; an earned reputation for self-sufficiency that the eastern cities sometimes found admirable and sometimes found standoffish.

## Connection to Concordia

Byrd is the most geographically distant surviving city from Concordia. Communication between them is limited by the Planetary Split Brain (Byrd is a different subnet) and by physical distance. Concordia knows Byrd is struggling; Byrd knows Concordia is the last major city. What either city can actually do for the other, given the distance and the infrastructure situation, is an open question that DLC 2 will need to address.

**How Byrd refugees reached Concordia — the aviation route:**
Byrd refugees who made it to Concordia did not travel overland across the continent. They flew. Byrd maintained aviation infrastructure — the only city in Tepenia to do so — and the distance from Byrd to Janbogo, while substantial, was within the operational range of the aircraft with one refueling stop.

That stop was Framheim / Little America, on the Ross Ice Shelf. The aircraft could not fly from Byrd to Janbogo in a single leg; they stopped at Framheim to refuel, then continued to Janbogo. Janbogo did not have the infrastructure to refuel the aircraft, so Framheim was the only point on this route where refueling was possible — the return journey worked the same way in reverse. Whether Framheim was purely a fuel stop or also functioned as a transit hub or destination in its own right is TBD.

From Janbogo, passengers made the rest of the journey to Concordia by road via Hwy 183. The aviation route's purpose was simply to bridge the gap between Byrd and the Tepenian road network; Janbogo was the point where that bridge landed.

This route is why any Byrd refugees exist in Concordia at all. Without the aircraft, the journey would have been functionally impossible. Even with them, it required a deliberate decision to leave — not a desperate last-minute escape but a conscious departure through a working, if limited, system.

**The breakdown of the aircraft:**
At some point — before, during, or after the Long Night War — Byrd's aircraft broke down due to the extreme environmental and climate conditions. For reasons not yet established in lore, they could not be repaired to working order. The specific reasons why repairs failed are TBD and may be significant to DLC 2's plot — whether it was lost expertise, unavailable parts, damage to the maintenance infrastructure, or something else entirely has not been determined.

The practical consequence is absolute: when the aircraft stopped flying, Byrd stopped being reachable from the rest of Tepenia. No overland road connects it to the highway system in any established way. The refugees who made it to Concordia via the aviation route were the last people to leave Byrd for Concordia. Everyone who remained after the aircraft broke down has been there since.

Byrd refugees in Concordia are therefore not merely rare — they are a closed population. No one has joined them from Byrd since the aircraft went down. The Byrd community in Concordia, small as it is, knows things about Byrd's current state only up to the point when the last refugee left. What is happening in Byrd now is unknown to everyone in Concordia.

## Current Status

Byrd is surviving but struggling. The nature of the struggle is TBD for DLC 2 design — whether it is a resource crisis, a governance crisis, a geological crisis from ice sheet instability, or some combination of these has not been established.

What is established: the city is alive, it is not comfortable, its aircraft are grounded and irreparable, and it has been effectively sealed off from the rest of Tepenia since the last flight out. No one has arrived from or departed to Concordia since the aviation route closed. The Byrd community in Concordia is a closed population with outdated information about a city they can no longer reach. Something is wrong enough that a DLC is built around getting there — which itself implies the journey is not straightforward — to find out what.

## Legacy

Byrd's legacy in Concordia is primarily the fact of its existence: the knowledge that there is another city, on the other side of the continent, still standing. In the dark periods of Concordia's energy crisis, that knowledge has a specific kind of weight — you are not the last place, not quite, and whatever is happening in Byrd is happening independently. Whether that is comforting or not depends on what you believe is happening there.
