## Geographic Basis

Framheim sits on the Ross Ice Shelf — the largest floating ice shelf in the world, roughly the size of France, projecting from the continental coast into the Ross Sea. Like Halley on the Brunt Ice Shelf, structures built here are built on moving ice: the shelf flows slowly seaward, accumulates snow, and periodically calves icebergs at its edge. Unlike Halley, which was architecturally designed from the start for relocation, Framheim's historical basis (Amundsen's huts, then Byrd's Little America stations) were semi-permanent structures that were progressively buried by snow accumulation and eventually lost to calving or burial.

The Bay of Whales — the natural harbor indentation in the Ross Ice Shelf edge where both Amundsen and Byrd established their bases — is the most significant geographic feature of this site. In the real world, the Bay of Whales no longer exists in its 1911 form; successive calving events throughout the twentieth century reshaped the ice shelf edge and eliminated the protected harbor. In Tepenia, the question of whether the Bay of Whales survived into the exile era, whether it persisted through centuries of ice shelf dynamics, or whether Framheim had to adapt to the loss of its natural harbor is a significant open question.

Framheim sits at approximately 78°30'S — on the Ross Ice Shelf, between the Ross Sea coast and the deep interior. The latitude is similar to Fort McMurdo, giving it a comparable polar night, but the ice shelf environment is colder than the coastal McMurdo Sound, warmer than Byrd's interior position, and without the volcanic or geological complexity of Ross Island.

The name "Framheim" means "Home of the Fram" in Norwegian — the Fram being Amundsen's ship, which anchored at the Bay of Whales in January 1911 while Amundsen established his winter base. The name was chosen for the Tepenian settlement as an act of deliberate inheritance: this was where St. Roald prepared for the journey that made him a Saint.

#### Notable Weather Phenomena

- **Snow burial:** the primary long-term environmental threat at Framheim; the ice shelf accumulates snow continuously; structures not maintained against accumulation are progressively buried; the historical Little America stations were all eventually buried and some were lost to calving; Framheim's relationship with snow burial is a defining feature of the city's history and architecture
- **Calving events:** the Ross Ice Shelf periodically calves large icebergs from its seaward edge; the Bay of Whales area was particularly vulnerable in the real world; in Tepenia, the question of whether major calving events have affected Framheim's position or accessibility is TBD
- **Barrier winds:** the Transantarctic Mountains create barrier wind effects on the Ross Ice Shelf; these can produce unexpectedly strong wind events on the shelf surface even far from the mountains
- **The missing bay:** if the Bay of Whales no longer exists in Tepenia (lost to calving), Framheim's maritime access situation would have fundamentally changed at some point in its history

#### Gameplay Notes

- **The buried history:** like Byrd, Framheim has layers of buried older construction accessible underground; Amundsen's 1911 huts and the Little America stations are beneath the current city surface, accessible by tunnel if they haven't collapsed; these would be extraordinary archaeological spaces
- **The aviation connection:** Framheim appears in the Byrd aviation route as the refueling stop; the runway, fuel storage, and aviation infrastructure (or its ruins) would be distinctive features; whether this infrastructure still functions or is entirely derelict is TBD
- **The Bay of Whales question:** if the bay is gone, Framheim's former harbor is now ice shelf edge or open sea; the ruins of harbor infrastructure in that case would be partially calved away — a dramatic and eerie visual
- **Hardcore Mode influences:**
  - Snow burial as active mechanic: same as Byrd; surface access points become blocked; routes require clearing or digging
  - Ice shelf structural uncertainty: calving risk zones and buried voids create hazardous terrain in a Hardcore Mode context
  - Aviation fuel infrastructure: if any fuel remains in Framheim's storage from the last flights, it would be an extraordinarily valuable Hardcore Mode resource

## Founding

**Settled:** Not on pre-existing infrastructure — by the time of the Falkland Treaty (2564), the original Framheim huts (1911) and all five Little America stations (1929–1958) had long since been destroyed. Centuries of Ross Ice Shelf movement, snow burial, and calving had eliminated every physical trace of the historical sites at the Bay of Whales. There was nothing left to build on.

The settlement of Framheim was instead an act of deliberate reconstruction. After Byrd was established — itself founded via old maps found at Palmer, Rothera, and Belgrano II, then expanded downward through buried tunnel discovery — the underground archives at Byrd contained the historical records of Framheim and the Little America stations: their locations, their layouts, their ice shelf context. Working from this documentary foundation, a later expedition calculated the current Ross Ice Shelf position accounting for centuries of movement since the 1910s, and traveled there to establish a new city. The name "Framheim" was chosen in full awareness that this was not the original — it was a successor settlement at a location derived from scholarship, not inheritance.

Critically, the founders built with ice movement in mind from the start. The fate of the original Framheim and Little America stations was known from the historical record; the same mistake would not be made twice. Framheim's architecture was designed for the Ross Ice Shelf's realities — accounting for snow accumulation, potential calving, and the shelf's slow seaward movement — in the same philosophical tradition as Halley's ski-mounted modules, though with its own specific engineering approach.

Byrd Station itself was abandoned as a surface facility by the early 21st century (around 2005) and had been progressively buried by snow accumulation for decades before that. By 2564, Byrd's inhabited presence was entirely underground — a tunnel city that had grown downward as the surface became inaccessible. The historical records found in Byrd's underground archives, preserved through centuries of burial, were the documentary foundation on which Framheim was rebuilt. Those records are the most direct link between the pre-exile Antarctic world and the Tepenian settlements that inherited it.

**Founding population:** The Byrd subnet's western character (primarily American and Pacific-region communities) is reflected at Framheim; specifics TBD. The site's dual Amundsen/Byrd heritage gave it an unusual split identity — Norwegian through St. Roald's name, American through the Little America legacy and St. Richard's later connection.

The name Framheim was chosen deliberately, privileging the older and more legendary association. St. Roald is the Saint of the South Pole; Framheim is where he prepared. The founders knew they were naming a reconstruction, and named it anyway — the act of naming was itself the claim of inheritance.

## Character & Culture

Framheim's character was shaped by its function as much as its location. It was never a large city — the Ross Ice Shelf, away from the coastline and highway network, was not a natural population center. What Framheim was, and what it remained, was a waypoint: the place you passed through, the place you stopped. Its role as the aviation refueling hub between Byrd and Janbogo gave it a specific transient quality. People came to Framheim on their way to somewhere else.

This produced a culture of hospitality and logistics rather than deep civic identity — a city oriented toward the needs of travelers. The dual heritage of Amundsen and Byrd gave it historical weight that its size did not naturally command, and the sense of being a place where significant journeys began (or paused) was part of how Framheim understood itself.

Whether Framheim was purely a fuel stop on the aviation route or also a transit hub and destination in its own right — whether people came to Framheim rather than just through it — is TBD.

**Nationally known for:** The aviation route. St. Roald's preparatory base. Being a city rebuilt from scholarship rather than inheritance — founded not on surviving infrastructure but on records found in Byrd's underground archives. Being the Ross Ice Shelf city.

## Connection to Concordia

Framheim's connection to Concordia was indirect — through the aviation route and through Janbogo. The chain was: Byrd (by air) → Framheim (refuel) → Janbogo (by air, then by road) → Hwy 183 → Concordia. When the aircraft flew, Framheim was a link in the only chain connecting the Byrd-subnet interior to Concordia. When they stopped, that chain broke.

Concordia has awareness of Framheim through the Byrd diaspora community — the refugees who traveled this route carry knowledge of Framheim as a waypoint in their journey. It is part of the story of how they got here, not a place most of them lingered.

## Legacy

Framheim's legacy is the journey it enabled and the journeys it remembered. As the place where St. Roald prepared to reach the South Pole, it carries a specific kind of sacred weight in Tepenian culture — not where he arrived, but where he left from, which is its own category of significance. And as the refueling stop that made the Byrd aviation route possible, it is part of the story of how the last refugees from Byrd reached Concordia at all.

That these two legacies — the ancient (known only through records, the physical sites long gone) and the recent (a reconstructed city that honored its predecessor by name) — inhabit the same location is the specific quality of Framheim that no other Tepenian city shares. It is the only Tepenian city founded not on ruins but on the memory of ruins.
