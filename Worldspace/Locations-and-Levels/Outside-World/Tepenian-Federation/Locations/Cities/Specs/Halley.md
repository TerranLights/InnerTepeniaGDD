# Halley

**Based on:** Halley Stations I–VI (UK), Brunt Ice Shelf, Coats Land, Weddell Sea (~75°35'S, 26°34'W)
**Status:** Damaged; partially operational — DLC 5 content
**Arcanet Subnet:** Halley ("Atlantic") — subnet hub
**Highway access:** Hwy 59 (Atlantic Throughway / Arcanet Line — runs south from Halley to Amundsen Station at the South Pole); Hwy 7 (Queen Maud Land coastal highway — runs southwest to Belgrano and northeast along the Queen Maud Land coast to the other Halley subnet cities)
**Significance:** The city that moves; one of four Tepenian cities built on floating ice shelves (alongside Neumayer on the Ekström Ice Shelf and Framheim/Little America on the Ross Ice Shelf), and the only one architecturally designed from the start for relocation across the moving ice; hub of the Atlantic subnet; anchor point of Hwy 59, which functions simultaneously as a highway and an Arcanet cable corridor — damage to Hwy 59 disrupts both surface transit and Atlantic subnet communications simultaneously
**DLC:** Halley subnet — DLC 5 (Atlantic Coastal Region); damaged but partially operational — DLC 5 centerpiece and subnet hub

## Geographic Basis

Halley sits on the Brunt Ice Shelf — a floating platform of ice projecting from the Coats Land coast into the Weddell Sea. The ice shelf is not bedrock. It is not continent. It is frozen ocean, compressed snow, accumulated ice, moving at a rate of roughly 400–700 meters per year as it flows slowly toward the open sea. Eventually, pieces of it calve off as icebergs and drift away. This has been happening throughout Halley's existence, and it will continue to happen regardless of what the city does.

The real Halley Stations were rebuilt six times as the ice moved and earlier versions were buried by snow accumulation or lost to calving events. Halley VI, the most recent real-world iteration, was built on ski-mounted modules specifically so it could be towed to a safer position when the ice dynamics required it. Tepenia's Halley inherited this design logic and extended it across centuries: the city is not a fixed place in the way other cities are fixed places. It is a city that has moved, is moving, and will move again.

The Weddell Sea is one of the most ice-choked bodies of water in the world — the pack ice that fills it in winter was responsible for trapping and eventually crushing Shackleton's *Endurance* in 1915. Maritime access to Halley is seasonal and always contingent on ice conditions. The city faces the sea on one side and the Antarctic continent on the other; Hwy 59 runs inland from Halley toward the South Pole.

#### Notable Weather Phenomena

- **Ice shelf dynamics:** the Brunt Ice Shelf is in constant slow motion; calving events — when large sections break off as icebergs — are part of the city's long-term reality; major calving events require portions of the city to be relocated before the ice beneath them separates from the shelf
- **Weddell Sea pack ice:** the sea freezes over in winter and breaks up in summer; in heavy pack ice years, maritime access is severely limited or impossible; the *Endurance* was crushed by this ice in 1915, and that possibility has always been present for any ship approaching Halley
- **Blizzards:** the open ice shelf position with no terrain shelter means blizzards at Halley are particularly severe; wind-driven snow with no obstacles to slow it
- **Ground blizzards:** even in calm overhead conditions, wind can strip loose surface snow and create low-level blizzards at ground height without being visible from above
- **Auroras:** the Weddell Sea sector produces excellent Aurora Australis displays during the polar night

#### Gameplay Notes

- **The moving city as environmental mechanic:** in DLC 5, the current position of Halley relative to its historical positions — and the visible evidence of older relocated sections — could be a navigational and exploratory element; the city's history is literally written on the ice in abandoned infrastructure
- **Ice calving as dynamic event:** a calving event during DLC 5 would be one of the most spectacular environmental events in any Tepenian DLC; part of the city breaking off and beginning to drift is an irreversible, large-scale change to the environment
- **Hwy 59 vulnerability:** the highway and Arcanet cable run together across ice terrain from Halley to the South Pole; disruption of this route is simultaneously a travel problem and a communications problem; it is the single most strategically critical infrastructure corridor in the Atlantic subnet
- **Maritime access windows:** the Weddell Sea's seasonal ice creates natural time-pressure mechanics for any maritime approach or departure from Halley
- **Hardcore Mode influences:**
  - Ice shelf structural awareness: certain areas of Halley may be flagged as structurally uncertain — older sections near a calving zone, areas where the ice beneath is thinning; a Hardcore Mode player ignoring these warnings risks the ground beneath them separating
  - Supply seasonality: maritime resupply is only possible during the open-water window; a player arriving in winter has to work with what's already there
  - Hwy 59 as the only land exit: if the highway is damaged or impassable, Halley is accessible only by sea; in Hardcore Mode, both routes being compromised simultaneously would create genuine supply isolation

## Founding

**Settled:** Post-Falkland Treaty. The British exiles arrived at Halley Station — whichever iteration was current at the time — and built on it. They inherited both the infrastructure and the problem: a station on moving ice requires constant management, and the founding generation had to decide immediately what their relationship with that movement would be.

**Founding population:** Primarily British exiles alongside a broader Atlantic-region community. The UK had maintained Halley Station for over a century before the exile era; the institutional knowledge of how to live on a floating ice shelf came with the founding population.

The founding generation made a decision that defined the city ever after: they would not fight the movement. They would design for it. Halley's architecture was built to be relocated — modular structures on ski-mounted platforms, designed to be towed across the ice surface when the ice dynamics required it. This was not a compromise; it was a philosophy. The city acknowledged that it lived on borrowed ground and built accordingly.

## Character & Culture

Halley is the city that knows it is temporary. Not in a morbid sense — the city has existed for centuries and fully intends to continue existing — but in the structural sense that every building in Halley is designed to be moved, that portions of the city have been moved within living memory, and that the question of when to move and where to move to is a recurring civic conversation rather than a crisis.

This produces a specific kind of culture: pragmatic about impermanence, attentive to the physical environment in ways that inland dome cities are not, and deeply invested in the collective decision-making processes that determine where the city goes next. Halley residents understand their city as an ongoing project rather than a fixed inheritance.

The relationship with the Weddell Sea is central. The sea is the city's most constant environmental presence — the source of the worst storms, the reason for seasonal maritime isolation, the body of water that determines when ships can reach them and when they cannot. Halley residents read ice conditions the way agricultural communities read weather. It is vocational knowledge, passed down and maintained.

**Nationally known for:** Being the city that moves. The engineering achievement of a city designed for relocation. The Weddell Sea coast. The particular cultural attentiveness to impermanence that its architecture produces.

- **The ski-mounted district modules** — the relocatable structural sections of Halley; each major building cluster sits on ski platforms capable of being towed by tracked vehicles; the visible ski infrastructure beneath city structures is one of Halley's most distinctive visual features

## Connection to Concordia

Halley is the geographically closest Atlantic subnet city to Concordia in terms of the Arcanet relay — its signals ran through Amundsen Station to reach the Mirny subnet. After the Split Brain, that connection is severed. Halley's Arcanet subnet now holds an isolated version of Atlantic history that Concordia cannot access without reaching the Amundsen Station archive.

Halley refugees in Concordia exist but are not numerous — the Atlantic coast is far from Concordia and the overland route is not straightforward. Those who made it would have traveled a significant distance.

## Current Status / Destruction

Halley was damaged during the Long Night War but remains partially operational. The damage interacted with the city's existing mobility in ways that other cities' damage did not — structural damage to ski-mounted modules may have made certain sections unmovable, leaving them in place even when ice dynamics would normally have required relocation. ## Legacy

Halley's legacy is its architecture — the proof that a city could be built on moving ice and survive. In Tepenian engineering history, Halley represents a design philosophy that no other city adopted because no other city faced the same conditions. The question of whether that philosophy was wise or merely necessary has been answered by the city's survival: it worked.

In broader Tepenian culture, Halley is known for the thing that sounds impossible and turned out to be achievable — a city that you move when the ground beneath it moves. That image carries weight in a civilization that has spent centuries building permanence in an environment that resists it.
