## Geographic Basis

Little America and Framheim occupy the same general area of the Ross Ice Shelf — the Bay of Whales region — and share all the same geographic conditions: floating ice, snow accumulation, calving risk, the barrier winds off the Transantarctic Mountains, and the question of whether the Bay of Whales still exists at all by the time of the game. The two cities are distinct settlements but proximate ones, and in many respects they function as a pair.

The historical Little America stations (I–V, 1929–1958) were not all at identical positions. The first four were clustered near the Bay of Whales; Little America V was repositioned somewhat inland on the shelf. By 2564, all five stations had been lost — buried or calved, same as Framheim's predecessor structures. The Tepenian city was reconstructed from Byrd's archives at a calculated position that best approximated the historical site, with the same ice-movement-conscious architecture as Framheim.

For full geographic and climate detail, see **Framheim.md** — the two cities are at effectively the same location on the Ross Ice Shelf and their physical environments are not meaningfully distinguishable.

## Founding

**Settled:** After Byrd was established and its underground archives were catalogued — same founding chain as Framheim. The historical records of Little America Stations I–V, preserved through the buried Byrd archive, gave the reconstruction expedition detailed information on the site: five separate construction cycles, each documenting what the previous iteration had failed to account for. The founders of Tepenian Little America had better engineering data than the founders of Tepenian Framheim, because Richard Byrd had built and lost the same city five times and written down what happened each time.

The reconstruction was built with ice movement accounted for from the start — the lesson of all five Little Americas, and of Framheim's predecessor structures, finally applied in the design rather than learned from failure.

**Founding population:** Primarily American exiles and Pacific-region communities, closely overlapping with Byrd's founding population. The emotional weight of the name "Little America" in the exile context is significant: the people who named this city were people who had been expelled from America (or whose forebears had been), and they named their Ross Ice Shelf settlement after a small version of the home they could not return to. Whether that name was chosen in longing, in irony, or in defiance — or some combination of all three — is an open question that the city's culture probably never fully resolved.

St. Richard — Richard E. Byrd — is the Saint most directly associated with Little America. Byrd's five stations bore this name; the Tepenian city bears it in his memory. Byrd city in the West Antarctic interior carries his name as a place-name; Little America carries the name of what he built.

## Character & Culture

Little America's character is shaped by the repetition embedded in its name. Five stations, each one built because the previous one was lost. The Tepenian city knows this history — it is the sixth attempt, the one that was supposed to learn from all five failures. That awareness gives Little America a specific kind of cultural personality: practical, iterative, attentive to what went wrong before, disinclined toward the grandeur that Framheim sometimes reaches for with its Amundsen mythology.

Where Framheim has the romance of the single legendary expedition — Amundsen's 1911 crossing — Little America has the unglamorous lesson of repetition. You build it. The ice takes it. You build it again, better. You are still on the ice. The city's relationship with its own impermanence is more matter-of-fact than existential.

The proximity to Framheim — both cities in the same area, both rebuilt from the same archive, both on the same shifting ice — creates a relationship between them that is close but distinct. Framheim has the older name and the Saint of the Pole; Little America has the American legacy and the institutional memory of five iterations. They are not the same city. They are neighbors who know exactly why the other is there.

**Nationally known for:** St. Richard's five stations. The iterative founding history — the city that learned from five failures before it was built. The name, and what it means to carry that name in exile. Being Framheim's neighbor and counterpart.

## Legacy

Little America's legacy is in its name and its number. It was the sixth attempt at something that failed five times. The fact that it existed at all — that someone built it knowing the history — is its own statement about the kind of civilization Tepenia was building. Not naive about what the ice does, not deterred by it either.

In Concordia, the name "Little America" carries the layered resonance it always has: a piece of somewhere else, named by people who could not go back to the original, rebuilt by people who knew the reconstruction was what it was. It is a smaller kind of inheritance than Framheim's mythology, and more honest about what it is.
