# Amundsen Station

**Based on:** Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station (USA), Geographic South Pole (90°00'S)
**Status:** Destroyed (Long Night War) — most significant ruins in Tepenia; DLC 1 setting
**Arcanet Subnet:** Inter-subnet relay — not a member of any subnet; served as the neutral switching point connecting all six subnets before its destruction
**Highway access:** Hwy 59 (Atlantic Throughway / Arcanet Line) terminates here from the north; other highway connections TBD
**Significance:** The geographic South Pole; former inter-subnet Arcanet relay; site of the Planetary Split Brain; site of the Amundsen Tower (space elevator); the only location where the six subnets' divergent post-Split-Brain histories can be reconciled; home to Kendra Heinrich

---

## Geographic Basis

The South Pole is the southernmost point on Earth — 90°00'S, where all lines of longitude converge and the concept of "direction" collapses into a single word: north. Everything from the South Pole is north. The pole sits on the East Antarctic Plateau at an elevation of approximately 2,835 meters above sea level, on ice approximately 2,850 meters thick. The ice itself moves — the geographic pole is fixed, but the station built on the ice drifts roughly ten meters per year, requiring periodic repositioning of the ceremonial pole marker.

The real Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station was named jointly for Roald Amundsen, who reached the pole first in December 1911, and Robert Falcon Scott, who reached it second in January 1912 and died on the return. In Tepenia, the station carries only Amundsen's name. Scott has his city. Amundsen has the Pole.

The South Pole's position — equidistant from all six subnet regions, at the terminus of the continent's interior, connected to everywhere by the Arcanet cable infrastructure and to nowhere easily by road — made it the natural neutral ground for the inter-subnet relay. It belonged to no subnet because it was too central to belong to any one. It served all of them equally, and when it fell, it failed them all equally.

#### Notable Weather Phenomena

- **Six-month polar night / six-month midnight sun:** at 90°S, the transition between darkness and light is binary — there is no gradual shortening or lengthening of days, only the long darkness and the long light, with brief transitional twilight periods at the equinoxes; the psychological and practical implications of this for anyone stationed here are extreme
- **The equinox sunrise:** when the sun returns after six months of darkness, it rises all the way around the horizon simultaneously — a 360° sunrise with no directional component, because at the South Pole every direction is north; this is one of the most extraordinary visual phenomena in all of Tepenia
- **Diamond dust and ice halos:** common in clear conditions; with no moisture source, precipitation almost never falls in the conventional sense; the ice crystals present in the air are simply there
- **Extreme isolation:** the South Pole is the most difficult location in Tepenia to reach; there are no nearby cities, no coastal access, no external reference points; in DLC 1, the journey is the challenge before the destination is encountered

#### Gameplay Notes

- **The equinox sunrise as a narrative event:** the 360° simultaneous sunrise after six months of darkness is one of the most visually distinctive moments achievable in any Antarctic setting; worth building a DLC 1 story beat around
- **Six-month polar night:** the DLC 1 setting during polar night means the ruins are encountered in complete darkness broken only by artificial light and aurora; during midnight sun, the same ruins feel entirely different — exposed, bright, no shadows to hide in
- **Every direction is north:** the directional collapse at the South Pole is a genuine navigational and psychological fact; compass-based navigation doesn't work at the magnetic pole (which is nearby but not identical to the geographic pole); the player's spatial orientation systems may behave unusually here
- **Hardcore Mode influences:**
  - The most extreme cold of any location in Tepenia; even Concordia's winters are mild by comparison; protection requirements here exceed anything in the main game
  - Total supply isolation: there is no resupply at the South Pole; whatever the player carries in is all they have; resource management reaches maximum pressure
  - The Kharkovchanka breakdown mechanic (established in DLC 1's design) occurs in this environment — repairing the vehicle in these conditions is the harshest version of that challenge

## Founding

**Settled:** Post-Falkland Treaty, as a neutral inter-subnet infrastructure installation rather than a conventional exile city. Amundsen Station was never primarily a population center — it was a facility, operated by a rotating technical staff maintaining the Arcanet relay hardware and the Amundsen Tower. The population was small relative to the station's strategic importance.

**Founding population:** Multi-subnet technical crews, with no dominant national or cultural community — the station's neutrality was structural, built into its staffing from the beginning. The people who worked at Amundsen Station came from across Tepenia on rotation, served their term, and returned to their home cities.

## Character & Culture

Amundsen Station had the culture of a facility, not a city. The rotating staff developed the specific camaraderie of people sharing extreme isolation together — intense, temporary, and real — but there was no permanent Amundsen community in the way that Scott had a community or Janbogo had a community. People came from their cities, did their work, and went home.

The station's political neutrality was its most significant cultural feature. In a Federation divided into six subnet communities with distinct identities and histories, Amundsen was the one place that belonged to all of them and therefore to none. Disputes that could not be resolved within a subnet were sometimes brought to Amundsen Station for mediation — not because it had formal authority over inter-subnet matters, but because its neutrality was respected. When the station fell, that neutral ground disappeared with it.

**St. Roald:** Roald Amundsen is venerated as a Saint in Tepenian civic culture — the first person to reach the South Pole, who survived where Scott did not. The fact that Amundsen's name is on the most strategically critical installation in all of Tepenia, while Scott's name is on a city, reflects a complex relationship between the two Saints in Tepenian memory. Neither is valued above the other. The pole belongs to Amundsen because he reached it first; the city on the peninsula belongs to Scott because that was where his legacy was most deeply felt. Both sacrificed. The Federation honors both.

**Nationally known for:** Being the South Pole. The Amundsen Tower. The relay. The place that connected everything — and, after its fall, the place whose absence disconnected everything.

## Economy & Industry

Not applicable in the conventional sense. Amundsen Station was a strategic infrastructure installation, not an economic city. Its "industry" was the Arcanet relay and whatever support operations the Amundsen Tower required. All supplies came in from elsewhere; nothing was produced for export.

## Connection to Concordia

Amundsen Station's connection to Concordia is structural and historical rather than social. The station relayed Arcanet signals between all six subnets, including the Mirny subnet that Concordia belongs to. Its destruction is the reason Concordia cannot communicate with Byrd, Halley, Mawson, Janbogo (beyond the physical hardware in Gemini), or Palmer. Every informational isolation that defines Concordia's current situation traces back to what happened at the South Pole.

The pre-Split-Brain archive at Amundsen Station is relevant to Concordia for reasons that the Planetary Split Brain questline will establish — the archive contains information that each subnet's isolated post-Split version of history has either lost, distorted, or never had access to.

Michelle Stanton provides the Kharkovchanka and teaches the player to repair it as the entry point for DLC 1. Her role is documented in `Storyline/DLC_Overview.md`.

## Current Status / Destruction

Amundsen Station was destroyed during the Long Night War. The Amundsen Tower — the space elevator whose base was at the station — was destroyed along with it, and the destruction of the tower is what caused the Planetary Split Brain: the cable infrastructure that carried the inter-subnet Arcanet signals ran through or alongside the tower system, and when the tower came down, the connections between subnets were severed permanently.

The ruins are enormous. The tower's collapse created a debris field of structural material, cable, and counterweight components that surrounds the station site — referred to as the "scrap mountain" in DLC 1 design notes. The station facility itself was destroyed, but not everything within it was obliterated; the pre-Split-Brain archive survived in a protected location within the ruins.

Kendra Heinrich is present. How long she has been there, and what condition she is in, is DLC 1 content.

---

## Legacy

Amundsen Station's legacy is the Planetary Split Brain — the fractured, contradictory, isolated informational reality that defines Tepenian life after its destruction. Every subnet now holds a different version of what happened and what is true. The archive at the ruins is the only instrument capable of reconciling those versions, and reaching it requires making the journey that DLC 1 is built around.

The station is also the clearest example of what Tepenia lost when the war reached the infrastructure that had held the Federation together. The coastal cities were communities. Amundsen Station was a system — and when you destroy a system that everything else depends on, the loss is not grief exactly but a different kind of damage: the slow downstream consequence of something critical going absent.
