## Geographic Basis

Cape Adare is a headland at the northern tip of the Adare Peninsula, where Victoria Land meets the Ross Sea. It is the most accessible point on the Ross Sea coast from the open Southern Ocean — the first landfall on the continental coast for ships approaching from the north. This accessibility is precisely why it matters historically: it was the most natural point of first arrival, the place ships could reach before committing to the deeper, more difficult waters of the Ross Sea.

The cape is low, rocky, and exposed. A large Adélie penguin rookery occupies the cape — one of the largest in the world, estimated at over 250,000 pairs in real-world records. The smell and noise of the rookery is the dominant sensory fact of Cape Adare for any visitor. The penguins were there before humans arrived and continued through everything that followed.

In 1899, Carsten Borchgrevink's Southern Cross expedition built a hut at Cape Adare and became the first humans to deliberately winter on the Antarctic continent. The hut still stands in the real world, preserved as a heritage site. In Tepenia, that hut — or its ruins — is the oldest human structure on the continent.

At 71°17'S, Cape Adare has a meaningful polar night and midnight sun, though not as extreme as the deep interior cities.

#### Notable Weather Phenomena

- **Open ocean exposure:** Cape Adare's northern position means it receives weather systems from the open Southern Ocean before they reach the rest of the Ross Sea coast; it is often the first point to be hit by incoming storms
- **Adélie penguin rookery:** one of the largest Adélie penguin colonies in Antarctica; the scale of the rookery — hundreds of thousands of birds — means it is an environmental constant; in the ruins, the penguins occupy whatever space they choose
- **Sea ice variability:** the northern location means sea ice comes and goes more dynamically here than at the southern Ross Sea cities; maritime access windows are longer but the transition periods are less predictable

#### Gameplay Notes

- **The oldest structure on the continent:** Borchgrevink's 1899 hut — or its ruins, or the ruins of later construction built around it — is the most historically significant physical object at Cape Adare; in DLC content, entering this space would carry specific historical weight
- **The penguin rookery as environment:** like Dumont d'Urville, Cape Adare's penguin colony continues in the ruins; but at the scale of Cape Adare's rookery (one of the world's largest), this is a dominant environmental feature rather than a background detail — the ruins are effectively inside the colony
- **First landfall on the continent:** Cape Adare's geographic significance as the most accessible point on the Ross Sea coast from open ocean gives it a specific identity as a threshold; it was where the continent began for approaching ships; in ruins, it remains the northernmost edge of the Janbogo subnet territory

## Founding

**Settled:** Post-Falkland Treaty, on the foundation of the existing heritage site infrastructure at Cape Adare. The settlement was always historically conscious — building at Cape Adare meant building at the place where Antarctica's human story began. That awareness shaped the founding community's relationship with the site from the start.

**Founding population:** Mixed, given Cape Adare's position as an entry point on the Ross Sea coast; no single dominant national community is established; TBD.

The city inherited the association with **St. Carsten** — Carsten Borchgrevink, the Norwegian-British explorer who led the Southern Cross expedition and chose Cape Adare as his wintering site in 1899. He was the first person to deliberately spend a winter on the Antarctic continent. That fact gives St. Carsten a specific kind of precedence in Tepenian civic memory: not just an explorer who came here, but the first person who stayed.

## Character & Culture

Cape Adare had the character of a city that knew it was first. Not the largest, not the most strategically important, not the wealthiest — but the oldest, or rather, the most directly descended from the oldest. Borchgrevink's hut was there, preserved, part of the city's daily landscape. The city grew around it and with it.

The penguin rookery was not incidental. A city built inside one of the largest Adélie penguin colonies in the world develops a specific relationship with that fact — not as tourists but as neighbors, as people who have learned to coexist with hundreds of thousands of birds that have no interest in coexisting with anyone. The smell, the noise, the constant presence were part of what Cape Adare was.

The northern position on the Ross Sea coast gave Cape Adare the feeling of a gateway — the city that ships reached first, the outpost where the rest of the continent began. This position created a specific kind of culture: outward-facing, accustomed to arrivals and departures, aware of the open ocean to the north in a way that the more sheltered southern Ross Sea cities were not.

**Nationally known for:** Being first. Borchgrevink's hut. The penguin rookery. The gateway character — the northernmost point where the Ross Sea coast was inhabited.

## Connection to Concordia

Cape Adare's connection to Concordia ran through the Janbogo subnet network — the Ross Sea coastal road south toward Janbogo and then Hwy 183 to Concordia. As the northernmost Ross Sea city, Cape Adare was at one end of the Janbogo subnet's coastal reach.

Cape Adare's historical significance — as the oldest settled site on the continent — gives it a specific kind of weight in Concordia's cultural memory even after its destruction. The city is gone; the fact of what it was is not.

## Current Status / Destruction

Destroyed in the Long Night War. The ruins sit at the northern tip of the Adare Peninsula. The penguin rookery continues undisturbed. Whether Borchgrevink's 1899 hut survived the destruction is one of the most emotionally significant open questions in Tepenian heritage — it is the oldest structure on the continent, and its fate in the ruins is TBD.

## Legacy

Cape Adare's legacy is its precedence. It was the first place. Borchgrevink came here and stayed through the winter of 1899, and everything that followed — the exploration, the science, the War of Upper Earth, the Falkland Treaty, Tepenia itself — followed from that first decision to remain. The city that grew here was the most direct inheritor of that original act.

In Tepenian culture, the destruction of Cape Adare is not simply the loss of a city. It is the loss of the place where Antarctica's human story began. Whether that story continues in Concordia and the surviving cities, or whether something was severed when Cape Adare fell, is a question Tepenian philosophers have not finished answering.
