## Geographic Basis

Fort McMurdo sits on the southern tip of Ross Island — a volcanic island in the Ross Sea, connected to the Antarctic mainland by seasonal sea ice across McMurdo Sound. Ross Island is defined by four volcanoes, the most significant of which is **Mount Erebus**: the southernmost active volcano on Earth, rising 3,794 meters above sea level, with a permanent lava lake in its summit crater. Erebus is visible from virtually everywhere in Fort McMurdo — a constant presence on the skyline, generating a visible plume of gas and ash in most weather conditions. It is one of the most dramatically situated urban backgrounds of any city in Tepenia.

Across McMurdo Sound, directly west of the city on the Antarctic mainland, lie the **McMurdo Dry Valleys** — the largest ice-free area in Antarctica. The Dry Valleys exist because the surrounding mountains block glacier flow and because strong katabatic winds evaporate any precipitation before it accumulates. The landscape there is exposed rock and sediment rather than ice, unlike virtually anywhere else on the continent. The Dry Valleys were scientifically significant before the exile era and presumably remained significant to Tepenian industry — whether for mining, material extraction, or other purposes is TBD.

The city itself occupies a small peninsula of volcanic rock at the south end of Ross Island. The real McMurdo Station was the largest in Antarctica — essentially a small town with hundreds of buildings, multiple piers, a harbor, vehicle depots, fuel storage, and full utility infrastructure. Fort McMurdo inherited and dramatically expanded that footprint. By pre-war peak, it was the largest city in Tepenia by physical scale.

Scott Base — New Zealand's Antarctic station, now the Tepenian city of **Scott** — sits approximately three kilometers from Fort McMurdo on the same peninsula. The two cities are functionally adjacent; the boundary between them is a political line more than a physical one.

#### Notable Weather Phenomena

- **Katabatic winds:** strong and persistent, channeled down from the polar plateau through McMurdo Sound; create dangerous wind-chill conditions even at relatively moderate ambient temperatures
- **Erebus weather effects:** Mount Erebus generates its own localized weather — ash falls during periods of heightened volcanic activity; volcanic gas plumes; occasional sulfur smell in the city during certain wind directions; the volcano is a constant atmospheric presence
- **Sea ice dynamics:** McMurdo Sound freezes solid in winter and breaks up in summer; the sea ice road across the Sound (to the Dry Valleys and mainland) is usable only during the solid-freeze period; summer breakup opens maritime access but closes the overland crossing
- **Polar night duration:** at ~118 days, Fort McMurdo's polar night is among the longest of any inhabited Tepenian city — roughly six weeks longer than Janbogo's; this significantly shapes the city's culture and industrial rhythms
- **Ice crystals and auroras:** as with other high-latitude cities, the polar night brings spectacular Aurora Australis displays; diamond dust and ice crystal phenomena common in clear cold conditions

#### Gameplay Notes

- **Erebus as environmental presence:** the volcano is not just scenery — ash fall events, volcanic tremors, gas plumes, and the perpetual summit glow at night create a dynamic environmental backdrop for the DLC 6 setting; the lava lake visible from the summit (accessible by Kharkovchanka to the flanks, by foot to the crater rim) is one of the most extraordinary locations in all of Tepenia
- **The Dry Valleys crossing:** the sea ice road across McMurdo Sound — usable only in winter during the solid-freeze period — creates a seasonal access mechanic; the Dry Valleys are only reachable overland in winter; in summer, only by sea
- **Polar night (~118 days):** the longest polar night of any major coastal city; industrial operations would have adapted around it; ruins exploration in DLC 6 during the polar night period offers a very different atmosphere from summer
- **Scale of ruins:** as the largest pre-war city, Fort McMurdo's ruins would be enormous — multiple km² of damaged and collapsed structures; significant portions may still be partially standing; the scale itself is a design asset
- **Hardcore Mode influences:**
  - The long polar night creates the most extended period of artificial-light dependence of any coastal city; power for lighting becomes a resource concern during the ~118-day darkness
  - Industrial ruins mean hazardous environments — structural instability, exposed machinery, chemical storage, fuel reserves; exploration in Hardcore Mode would carry environmental hazard risks beyond simple cold exposure
  - The Erebus factor: volcanic gas exposure (SO₂, CO₂) in certain areas of the ruins or on approaches to the volcano could create a unique environmental hazard not present in any other Tepenian city

## Founding

**Settled:** Shortly after the Falkland Treaty (June 21, 2564). McMurdo Station was the largest and best-equipped Antarctic station in the pre-exile world — the Americans had built it into a genuine small-town infrastructure with piers, fuel storage, vehicle depots, a harbor, and full utilities. The exile settlement inherited the most substantial ready-to-use foundation of any city in Tepenia.

**Founding population:** Primarily American exiles alongside a broad international community — McMurdo Station had always been a multinational working environment, and the exile city continued that pattern. The Americans founded and named it but never exclusively owned it.

The "Fort" prefix was added deliberately — a reference to Fort MacMurray in Alberta, Canada, which by the 2500s was synonymous with industrial resource extraction at scale. The founders were not being subtle. They knew what they were building: a working city, not a cultural capital. The name said so upfront.

## Character & Culture

Fort McMurdo was Tepenia's industrial backbone. Palmer City was where Tepenia celebrated itself; Fort McMurdo was where it worked. At peak, it was the largest city in the Federation by population and physical scale — a sprawling, functional, largely unbeautiful place that kept the lights on across the continent.

The culture was working-class, direct, and meritocratic in the way that physically demanding industries tend to produce: status earned by what you could do, not where you came from. Transient labor was a feature of the city from early on — mining and extraction operations attracted workers from across Tepenia on seasonal or contract rotations, and Fort McMurdo absorbed and processed enormous numbers of people moving through.

Mount Erebus shaped the culture in ways that are hard to quantify but easy to understand: you cannot live in the shadow of an active volcano for generations without it becoming part of how you think about the world. Erebus was never threatening in a day-to-day sense — it was simply always there, always smoking, occasionally rumbling. Forts McMurdans (demonym TBD) developed a particular relationship with geological scale — an easy familiarity with forces larger than human or robot agency — that outsiders sometimes found unsettling and residents found clarifying.

**Nationally known for:** Being the largest city. The industrial output that supplied materials to construction projects across Tepenia. Mount Erebus. The particular directness of its people.

## Notable Locations

- **Mount Erebus** — the active volcano dominating the Ross Island skyline; the summit crater holds a permanent lava lake; visible from Fort McMurdo at all times; one of the most extraordinary geological features in Tepenia; accessible to the flanks by Kharkovchanka, to the crater rim on foot
- **The McMurdo Sound sea ice road** — seasonal overland crossing to the Antarctic mainland and the Dry Valleys; usable only during the solid-freeze period; a highway that exists only in winter
- **The Dry Valleys (across the Sound)** — largest ice-free area in Antarctica; accessible via the ice road or by sea; significant for extraction operations; a landscape utterly unlike the rest of the continent
- **Cape Evans** — expedition hut of Robert Falcon Scott's Terra Nova expedition (1910); pre-exile historical site; a few kilometers from the city on Ross Island's coast; preserved in Antarctic cold; likely the most intact pre-exile structure in the Janbogo subnet
- **Cape Royds** — Ernest Shackleton's Nimrod expedition hut (1907); pre-exile historical site; further up the Ross Island coast; similarly preserved

## Connection to Concordia

Fort McMurdo sits near Hwy 183 — connected to the mainland coastal route via a spur road across McMurdo Sound rather than directly on the main highway — making it a significant stop but one that requires a deliberate detour rather than a pass-through. Before and during the Long Night War, Fort McMurdo's industrial output fed construction and infrastructure projects across Tepenia including Concordia itself — the physical city of Concordia, its dome structures, its corridor systems, its equipment, was built substantially from materials that passed through Fort McMurdo.

Fort McMurdo refugees in Concordia are present across multiple districts — the city was large enough to have produced a significant diaspora — but they did not form a concentrated enclave the way the Janbogo community formed in Gemini. Working-class industrial refugees tend to disperse into wherever the work is.

## Current Status / Destruction

Fort McMurdo was hit during the Long Night War and left damaged but not fully destroyed — its sheer physical scale made complete destruction difficult, and portions of the city's infrastructure remain at least structurally standing. "Partially operational" in Fort McMurdo's case likely means small survivor populations occupying the most intact sections of a massive ruin — a city-that-was, with pockets of life amid the wreckage.

The volcanic geology of Ross Island means the ruins have a different character from other damaged cities: basaltic rock, volcanic ash accumulation, and the ever-present influence of Erebus. Structures that survived the war have continued to face geological stressors.

---

## Legacy

Fort McMurdo is remembered in Concordia primarily through the things it built — the infrastructure, the equipment, the construction materials that made the dome system possible. It is not remembered with the same emotional weight as Palmer City because it was never a city of moments or stories in the same way. It was where things were made. That legacy is present but not easily narrated.

The expedition huts at Cape Evans and Cape Royds are the pre-exile heritage of the Ross Island area — Scott and Shackleton walked this same island. Fort McMurdo residents were aware of this. It was a point of local pride that Tepenia's industrial capital occupied the same geography as the Heroic Age of Antarctic exploration.
