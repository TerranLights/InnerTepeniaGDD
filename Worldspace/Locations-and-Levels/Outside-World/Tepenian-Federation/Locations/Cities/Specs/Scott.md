## Geographic Basis

Scott occupies the same Hut Point Peninsula as Fort McMurdo — approximately three kilometers away on the same volcanic rock promontory at the southern tip of Ross Island. The two cities are close enough that their outer edges nearly touch. Everything said about Fort McMurdo's geography applies here: Ross Island, McMurdo Sound, the Dry Valleys across the water, Mount Erebus visible from everywhere, the same sea ice dynamics, the same katabatic wind patterns, the same spur road connection to Hwy 183 on the mainland coast.

The difference is scale. The real Scott Base was a compact, tightly organized station of roughly a dozen primary structures — a deliberate design choice by New Zealand, which maintained a smaller but carefully considered Antarctic presence rather than McMurdo's sprawling city-scale infrastructure. Scott the Tepenian city was always the smaller of the two, and its character grew directly from that: intimate, organized, close-knit in ways that a city of Fort McMurdo's scale could not be.

The proximity to Fort McMurdo was both the defining fact of Scott's existence and the central tension of its identity. The two cities cooperated on nearly everything — shared logistics, shared access roads, shared emergency infrastructure — while maintaining distinct political and cultural identities. The line between them was a real line, observed by both sides, even when it was functionally arbitrary.

## Founding

**Settled:** Shortly after the Falkland Treaty (June 21, 2564). Scott Base had been New Zealand's Antarctic presence since 1957 — one of the older continuously occupied stations. The infrastructure was modest by McMurdo's standards but solid and well-maintained. New Zealand exiles built on it and kept it modest by choice.

**Founding population:** New Zealand exiles primarily, alongside a broader Pacific-region community. New Zealand's Antarctic program had always emphasized quality of relationship with the environment over scale of operation, and the founding population carried that sensibility into the city's design.

The name was kept as-is from the station. Robert Falcon Scott — British explorer, died on the return journey from the South Pole in 1912 — had been the organizing spirit of New Zealand's Antarctic identity for centuries by the time of the exile. Keeping his name was not a statement of British loyalty (New Zealand had been independent for centuries) but of Antarctic heritage.

## Character & Culture

Scott was the city that Fort McMurdo was not. Where Fort McMurdo was large, industrial, and transient, Scott was small, precise, and stable. Where Fort McMurdo's culture was shaped by the constant churn of contract workers and extraction operations, Scott's was shaped by long-term residents who knew each other's names and chose to stay.

The New Zealand cultural influence gave Scott a particular quality — pragmatic without being harsh, community-oriented without being insular, comfortable with the outdoors in a way that read differently from the Frostlander outdoor culture in Concordia. New Zealand's national self-image had long been built around competence in difficult landscapes; that translated well to Antarctica.

The relationship with Fort McMurdo was genuinely collaborative and genuinely competitive in equal measure. Scott residents were proud of their city's distinct character and somewhat allergic to being perceived as Fort McMurdo's smaller sibling. Fort McMurdo residents tended to regard Scott with a mixture of respect and mild condescension that Scott residents found both understandable and irritating. Both cities needed each other too much for this to become serious conflict.

**St. Robert:** Scott's most significant cultural distinction from any other city in Tepenia is its relationship with its namesake. Robert Falcon Scott is venerated in Tepenian culture as a **Saint** — the honorific given to pre-War of Upper Earth figures who were significant in the exploration and development of Antarctica, and whose sacrifices are understood as having made the exile civilization possible. Within the city of Scott specifically, he is known as **St. Robert**: *"who gave his life so that we could have a city to call home."*

The Tepenian interpretation of Scott's death is distinct from how Upper Earth history records it. In the outside world, Scott died on a failed expedition — a tragedy of exploration. In Tepenia, the pre-exile explorers are understood as people who mapped, endured, and documented Antarctica at tremendous personal cost — not knowing they were preparing a home for a people who did not yet exist. Their sacrifice was unknowing, which in Tepenian civic theology makes it purer, not lesser. They gave everything for a future they could not have imagined. The exiles inherited that gift.

Scott the city carries this framing more personally than any other place in Tepenia. The city is named for him. His hut is within its limits. He is not an abstract historical figure here — he is the reason the city exists at all.

*(Note: the Saints framework is a pan-Tepenian cultural concept applicable across the Federation. Other Saints include figures associated with their respective cities — St. Ernest (Shackleton), St. Roald (Amundsen), St. Douglas (Mawson), St. Richard (Byrd), and others. A dedicated entry on Tepenian civic sainthood should be created in the culture documentation — see TODO.)*

**Nationally known for:** Being Fort McMurdo's partner city; the name it carried and the Saint it honored; the Hut Point remembrance tradition; the particular quality of its community and organization.

## Economy & Industry

Scott's economy was deeply intertwined with Fort McMurdo's — the two cities shared logistics infrastructure and Scott's economy was partly organized around supporting, complementing, and in some cases specializing against Fort McMurdo's industrial operations. Where Fort McMurdo extracted and processed at scale, Scott may have focused on more precise or technical operations. Full economic detail TBD for DLC 6 design.

New Zealand's real-world Antarctic program had a strong scientific and environmental monitoring component; that may have translated into a research or measurement industry in Scott distinct from Fort McMurdo's extraction focus.

## Notable Locations

- **The Scott–Fort McMurdo border** — a formally maintained but practically porous boundary between the two cities; a line on maps that both sides acknowledged and occasionally made pointed reference to
- **Hut Point** — the historic site on the peninsula where Robert Falcon Scott's expedition parties cached supplies in 1902 and used as a refuge; a pre-exile historical site within the city's footprint; the discovery hut is one of the oldest surviving human-made structures in Antarctica, preserved by the same cold that defines Tepenian life. Hut Point is Scott's most sacred civic location — not a museum, but a living site of remembrance. On days of national and cultural importance, most prominently **Tepenian Independence Day** (June 21), residents of both Scott and Fort McMurdo come to Hut Point and lay items of remembrance at the site: candles, bouquets of flowers, personal tokens. The tradition is not mandated; it simply happens, year after year, because people feel it should. Both cities participate — the Saint belongs to the whole peninsula, not only to the city that bears his name.

## Connection to Concordia

Scott refugees in Concordia are present but not concentrated — the city was small enough that the diaspora dispersed into whatever communities fit them best rather than forming a dedicated enclave. Their reputation in Concordia, where known, tends to be shaped by the Fort McMurdo association: people from "Ross Island" rather than people specifically from Scott.

Scott's contribution to Concordia's physical infrastructure parallels Fort McMurdo's — technical expertise and precision work rather than raw industrial output.

## Current Status / Destruction

Scott was damaged during the Long Night War and remains partially operational, like Fort McMurdo. Given the two cities' physical proximity and shared infrastructure, their war experience was likely intertwined — what hit one affected the other. Whether Scott's surviving sections and Fort McMurdo's surviving sections are in contact with each other in the present day, and what that relationship looks like, is a DLC 6 question.

## Legacy

Scott's legacy in Tepenian memory is anchored to its Saint. The city is the most personally connected place in all of Tepenia to the pre-exile explorer culture that the Saints framework honors — the hut is here, the name is here, and the remembrance tradition belongs to this peninsula.

Upper Earth tells Robert Scott's story as tragedy: the man who lost the race to the Pole and died on the way home. Tepenia tells it differently. He did not die failing — he died exploring a place that would become home to a people who had no other home. That reframing is not something Tepenians invented as consolation. It is, from their perspective, simply accurate. The explorers gave what they gave. The exiles received it. The debt is real, and Hut Point is where it is honored.

The city of Scott, even in ruins, carries that weight. It is not the largest city, or the most strategically important, or the most culturally vivid. But it is the city that knows whose name it bears and why, and that knowledge gives it a kind of gravity no other city in Tepenia quite has.
