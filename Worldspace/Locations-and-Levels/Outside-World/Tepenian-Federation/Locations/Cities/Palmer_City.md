# Palmer City

**Status:** Destroyed (Long Night War) — off-map; referenced in lore throughout Concordia
**Based on:** Palmer Station, Anvers Island, Antarctic Peninsula (64°46'S, 64°03'W)
**Significance:** First settled location in the Federation of Tepenia; cultural and entertainment capital of pre-war Tepenia

---

## Geographic Basis

Palmer Station sits on Anvers Island off the Antarctic Peninsula — the northernmost finger of the continent, reaching toward South America. Of all the Antarctic stations, Palmer is the most temperate and the most accessible from the outside world. Temperatures rarely drop below −20°C in winter and occasionally rise above freezing in summer. The surrounding sea is navigable for longer periods than most Antarctic waters.

This accessibility is precisely why Palmer became the first point of arrival. When the Falkland Treaty exiles departed Upper Earth for Antarctica in 2564, ships put them ashore here first. They found an abandoned research station and made it home.

---

## Founding

**Date:** June 21, 2564 — the same day the Falkland Treaty was signed. The exiles did not wait.

The first residents of what would become Palmer City took up residence in the old pre-war station buildings immediately on arrival. The infrastructure that would eventually become a city was built by the exiles themselves, from scratch, using what they found and what they brought. In those first days it was a camp. The transition to city happened gradually, then all at once.

**The incoming population** consisted of three groups arriving together:
- Robots exiled under the Falkland Treaty's blanket provision — by virtue of being robots, not for any individual act
- Humans in committed, long-term monogamous relationships with specific robots — partners who chose exile alongside the people they loved rather than remain in Upper Earth without them
- Human supporters who sided with robots on ideological grounds — people who believed the exile was unjust and refused to accept it as a condition of their remaining in Upper Earth

These three groups arrived together and built together. The distinction between "romantic exile" and "ideological exile" was real but not socially significant in Palmer City's early years. Everyone was there because they chose to be, which was different from most of the world.

## The Founding Artifacts

Among the objects recovered from the old pre-war Palmer Station buildings, two became foundational to Palmer City's identity and to Tepenian cultural memory more broadly.

### The Antarctica Flag

A torn and tattered fabric flag of Antarctica — the real pre-war flag, a white silhouette of the continent on a light blue field. It had been in the station before the exiles arrived. No one brought it. It was simply there.

The exiles kept it. The symbolic weight was not lost on anyone: they had been sent to Antarctica, and Antarctica had already named itself and put up a flag. The land was waiting. The tattered condition of the flag — worn, imperfect, survived — became part of its meaning rather than a reason to replace it.

The Antarctica flag is distinct from the Federation of Tepenia's national flag (the circuit diamond design, developed later as a deliberate national identity). The found flag predates Tepenia's existence as a country. It is a historical artifact — displayed at 100 Miles Davis Blvd. and later in cultural institutions across Tepenia.

### The Jazz Records

Also recovered from the station buildings: a collection of Jazz records spanning the classic era — pre-War of Upper Earth and pre-2083 global war. Artists from the full arc of Jazz history, preserved in physical format through everything that followed.

These records became Palmer City's soundtrack. Jazz — a music born from a marginalized community creating something transcendent from difficult circumstances — resonated with an exile population building a country from abandoned buildings on the southernmost accessible point of the world. The music was old. The situation it fit was new. The connection was immediate.

---

## Miles Davis Boulevard

The first main street of Palmer City was an ice path — the equivalent of a dirt road, packed and worn by foot traffic between the station buildings. It was named **Miles Davis Boulevard** after the discovery of the Jazz records.

At one end of Miles Davis Boulevard stood a small building repurposed as Palmer City's first customs office — a makeshift reception point for incoming robots and their partners and supporters. The building was given the address:

**100 Miles Davis Boulevard, Palmer City**

This was the first official address in the Federation of Tepenia.

The customs office was not a formal bureaucratic institution. It had no official legal standing in those earliest days because Tepenia was not yet a fully functioning state. It was simply a place where people arrived and were received — where robot-human pairs came through the door together and were acknowledged as having arrived. Its informality was part of what it was.

The address 100 Miles Davis Boulevard eventually became the most historically significant address in Tepenia — the first place the nation formally existed in physical space, the first threshold the exiles crossed into a country that was their own.

## Growth and Character

Palmer City grew from an ice-path settlement into the cultural and entertainment capital of the Tepenian Federation. The jazz records were the seed. The city that grew from them became something that combined the spectacle and excess of Las Vegas, the boardwalk energy of Atlantic City, the cultural depth and musical soul of New Orleans, and the grand ambition of St. Petersburg.

It was not the governmental capital of Tepenia — that distinction belonged to another city (TBD). Palmer City was something different: the place where Tepenia celebrated itself.

**Why neutral ground:** Palmer City's status as the first settlement, the arrival point, the place everyone passed through, gave it a character that no later-founded city could replicate. No faction had claim to it because every faction's predecessors had walked its ice paths first. Neutrality was not a policy — it was an inheritance from being the beginning.

**What it looked like at peak:** A coastal city on the Antarctic Peninsula, built outward from the original station buildings in layers of decades. Closer to sea level than the high-altitude inland cities. More temperate. More accessible. Architecturally layered — old station buildings at the core, surrounded by everything the exiles built, surrounded by everything the next generation built. Entertainment districts. Cultural venues. Music. The kind of city where things happened that you could not have predicted.

---

## Destruction

Palmer City was destroyed during the Long Night War (early 2700s). Its location — coastal, on the Antarctic Peninsula, the most accessible part of Antarctica from Upper Earth — made it among the first and most thoroughly targeted of the Tepenian cities when Upper Earth's militaries attacked. The same accessibility that made it the first settlement made it an easy target.

The city that was built first was among the first to be destroyed.

---

## Legacy in Concordia

Palmer City refugees in Concordia are relatively rare — the Antarctic Peninsula is as geographically distant from Concordia as any point in Antarctica. Those who made the journey are among Concordia's most traveled and, in some respects, most bereaved residents.

What survived of Palmer City's culture arrived in Concordia through people, not infrastructure. Jazz as a musical tradition. The story of 100 Miles Davis Boulevard. Knowledge of the Antarctica flag and where it was found. The founding image of robots and their human partners arriving together and being received at a door at the end of an ice path.

The Bonded Lattice faction's philosophy — that robot-human pair bonds deserve structural recognition and support — traces its deepest roots to Palmer City's founding population. The first humans in Tepenia were largely there because of specific robots they loved. The Bonded Lattice is, in a sense, the institutional continuation of something that began at 100 Miles Davis Boulevard.
