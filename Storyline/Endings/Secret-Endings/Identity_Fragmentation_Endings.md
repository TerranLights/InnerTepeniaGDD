# Identity Fragmentation Endings

## Trigger Condition

These endings are available when the player's **Identity Fragmentation meter** reaches specific thresholds. The Identity Fragmentation meter is a world-state float that increases each time the player re-specs their character — redistributing MACHINE stats, reassigning skills, or rebuilding their build in ways that represent abandoning one version of themselves for another.

High fragmentation does not mean the player played poorly. It means the player played as many different people, and committed to none of them long enough to carry one version of themselves to completion. The meter measures instability of self, not failure.

**Thresholds:**
- **75–89:** The Unmoored
- **90–99:** The Ghost in the Machine
- **100:** The Hollow

A player who qualifies for a higher threshold does not also access lower-threshold endings. The game routes to the highest qualifying ending.

---

## Design Philosophy

Every other secret ending category is about what the player did to the world — who they became in relation to it. Identity Fragmentation endings are about what the player did to themselves. They are the only ending category in which the player's specific mechanical history (their actual re-spec decisions) is the subject of the ending itself.

These are not punishment endings. They are what it looks like when you never stayed long enough to find out who you were.

## IF-1. The Unmoored
*Fragmentation 75–89*

The player has been multiple people. The city's administrative record shows it. The player's document history — faction affiliations, skill certifications, civic associations — contains entries that contradict each other across different periods. Not because of deception. Because the player genuinely was different things at different times and never consolidated into one.

**World Trigger**
A Libra administrative document — a civic identity record, a resource allocation history, a reputation summary — that the player encounters during normal late-game activity. It lists the player's complete recorded history across every district and faction. At 75+ fragmentation, this document is long enough and internally inconsistent enough to be visibly strange. It reads like multiple different people sharing a name. One section lists skills and affiliations from an early build. Another lists a completely different skill configuration from a later one. Neither is false. Both are the player. That specific document, in that specific location, is the trigger. The player reads it and recognizes themselves in all of it and none of it.

**The End**
The player identifies one specific action that a past version of themselves — a build they abandoned — was specifically capable of and that their current build is not. They reach back to that past self: not by re-speccing, but by doing the thing that self would have done, using whatever residual understanding they carry from having been that person. The action is available because the player was that person once. The completion of that action — performed with a past self's logic by a present self's hands — is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The action was correct for who the player was, not who they are. The player used a past self's understanding to make a past self's decision — but the consequences of that decision fall on the current player, who is not the person who made it. The city received the action as coherent. It was not. The gap between the decision and the person living with its results is real, and it persists.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The Libra document that triggered the ending is now flagged for administrative review — an identity record this internally inconsistent is an anomaly that Libra's systems are not designed to process without investigation
- The community or faction that benefited from the player's action knows the player from their past-self period — the relationship they're resuming is with someone who no longer fully exists, and the mismatch will eventually show
- The player's current build is not optimized for the choice they made — the action that felt right was also the action they're least equipped to follow through on cleanly
- The player's fragmented administrative record is now a specific political vulnerability: multiple factions can point to different periods of the player's history to claim the player was working with them, and not all of those claims are false

## IF-2. The Ghost in the Machine
*Fragmentation 90–99*

The player has been so many people that the city's systems can no longer synthesize them. Not because the data is missing — all of it is there — but because the accumulated data is irreconcilable. The player is present in the city's records as a dozen different configurations of a person, none of which are currently operative. They are less a citizen than a haunting.

**World Trigger**
A Hub administrative terminal — one of the civic identity display points used to verify a citizen's current standing and permissions — attempts to display the player's identity profile. At 90+ fragmentation, the terminal cannot produce a stable output. Each connected data source asserts something different about the player: one lists them as a combat specialist, one as a diplomatic operator, one as an infrastructure technician. The terminal cycles through these outputs without settling. It is not broken. It is accurately reflecting that the player has no stable civic identity that the system can display. The player is looking at a machine trying to tell them who they are and producing every answer simultaneously. That terminal, in that location, is the trigger.

**The End**
The player identifies an action that none of their previous builds could have taken alone — something that requires being capable of many things simultaneously rather than deeply capable of one thing. The fragmentation that destroyed stable identity makes them, in one specific moment, the only person in the city who can do what needs to be done. They do it. The single exceptional act performed by a person who has been everyone is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The advantage that came from being everything is not repeatable and not transferable. The player used their fragmented state to accomplish something once that no specialized person could accomplish. The same state that made this possible also makes sustained, coherent action nearly impossible. What the city received was real. The person who provided it cannot provide it again in the same way, and the city has no framework for understanding why.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The Hub terminal's display of the player's cascading contradictory identity profile is publicly visible during the ending sequence — anyone present in the terminal at the time saw the cascade, and what they saw is difficult to explain and impossible to officially suppress
- The action the player performed required simultaneously accessing capabilities from multiple abandoned build states — the performance of it left the player more fragmented, not less; the city is slightly safer, and the player is further from coherence
- Factions that had the player affiliated with them during different periods are now actively disputing ownership of the player's legacy in the aftermath — the fragmented record creates multiple competing claims on what the player did and why
- The player's current state is the product of everything they abandoned, which makes them capable in unexpected ways and unreliable in predictable ones; the city has acquired a specific kind of dependency on someone who cannot be counted on to behave consistently

## IF-3. The Hollow
*Fragmentation 100*

The player has been every possible configuration of themselves and committed to none of them long enough to become one. At 100 fragmentation, the player is not an unstable person. They are not a confused person. They are not a person in the process of becoming. They have exhausted the process. What is left is not nothing — it is the specific shape of what remains after you have been everyone and kept nothing.

**World Trigger**
Scorpio's communal archive maintains, among its records of the city's psychological landscape, a file under "unidentified individuals" — people Scorpio's community observers have noted and documented but could not assign a stable identity to. At 100 fragmentation, this file contains an entry that is unmistakably the player. The right physical presence, the right districts visited, the right time period. But Scorpio's careful observational language cannot assign the subject an identity category, because every observation contradicted the previous one. The entry concludes: *"Subject appears to be cycling through identity states rather than occupying any. Recommend: observation, not intervention."*

The player finds this record. It is a description of themselves written by a system that tried and could not find who they were. That record, in Scorpio's archive, is the trigger.

**The End**
The player finds the one action in the game that is available specifically because they are no one. Not because they have low reputation. Not because they lack access. Because the action requires the absence of consistent prior behavior — something only a person the city cannot categorize can do, because every other person in the city has a record that disqualifies them. The specific action varies depending on what the player's specific combination of abandoned builds left behind, but every version of it is something only a hollow person could do. The completion of that action is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The action the player took required them to be no one, and in performing it, they confirmed that's what they are. The city received something real from the Hollow's action. The player did not — or rather, there is no continuous self that receives or values or experiences what was gained. The game is over. What the Hollow accomplished, they accomplished. What it meant to them is not a question the ending answers, because the ending does not have a them to answer it for.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Scorpio's "observation, not intervention" note is now outdated — the subject acted, which requires a new assessment, but Scorpio's assessors cannot agree on how to categorize what they witnessed or who performed it, because the actor does not fit any existing category in their framework
- The city that benefited from the player's action has no vocabulary for acknowledging it — how do you honor someone who is no one? The silence that follows is not ingratitude, it is a failure of institutional language
- The other individuals in Scorpio's "unidentified" file — people tracked for similar reasons — are now more visible to the city as a category, because the Hollow's action demonstrated what a person without stable identity can do; some of them did not want to be visible, and some of what they can do is not benign
- The player's re-spec history is now fully legible in Concordia's administrative record as a complete story — a person who kept becoming someone else and never arrived — and that story is available to anyone who looks for it; it is not a flattering story, and it is permanent

## Implementation Notes

- The Identity Fragmentation meter should be tracked as a world-state float, incremented each time the player re-specs any significant build element (MACHINE stat redistribution, major skill reallocation, perk reversal).
- Minor adjustments within the same build philosophy should not increment the meter. The meter is a measure of how many times the player fundamentally changed who their character was, not how often they fine-tuned.
- **The 75-threshold events** — at 75+ fragmentation, ambient NPCs and specific dialogue options should begin to reflect the player's fragmented record. Factions may reference earlier builds. Administrative NPCs may express confusion. This escalates through the 90 and 100 thresholds.
- **The Hollow (IF-3)** should feel like the only ending in the game that is about the absence of the player rather than the presence of them. It is complete and meaningful, but its meaning is inverted.
- As with all secret endings: these are not failure states. A player who reaches 100 fragmentation and finds IF-3 has played a very specific way and the ending should feel proportional to that specific kind of play.
