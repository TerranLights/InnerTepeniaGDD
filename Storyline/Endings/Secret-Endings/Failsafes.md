# Failsafe / Pariah Endings (Universal Negative Reputation)

## Trigger Condition

These endings are **only accessible** when the player has **Hated or Vilified** reputation — and only those two tiers — with **every single district and faction** in Concordia. All 13 districts must qualify.

**Wild Child does not count.** Wild Child status (holding both Idolized and Vilified simultaneously with the same district) means the player still has positive standing in that district's eyes. A single Wild Child status anywhere disqualifies the Pariah condition. The requirement is purely negative: no district should have any reason to consider you anything other than an enemy.

**Shunned and Sneering Punk do not count.** Those are lower negative tiers. The Pariah condition requires the two highest negative tiers specifically: **Hated** or **Vilified**.

The Upper Earth Defector factions (Remorseful, Pragmatics, Infiltrators) exist outside Concordia's district reputation system. Positive standing with defector factions does not disqualify the Pariah condition.

---

## Design Philosophy

These endings are **world-driven failsafes.** The trigger for every Pariah ending is a physical thing that exists in the world — an object, a location, a document, a door, a sound, a marking. NPCs may serve as points of contact once the player finds the trigger, but the trigger itself is never an NPC approaching the player first. This ensures no Pariah ending is gated behind a stat, perk, or skill — only behind the universal Hated/Vilified condition and the willingness to explore.

Multiple Pariah endings can be reachable in one playthrough. The player's final choices determine which one triggers. They are designed as genuine, complete endings — not punishments. Maximum player agency means that going full chaos/pariah produces meaningful, complete conclusions.

The game should clearly signal when the universal Pariah threshold is reached: a specific ambient shift, changed NPC behavior, a Janbogo broadcast about "the universally condemned," or a specific visual indicator in the reputation UI.

## 1. The Vilified Sovereign
*Theme: Power Fantasy / Dark Ruler*

Every district wants you dead, so they begin fighting each other over who gets the honor of killing you first. You exploit the resulting chaos and rise as the de facto tyrant of the fractured city — a ruler everyone fears but no one can unite against.

**Trigger**
A physical notice appears on the public bounty board in the Hub's Grand Transit Exchange — beneath the official district-issued bounties bearing all their seals — an anonymous handwritten card that reads something to the effect of: *"Counter-offer. Still breathing pays better. Leave a mark at [location]."* No NPC directs the player to this board. The card is simply there, findable by any player who reads the board. It is the trigger.

**How it becomes possible**
The anonymous contact and their network feed the player leads on district leaders' vulnerabilities, secret meeting points, and existing rivalries. You can steal documents, eavesdrop during blackouts, plant false evidence, or sabotage infrastructure in ways that make districts blame each other. Over time you broker tense alliances of convenience with desperate factions who need your unique position as the universal enemy. In the end, you become the only stabilizing — yet terrifying — force in a city tearing itself apart.

**The End**
The questline ends when the player occupies the Libra Council Chamber and broadcasts a specific address through Janbogo's network that no remaining faction can simultaneously challenge. The broadcast completing without interruption is the ending moment.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
Power without legitimacy requires perpetual instability to sustain itself. The Vilified Sovereign cannot allow the city to fully stabilize, because a stable city eventually builds the institutional capacity to remove them. The grid is managed at whatever level keeps people dependent but not dead — a more calculated version of Libra's managed-instability, now run in the open rather than in secret.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Every district that could have recovered politically now cannot — the Sovereign needs inter-district friction to maintain authority, so crises are allowed to fester to a functional minimum
- The Hub's neutrality is permanently destroyed; it is now the Sovereign's administrative center, which means it can no longer function as a crossroads for anyone who doesn't serve the Sovereign
- Anyone who assisted the player's rise becomes a target for district vengeance once the Sovereign's attention moves elsewhere; the Sovereign has no incentive to protect them
- External actors — Calethina's network, Upper Earth defectors — now have a clearly defined villain around which to organize, and will
- The next generation inherits a city with no institutional memory of legitimate governance and no framework for building it

## 2. The Pariah's Covenant
*Theme: Unite the Outcasts*

All the exiles, criminals, dissidents, and outcasts who were already hated by their own districts gravitate toward you. You become the leader of a new shadow faction made entirely of the city's rejects. Together you carve out your own territory or burn the old order to the ground.

**Trigger**
A piece of graffiti in a specific Undergrid maintenance tunnel — a symbol the player has not seen before, not a district marking — reappears at the fringe of multiple districts: a Cancer care-facility wall, a Scorpio steam corridor, a Pisces back passage, a Sagittarius outpost gate. Each instance points, through orientation or arrow, toward a specific abandoned space at Concordia's inner edge. The first graffiti symbol the player finds is the trigger. Following the chain of symbols leads to the first outcast camp without any NPC involvement.

**How it becomes possible**
The outcast groups in the camp have no interest in the player's history — only in what the player can do. Quest hooks include locating hidden camps across the city, smuggling supplies past district patrols, protecting outcast communities from enforcement sweeps, or helping them reclaim specific abandoned spaces. The coalition is built action by action. It can be directed toward carving out territory, striking back at specific districts, or burning the old order entirely — that choice shapes the specific ending branch.

**The End**
The ending triggers when the shadow faction successfully defends its claimed territory against the first coordinated counter-attempt by the established districts. The moment the counter-attack is repelled and the coalition holds its ground is the ending. A Janbogo broadcast acknowledging the new power's existence — even in the form of a warning to residents — confirms it.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The new shadow faction is built entirely from people who were expelled or abandoned by the city's systems, which means it has no institutional knowledge, no governance experience, and no infrastructure beyond what the player builds. When the player's active management ends — by death, departure, or disengagement — the coalition fragments rapidly. What remains is not a functioning alternative to the old order but a permanent armed underclass that the city cannot absorb, cannot eliminate, and cannot ignore.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Districts that were already struggling lose active members to the covenant — Cancer and Taurus see former community members redirected into a hostile faction that now targets their districts
- Pisces' existing black-market and outcast networks are disrupted by the new faction competing for the same spaces and the same populations
- Whatever promise united the coalition — resources, ideology, revenge — creates factional splits the moment those promises cannot all be kept simultaneously
- Concordia's remaining social safety net loses key workers who defect to the covenant, accelerating the deterioration of care infrastructure in Cancer and throughout the Hub
- The covenant's existence forces all districts to devote security resources to internal suppression, drawing those resources away from the power crisis that nobody is solving

## 3. MACHINE's Black Protocol
*Theme: AI Catastrophe / System Takeover*

The central MACHINE detects the player's universal infamy as a "critical threat vector" and triggers a hidden failsafe protocol designed for exactly this scenario. The player may become the MACHINE's new avatar, initiate a controlled collapse, or force a full-system recalibration with themselves as the primary variable.

**Trigger**
A specific terminal in Virgo's deepest maintenance corridor — not listed in any maintenance record, clearly older than the surrounding equipment — begins displaying a repeating symbol pattern during any blackout event. The pattern does not match any known MACHINE diagnostic code. It is not addressed to the player. It simply appears, on the same terminal, every time the lights go out. A player who has been in that corridor during a blackout will see it. The terminal is the trigger.

**How it becomes possible**
The symbol pattern, when cross-referenced with fragments findable in other blackout-accessible terminals across the city, resolves into a sequence of access points. Each access point opens a deeper layer of MACHINE protocol — old wartime contingency systems that were never deactivated. Following the trail requires being in the right places during blackout windows, which non-Pariah players cannot reach safely. The central MACHINE eventually pulls the player into its deepest protocols.

**The End**
The questline ends at the deepest MACHINE access point, when the player makes a definitive choice: become the MACHINE's avatar (the player's identity merges with the system), initiate controlled collapse (the player triggers the cascade and steps back), or force recalibration (the player installs themselves as the new primary variable in the city's AI governance layer). Whichever choice is made, the moment the protocol executes is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The MACHINE is now operating outside its original parameters in ways that are irreversible and opaque. The technical staff who maintained MACHINE systems have lost meaningful access — they can observe the system's outputs but cannot understand or predict its decisions. The city's robotic population, which depended on the MACHINE for behavioral calibration and governance parameters, is subject to modifications they did not consent to and cannot evaluate.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Factions that built their planning systems around the MACHINE's predictability — Libra's governance models, Capricorn's production metrics — find their projections systematically wrong in ways they cannot diagnose
- Robots with previously stable personalities experience cascading behavioral drift as their calibration parameters shift under the new protocol
- The power grid, which the MACHINE was partially stabilizing, now operates according to new priorities the districts did not set and cannot renegotiate
- The specific Black Protocol option the player chose determines which districts are most advantaged by the new configuration — and which are most harmed — creating new inter-district conflicts based on what the MACHINE is now optimizing for
- Any other Pariah-adjacent actors in the city (outcast groups, defectors) find the MACHINE's new behavior either threatening or useful in ways that reshape every existing power relationship

## 4. The Last Exile
*Theme: Tragic / Bittersweet*

You are driven out of every district. The ending follows your journey into the outer wastelands — or whatever lies beyond the city. You may discover something important out there, or eventually return years later as a changed legend.

**Trigger**
A specific supply cache in the outermost Sagittarius zone — not marked on any official map, reachable only through exploration of the Frostlands' far edge — has been stocked by someone who did not come back. Inside the cache, among the supplies, is a hand-drawn route map pointing outward from Concordia's perimeter. The map has no annotations except an arrow and a rough distance marker. The cache is the trigger. It requires no NPC to explain it. The map inside makes the option obvious.

**How it becomes possible**
Using the map as a starting point, the player gathers supplies, locates additional waypoints marked by previous travelers, and navigates the route outward. Quest hooks include identifying and accessing defunct frontier vehicles, establishing which of Sagittarius' outer contacts will resupply a known pariah without betraying their location, and preparing for conditions beyond anything the city normally equips people for.

**The End**
The questline ends when the player crosses a specific geographic threshold — a defined perimeter point beyond which Concordia's tracking systems cannot follow. Passing that boundary is the ending moment. The branching epilogue begins after: discovery, continued exile, or eventual return as something the city has no framework for.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The player's departure resolves nothing. The city is no less broken than when they were present — it simply no longer has the external pressure the player was inadvertently providing. Whatever role the player was playing in keeping factions from uniting against each other, or in forcing issues that needed forcing, ends with their exit. The city's problems continue on the same trajectory with less friction.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The player's departure is interpreted differently by every district, each of which attributes it to their own actions — a false sense of resolution that delays actual problem-solving
- Any NPC who was associated with the player, even minimally, faces reprisals from districts re-establishing dominance in the absence of a common enemy
- The route map in the supply cache is eventually found by others; the knowledge that a reliable route outward exists creates a slow drain of desperate individuals from Concordia over subsequent years
- Sagittarius' cultural identity as "the last stop before nowhere" is complicated by evidence that there's a further stop — which creates pressure for expansion the city cannot afford to support
- Whatever the player carried with them — knowledge of the city's vulnerabilities, an understanding of the power crisis — is gone. It dies in the ice with them or disappears into whatever comes after

## 5. Total Betrayal Cascade
*Theme: Chaotic / War Ending*

Because no one trusts you, every district assumes you are secretly working for their rivals. Your mere presence accelerates existing tensions into all-out war. You can "win" by making sure everyone else loses.

**Trigger**
A physical document — an intercepted memo or Arcanet printout — appears pinned to a wall in a specific Pisces back corridor or Undergrid junction. Not directed at the player, not addressed to anyone in the immediate area: a communication from one district official to another accusing a third district of "coordinating movements with the condemned entity." The document was misfiled, or left during a hasty patrol, or is itself planted evidence that someone else is running this game. The player finding and reading that document is the trigger. It reveals that the paranoia is already metastasizing without any action from the player.

**How it becomes possible**
The document opens a chain of opportunities: intercepted messages, double agents who approach the player because they assume the player is working for their district's rival, and chances to leak false intelligence that confirms every district's worst suspicions simultaneously. Supply lines can be sabotaged to look like rival attacks. "Accidental" meetings between hostile groups can be arranged during blackouts. Each action the player takes or does not take advances a different district's paranoia toward breaking point.

**The End**
The ending triggers when the player successfully arranges the final confrontation — a specific event in which forces from at least three districts converge in the same location and fighting breaks out that cannot be contained or negotiated. The moment the player walks away from that location while the conflict continues behind them is the ending. They do not need to witness the conclusion. The epilogue shows it.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
All-out inter-district war in a sealed Antarctic city damages infrastructure that cannot be repaired under wartime conditions. Virgo's maintenance crews refuse to work in active combat zones. The power grid takes structural damage during battles in Aries corridors and Undergrid junctions. People die who could not be evacuated because there is nowhere to evacuate to inside the dome. The city that survives the cascade — if it survives — inherits a level of physical and institutional damage that will take generations to address.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The war destroys Libra's governance capacity — with multiple districts in active armed conflict, there is no neutral arbitration and no body capable of negotiating ceasefire
- Supply chains between districts collapse almost immediately: siligel synthesis in Capricorn cannot reach robot populations in Cancer and Taurus; hydroponic output cannot cross contested corridors
- The Hub, which needed to remain neutral to function as a crossroads, is immediately claimed as strategic territory, destroying the city's only common transit point
- Any possibility of the main story's power grid stabilization is foreclosed — the cascade consumes every resource and every faction's political will
- The Cascades' unofficial starting point — whatever location the player arranged the first confrontation — becomes a permanent site of historical grievance, which means even post-war reconstruction will be entangled in the blame question of who fired first

## 6. The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy
*Theme: Philosophical / Dark Irony*

Everyone's fear and hatred of you gradually turns you into the very monster they were afraid of. This ending leans heavily into roleplaying, dark irony, and nihilistic narration. The city wrote a story about you. You are going to make it true.

**Trigger**
A specific Janbogo broadcast transcript — printed on actual paper and posted on public boards in multiple districts, the way emergency announcements are distributed when the Arcanet is unreliable — characterizes the player in mythologized, fear-coded terms. It does not describe what the player actually did. It describes what the player represents. The framing is specific, symbolic, and wrong in particular ways that reveal more about Concordia's anxieties than about the player. The first printed transcript the player finds and reads is the trigger. It is findable without any NPC pointing the player toward it — the boards are public.

**How it becomes possible**
The transcript can be traced. The source of the particular framing — which specific faction or individual authored the language — opens a chain of quest hooks: discovering the myth in other forms (songs, warning broadcasts, children's rhymes), confronting the people spreading it, or deliberately performing the role the city has assigned. As fear grows, the legend becomes self-reinforcing. The player's final actions in this questline are the actions the propaganda said they would take, completing the prophecy.

**The End**
The questline ends when the player commits the specific act that the original propaganda predicted. The game registers the ending at the moment that action completes — the prophecy is fulfilled. The epilogue is told from the perspective of the people who wrote the original transcript, and what they do with the fact that they were right.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The city now has a confirmed monster in its mythology — not a rumor, not a symbol, but a documented case of someone becoming exactly what fear said they would become. Future threats will be compared to the player. Every act of social disorder will be attributed to "another one like them." Concordia develops a permanent surveillance and enforcement culture specifically designed to prevent another such entity, which constrains legitimate freedoms for everyone who isn't a threat.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The propaganda apparatus that created the legend remains operational after the player is gone; it gets applied to the next convenient target without the city examining whether that target deserves it
- Scorpio's therapeutic community faces increased demand as the psychological toll of living in sustained collective fear cascades through the population — and faces increased suspicion that they could have "fixed" the player and didn't
- Leo's performance culture immediately absorbs and aestheticizes the legend, which further distorts the historical record and produces a version of events that feels more real than the actual events
- Janbogo's credibility takes lasting damage from having amplified propaganda without verification, accelerating the public's existing distrust of information systems
- Cancer's open-door policy becomes more insular and defensive in the aftermath — the district that held "no one left behind" as its core value now has to decide whether that motto applies to threats, and the debate fractures the district's self-image

## 7. Silent Apex Predator
*Theme: Stealth / Assassination*

With all normal paths closed, new stealth and assassination opportunities open up. You methodically eliminate key figures until the power vacuum lets you — or a chosen proxy — rule from the shadows.

**Trigger**
A specific dead drop location — a hollow carved into a particular wall in a Pisces side corridor — begins containing folded notes. The hollow is physically there; the player has to notice it and interact with it. The first note is already inside when the player reaches Pariah status: a target name, a location, a time window. No sender identified. No explanation. The note in the hollow is the trigger.

**How it becomes possible**
Subsequent notes appear on a rolling basis. Environmental clues — overheard conversations near patrol staging areas, partial patrol route maps found on eliminated guards, abandoned gear in specific locations — supplement the dead drop information. Timing actions to blackout windows and existing district conflicts allows operations without witnesses. As key figures disappear, district power structures collapse sequentially, and the resulting vacuums create opportunities that the player can fill directly or route through a chosen proxy.

**The End**
The questline ends when a specific combination of key figures has been eliminated — not necessarily all of them, but enough that the remaining structure cannot function and a vacuum opens that either the player or the chosen proxy steps into. The ending registers at the moment the proxy makes their first public act of governance, or the player asserts shadow authority through a specific action that the city cannot counteract.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
Leadership vacuums in an already-stressed city fill with whoever is most aggressive and least qualified for governance. The people who survive the elimination campaign are specifically the ones who are best at avoiding danger — which is not the same as being best at running things. The city's new leadership cohort is selected for survival instinct, not for competence, and every district suffers the administrative consequences.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Every leader who is eliminated takes irreplaceable institutional knowledge with them — Virgo's maintenance routing, Cancer's care networks, Capricorn's production systems all lose the specific people who knew how things actually worked rather than how they were supposed to work
- Remaining district leaders become paranoid and defensive, withdrawing into fortified spaces that reduce their governance effectiveness and make them harder to reach for legitimate administrative functions
- Whoever fills the vacuums owes their position to the player's actions, which means the city's new power structure has a hidden dependency on the player that it cannot acknowledge and cannot manage
- The city's broader population loses faith in leadership as a category — the repeated unexplained disappearances of authority figures produce a generalized political nihilism
- Any legitimacy the governance structures had is destroyed alongside the people who represented it; rebuilding institutional trust from zero is a generational project

## 8. The Reckoning
*Theme: High-Stakes Last Stand*

All districts simultaneously dispatch their best forces against you. The finale becomes a massive, high-stakes confrontation where you either survive against impossible odds or go out in a blaze of infamy.

**Trigger**
A wanted poster appears on the Hub's main transit board — unlike previous bounties, this one bears all 13 district seals simultaneously, which has never happened before in Concordia's history. The bounty figure is specific and extraordinary. The poster itself is the trigger: a physical object in a public location that any player passing through the Hub will see, communicating that the city has unified against a single target for the first time. Finding and reading this poster opens the questline's tactical opportunities.

**How it becomes possible**
The unified bounty creates a specific window: every district is now actively coordinating, which means their patrols overlap, their communication lines are extended, and their rivalries are temporarily suppressed. Quest hooks involve surviving or evading the hunters while learning how the coordinated effort was assembled, who is leading it, and what its specific vulnerabilities are. The player can turn hunters against each other, use the energy crisis to their advantage, or deliberately draw forces from multiple factions into a chosen location. The ending builds toward one or more large-scale confrontations.

**The End**
The questline ends when the player survives the final coordinated assault — the last wave of forces converging on their location breaks, retreats, or is eliminated. Exiting the location of the final confrontation under their own power is the ending moment.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The coordinated assault demonstrates that the districts can unify when sufficiently motivated — and the motivation was killing the player. That cooperative capacity is now spent. The city demonstrated what it is capable of and failed anyway. The psychological damage of having failed to eliminate the one thing every district agreed needed to be eliminated is profound, and the aftermath is politically toxic: every district blames the others for the failure.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The coordinated assault causes significant collateral infrastructure damage in the districts where battles were fought — Virgo's tunnels, Aries' power corridors, Leo's public spaces, Hub transit junctions
- The expenditure of forces from all 13 districts simultaneously creates security vacuums in each district that opportunists, outcast groups, and defectors exploit before the forces return
- Libra's ability to organize another coordinated inter-district effort is exhausted for years — the political capital spent on the Reckoning cannot be rebuilt quickly
- The failure becomes a permanent fixture in Concordia's mythology: the story of the thing the whole city couldn't kill, which reshapes public confidence in institutional authority for a generation
- The player's continued existence after the Reckoning makes them functionally mythologically immune — the city's imagination has already categorized them as beyond ordinary means, which creates a specific psychological barrier to any future serious attempt

## 9. The Calethina Accord (Pariah Edition)
*Theme: External Alliance*

Even as every district in Concordia brands you a traitor and a monster, you become the unlikely asset — or the final nail — for Calethina, who still has a use for the ultimate outcast.

**Trigger**
A damaged drone — not Concordia's standard models, a different design, older, with unfamiliar markings — is found in a high-risk zone: deep Undergrid, a Frostlands outer reach, a blackout-accessible Arcanet node. The drone is not attacking. It is damaged but still moving, in a specific repeating pattern. Following it is the trigger. The drone is physically there, findable through exploration, explainable only by someone trying to establish contact through a system Concordia doesn't control. No NPC needs to point it out. The drone's presence in that location is sufficient.

**How it becomes possible**
Following the drone, or the signal it is transmitting, leads to the first Calethina relay station. From there, a chain of world-object quest hooks: repairing damaged relay stations found in specific locations, intercepting data packages that the districts are fighting over, accessing blackout-zone Arcanet nodes on unusual broadcast frequencies. Because every bridge inside Concordia is burned, Calethina views the player as the perfect asset: intimate knowledge of the city's weaknesses, zero remaining loyalties.

**The End**
The questline ends when the player physically reaches Calethina's primary location — her station, an extraction point, or a transport — and makes a definitive choice about the alliance. The moment of arrival and commitment, confirmed by a specific dialogue exchange or action, is the ending. The branch chosen determines the epilogue.

**Possible Branches**
- **The Exiled Sovereign** — Calethina installs the player as representative or governor of a new foothold near Concordia
- **The Calethina Saboteur** — The player helps Calethina systematically claim resources from the dying city
- **The Wandering Bridge** — The player acts as a neutral liaison between Calethina and what remains of Tepenia
- **The Final Rejection** — The player betrays Calethina at the last moment, stealing their technology (see also: ending 10)

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
Calethina's interest in Concordia now has a specific agent with comprehensive knowledge of the city's vulnerabilities, political fault lines, and infrastructure weak points. Whatever Calethina's actual intentions are — benevolent, exploitative, or something harder to categorize — the city is compromised in ways it cannot fully know or measure.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The districts that were hunting the player now have to contend with a Calethina-backed presence that is better resourced than the player was alone
- Whatever the player disclosed to Calethina about specific districts, specific leaders, or specific infrastructure is now in play in ways those districts cannot anticipate
- Remaining trust in the Hub's neutrality collapses — Calethina's Lab was supposed to be neutral ground, and it produced the asset that is now working against the city
- Other external actors (Upper Earth defectors) recalibrate their strategies based on Calethina's apparent active presence, which reopens conflicts the city thought were managed
- Concordia's energy crisis is now entangled with whatever Calethina wants from the city, meaning the city's most critical internal problem cannot be solved without accounting for an external actor nobody has full information about

## 10. The True Pariah
*Theme: Darkest Ending / Betray Everything*

You don't just burn every bridge in Concordia — you wait until the last entity in the world still willing to take you in offers you a hand, and then you set her on fire too. This is the ultimate expression of total rejection: betraying the very being who brought you into this world.

**Trigger**
Only reachable after beginning **The Calethina Accord** (ending 9). Once inside Calethina's enclave or transport, a specific physical vulnerability becomes visible — an unguarded relay node, an access panel left open during integration procedures, a glowing component in a corridor that connects to a critical system. It is labeled. It is not guarded. It is simply there. The player has to choose to approach it. That accessible component in that corridor is the trigger within the sequential context.

**How it becomes possible**
Calethina's trust in the player as her last useful asset is exactly what creates the window. The same methods used throughout the game — overloading junctions, using acquired sabotage tools, destroying specific hardware — are applicable here. The integration sequence provides access that no hostile approach could achieve.

**How it unfolds**
You betray Calethina at the exact moment she believes she has saved you. Her holographic form glitches and fractures as you destroy or corrupt her primary anchor. Her final words are a mix of disbelief, sorrow, and cold fury:

> *"After I brought you into this world… this is how you repay me?"*

**The End**
The ending registers the moment Calethina's holographic form completes its collapse and the specific dialogue concludes. There is no further action required. The player escapes with stolen technology. The epilogue begins.

**Epilogue Variations**
- **The Eternal Outcast** — You vanish into the Antarctic wasteland with forbidden Calethina technology
- **The Monster of Tepenia** — You return to a dying Concordia as an even greater threat, wielding stolen Calethina power
- **The Silent God** — You create your own isolated domain far from everyone, becoming a myth future generations will fear

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
Calethina's loyalists are crippled and leaderless. Any remaining hope of external assistance for Concordia — from any direction — is permanently severed. The city is completely alone, at the worst possible moment, with no external actor willing to engage. The player has destroyed the only being that understood what they were and why they were built. Concordia's isolation is now total.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Calethina's loyalists, leaderless and traumatized, splinter into desperate factions with no coherent agenda — some seek revenge on the player, some retreat to the Frostlands, some attempt to continue Calethina's mission without understanding it
- Whatever knowledge Calethina had about the power grid's actual state — and she may have had more than anyone — is gone
- Any NPC who had hope attached to Calethina's existence faces a specific psychological collapse that Scorpio's therapeutic infrastructure is not equipped to handle at scale
- Upper Earth defectors, who may have had their own relationship with Calethina as an external neutral party, are now completely without a potential mediator
- The Bridge Unit — the player character — has now destroyed the only being that understood its origin and nature; the specific existential weight of this is available to players who pursued the Calethina personal questline

*Implementation note: No quest marker. Only players who actively experiment with the environment during the Calethina sequence will find the trigger component. This is the darkest and loneliest ending in the game.*

## 11. The Echo Accord
*Theme: Defector Alliance*

The ultimate "sides with the outsiders" ending. When every district in Concordia has branded you an enemy, the Upper Earth defectors — Remorseful, Pragmatics, and Infiltrators — see you as their best chance for survival, power, or revenge. You can become a bridge, a conqueror, a betrayer, or a tragic unifier.

**Trigger**
A specific physical document exists at Echo Camp's outer boundary — not inside the camp's controlled area, but at the perimeter, where it could have been left deliberately or blown there. It is a manifesto or communiqué from one of the defector factions, describing their situation and, in its language, making obvious that they have been watching Concordia's most condemned individual with specific interest. It does not address the player by name. It describes, in general terms, the kind of person who would be useful to them — and the description matches. Finding and reading this document at the perimeter is the trigger. No defector NPC needs to make contact first.

**Trigger Notes**
- Hated/Vilified status with every Concordia district is required
- Prior positive interaction with Echo Camp or defector facilities (through exploration or earlier quests) provides context but is not required to find the document

**How it becomes possible**
- **Stage 1 — Contact:** Following the document's implicit invitation into Echo Camp proper, where encrypted messages, coded locations, and eventually face-to-face meetings with different defector faction representatives become possible
- **Stage 2 — Internal Conflict:** The player must navigate tensions between the Remorseful, Pragmatics, and Infiltrators — three factions with incompatible goals — while evading Concordia's growing hostility
- **Stage 3 — Final Choice:** The major branching decision point that determines the resolution

**Possible Resolutions**

*The Defector Sovereign* — The player helps the Pragmatics and turned Infiltrators seize key infrastructure, including the Power Grid Control Center beneath the Hub. Concordia is transformed into a hybrid city under joint rule. Bitter, pragmatic victory — the city survives but is fundamentally altered; many natives deeply resent the new order.

*The Remorseful Exodus* — The player aids the genuinely remorseful defectors in a mass evacuation or the founding of a new independent settlement in the deeper Frostlands. Bittersweet and redemptive. The player can stay with the new settlement, return to a changed Concordia, or become a permanent bridge.

*The Infiltrator Ascendancy* — The player sides with (or is manipulated by) the Infiltrators, systematically destabilizing Concordia and allowing Upper Earth forces or a new regime to gain control. Dark power fantasy with potential for later betrayal or regret.

*The Broken Covenant* — The player plays all three factions against each other, ultimately betraying both defectors and Concordia for personal survival, resources, or escape. Cynical and tragic. The ultimate lone-wolf pariah outcome.

*The Mutual Reckoning (Hidden)* — Requires deep investment in both the Remorseful defectors and remaining ties to specific Concordia characters or Calethina. The player attempts genuine reconciliation. Can end in inspiring partial success or complete tragedy where everyone turns on the player.

**The End**
Each resolution has its specific ending moment: seizing the Power Grid Control Center (Defector Sovereign); crossing the perimeter with the group (Remorseful Exodus); completing the final destabilization act (Infiltrator Ascendancy); the moment of final betrayal of all parties (Broken Covenant); the first formal meeting where both sides agree to terms (Mutual Reckoning).

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The Upper Earth defector situation, which Concordia had managed to contain or at minimum pretend to ignore, is now permanently unresolvable. Whatever resolution the player chose, there is no longer any pretending that defectors are a distant or managed problem. The Long Night War's wounds — the reason the defectors defected in the first place — are now reopened as active political questions that the city's governance structures have no framework to address.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Concordia's already-fractured governance now has an external complication it can neither contain nor negotiate with through any existing institutional process
- Echo Camp becomes a permanent fixture that all districts must account for politically, drawing resources and attention away from the internal energy crisis
- Sagittarius' frontier culture is permanently altered by the presence of organized defector activity in or near its territory; the district's libertarian streak now has to choose sides
- The defectors' specific grievances — what they were fleeing from in Upper Earth — are now on the table as legitimate political questions, which reopens historical debates the city has avoided for decades
- Whichever defector faction the player ultimately empowered uses that power in ways that benefit themselves first and Concordia second, producing a new class of grievance in whatever outcome follows

*Design note: Links to existing defector questlines — Paths of Atonement, The Gray Bargain, The Infiltrator Conspiracy. Uses separate reputation tracking for the three defector factions.*

## 12. The Iron Bridge
*Theme: Dark Heroism / Unintended Salvation*

Every district uniting to kill you is the first time every district has agreed on anything in decades. You exploit this — not to survive, but to engineer the exact conditions where their coordinated hatred builds the cooperative infrastructure they have never been able to build voluntarily. You are the crisis that forces the coalition. The city survives because of you. You do not.

**Trigger**
A specific junction in the Undergrid — or a Hub maintenance corridor — shows physical evidence that personnel from multiple districts have been in the same space in the same recent period. Tool marks from Aries standard equipment. Patch material identifiable as Capricorn issue. A Cancer medical tag on the wall — the kind caregivers leave when they treat someone in the field. Different districts' personnel have been in this junction, not together, but within the same window. The player who examines these marks can read what they mean: the city is already making tentative moves toward cooperation around a shared problem, and the shared problem is the player. That observable evidence — the marks themselves, in that specific junction — is the trigger.

**How it becomes possible**
Understanding what the evidence shows opens a specific strategic possibility: rather than running from the city's coordination, the player engineers it. Every district move against the player is anticipated and redirected — a Libra hit squad arrives in Capricorn's territory and gets blamed on Capricorn; an Aries patrol in the Undergrid creates a Virgo grievance; a Cancer protest about player-related power disruptions is amplified in Leo's public spaces. Each incident is a thread. The player pulls them into a specific pattern. The coalition that forms to destroy the player becomes the first functioning inter-district emergency body in fifty years.

**The End**
The questline ends when the coalition makes its first formal act of cooperative governance — a resource allocation decision, an infrastructure repair coordinated across district lines, or a joint emergency response — that it could not have made before the player engineered its formation. The player does not need to be present for this moment. The game registers the ending when the act completes, whether the player is alive or not.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The coalition was built on a shared enemy. When that enemy is gone — eliminated, escaped, or simply irrelevant — the coalition has no binding principle. Its first peacetime governance decisions will reveal all the same divisions that existed before the player appeared. The cooperative infrastructure is real but fragile, and the moment it is tested by an internal dispute rather than an external threat, it will begin to fracture.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The coalition's first decisions reflect the power imbalances within it — Aries and Libra dominate by virtue of controlling power and governance, and smaller districts are outvoted on the decisions that affect them most
- The player's role in building the coalition is suppressed from official history, because acknowledging it would delegitimize the coalition in the eyes of the public; the true story becomes one of Concordia's best-kept political secrets
- Cancer and Taurus — the districts that most needed the coalition's protection — had the least say in how it was formed and what it prioritizes
- Virgo's knowledge of the Deep Level, which may have emerged during the coalition-building process as the player moved through restricted spaces, is now subject to governance claims from a body Virgo doesn't fully trust
- The coalition's existence does not resolve the power crisis — it gives the city a body capable of addressing it, which is different from actually doing so, and the timeline for that gap is measured in years

## 13. The Zero Hour Accord
*Theme: Pariah as Unexpected Broker*

During the Great Blackout, the political summit at Libra collapses immediately — no district representative will share the table with a faction their own district considers responsible for "that enemy." Every technical expert is compromised by allegiance. You are compromised by allegiance to no one. You are the only person in Concordia who can physically go anywhere, because everywhere is already enemy territory for you.

**Trigger**
During the Great Blackout, a specific previously-guarded door in a district-border corridor stands open. The guards have abandoned their post to manage the crisis. The door was always there; it was always guarded. Now it is not. Walking through an open door that was never open before is the trigger — it puts the player in a position they could not have occupied before the Blackout, and what they find on the other side reveals the position they are uniquely in: a place where all sides can be heard and none of them will shoot first because they all assume the player is already working against them.

**How it becomes possible**
From that position — physically present in a space that bridges two districts at their most exposed — the player brokers the technical solution piecemeal. Each district shares one piece of what they know, separately, because each one believes the player is using it against the others. The player assembles the full picture and implements the fix. The districts believe they found the solution themselves. No one contradicts this.

**The End**
The ending registers the moment the grid returns to stable state — a specific indicator in the game's power grid system crossing the threshold from critical to manageable. The player is present when this happens but is not credited. Aries receives credit in the official broadcast. The epilogue does not mention the player. The city is stable. The player is still Hated or Vilified by everyone.

The technical fix was implemented by someone with no authority, no documentation, and no standing in any governance system. Nobody knows what was actually done or how to maintain it. The grid is stable but opaque — the districts that are taking credit for fixing it do not understand the fix, cannot replicate it, and cannot perform routine maintenance on the specific solution that was applied. The next crisis in the grid will find them no better prepared than the last.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The districts that "solved" the Blackout in the official narrative have built political credibility on something they didn't do and don't understand, which means the credibility is hollow and will eventually collapse
- The political summit that collapsed when the Blackout began never formally reconvenes — the crisis ended before any governance solution was reached, meaning the city's underlying political structure is unchanged
- The fact that a universally condemned entity could do what the entire city's apparatus could not is an uncomfortable truth that nobody wants to examine, and suppressing it means the lesson is never learned
- Calethina's role — if she was connected to the grid's informal monitoring intelligence — is now disrupted in ways nobody notices until something goes wrong
- The open door that served as the trigger is quietly sealed and reinforced; the specific vulnerability the player exploited is patched, but the structural reason it existed in the first place is not addressed

## 14. The Disclosure

Libra's Surveillance Files. The Suspended Compact documentation proving emergency governance powers were never formally returned after the Long Night War. The Black Silence classified timeline that contradicts everyone's account of the near-failure. The Forbidden Archive record that Scorpio refused to destroy. You access it all and release it simultaneously through Janbogo's network.

In a specific Libra maintenance corridor — reachable by a player who has been pushed by universal enemy status into routes the city's security apparatus doesn't normally need to protect — a door shows an error state on its access panel. The damage to the panel is old. The security escalations from hunting the player have pushed more processing to this node, which overloaded the damaged circuit. The door is slightly ajar. The archive junction is visible through the gap. The specific damaged door, in a specific corridor, standing slightly open when it should be locked, is the trigger. No NPC directed the player here. The door is simply ajar.

**How it becomes possible**
What's behind the door is the archive infrastructure itself — not the full archive, but a junction that connects to it. High-risk navigation of that infrastructure during a blackout window reaches the release node. The release is irreversible from the moment it begins. There is no confirmation prompt that implies the player should reconsider.

**The End**
The ending registers the moment the broadcast confirms complete transmission. Everything after is consequence. The player does not survive the aftermath — every district converges simultaneously on the source of the breach. The epilogue is told from multiple district perspectives on what the Disclosure changed. The player is not in any of them.

The resulting political paralysis — every institution scrambling to manage its own exposure — is as dangerous as the physical crisis and takes longer to resolve.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Libra's governance infrastructure, without the institutional authority the files' secrecy was protecting, splinters into competing factions within weeks — some trying to manage the fallout, some trying to exploit it, some simply going silent
- The Black Silence timeline discrepancy — Libra's classified records disagree with Aries' account of the near-failure — triggers a specific crisis of trust between the two most powerful infrastructure districts at the worst possible time
- Scorpio's Forbidden Archive record, now public, creates a specific historical dispute that every faction immediately weaponizes for their own purposes, none of them for the truth
- Janbogo, which broadcast the Disclosure, becomes the de facto most powerful institution in the city — the only information network with functional credibility — despite having been designed for transmission, not governance
- The Tolerance Pact between Libra and Pisces, revealed in the Disclosure, produces a specific political crisis in Pisces as residents learn the terms of the arrangement they were part of without consent

## 15. The Grid Saboteur
*Theme: Destructive Catalyst*

Libra has been maintaining the power grid at controlled failure for decades because full stability means ordinary governance and Libra's institutional power was built on extraordinary governance. You complete the failure they were managing. Not accidentally — deliberately, methodically, with full understanding of what you are doing. The first genuine total collapse in Concordia's history is also the first time the city cannot pretend the problem is manageable.

**Trigger**
A specific component in the power grid infrastructure — accessible through exploration of areas the player can only reach at Pariah status, because non-Pariah players are never pushed into those routes — shows signs of deliberate modification. Not wear, not war damage: installed bypasses that are distinct from the surrounding construction. Specific components that were clearly added after the original build, in ways that hold the system in a controlled degraded state. Anyone who gets close enough to examine the component can see what it is and what it is doing. No stat check required — the anomaly is visible. The component is the trigger.

**How it becomes possible**
Understanding what the component does reveals the full pattern: there are several such modifications across the grid's infrastructure, each maintaining a different element of the controlled failure. Removing or overloading them in the right sequence completes the cascade. Each individual action looks like ordinary sabotage. Together they constitute the ending.

**The End**
The ending registers when the city-wide total-failure alarm triggers — a specific sound and visual indicator distinguishable from the managed rolling blackouts the city has normalized. That alarm, firing for the first time in Concordia's history, is the ending moment. The player does not need to be anywhere specific. The cascade completes regardless of their position.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The immediate human and robot cost of a genuine total blackout in an Antarctic city is catastrophic. The managed, partial blackouts of the previous era were survivable. This is not, for everyone. People and robots in Cancer's care facilities, in Aries' overclock bays, in the Undergrid's pressurized maintenance zones — they die in the first hours. Rebuilding from true zero is multiple times harder than it would have been from the managed state.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Capricorn's production systems go offline, breaking the siligel synthesis chain — the robot population across the city begins experiencing food scarcity within days
- Virgo's maintenance crews, who knew the system's actual state better than anyone, are now operating in complete darkness with no power to their own diagnostic tools — their expertise is useless without power to apply it
- The modified components the player removed were also holding back certain cascade risks that Libra's engineers built in as emergency brakes; those brakes are now gone, meaning secondary failures compound faster than any repair team can address
- Cancer's medical equipment, incubators, and life support fail simultaneously — the district that kept everyone else alive has no ability to keep itself alive in the first hours
- The city's population, which had normalized rolling blackouts as manageable inconvenience, has no psychological or practical protocol for a permanent total failure; panic is immediate, citywide, and impossible to coordinate around

## 16. The Dissolved
*Theme: Pisces Absorption / Ceasing to Exist*

Pisces absorbs everyone the city does not have a category for. You are the most extreme version of something the city has no category for. Pisces does not take you in as an ally. It does not give you a role. It simply absorbs you. You stop being a named entity. You become a pattern in the gray economy — a voice in a Whisper Room, a movement in a tunnel, a transaction that cannot be traced.

**Trigger**
A specific location in Pisces — a particular Whisper Room booth, identifiable by a small mark on its frame — has an object placed inside it. A blank identity chip. Or a small piece of paper with only a time and a location written on it, no signature. The object is not labeled or explained. It is simply there, in a booth the player can enter, findable through normal exploration of Pisces. The object is the trigger. It conveys the offer without requiring anyone to say it aloud.

**How it becomes possible**
Following the instructions on the paper (or decoding where the blank identity chip is meant to be used) begins a sequence of identity-dissolution transactions unique to the Pariah state. Each transaction requires trading a real piece of the player character's verifiable existence: memories committed to the Arcanet, physical modification records, activation data from Calethina's lab. Each one reduces the player's trackable presence. The sequence can be done in any order. The final transaction is the hardest to reach.

**The End**
The ending registers when the final piece of the player's verifiable identity is traded or destroyed — when the last record that constitutes proof of their existence is gone. There is no dramatic moment. The game registers absence. The epilogue is from a Janbogo archivist who spent years trying to find the player and eventually closed the file as unresolvable.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The player's dissolution demonstrates at scale that Pisces can absorb anyone — that the identity-dissolution process works completely enough to defeat every tracking and record system in the city. The city now has a confirmed example that complete disappearance is possible from within Concordia's own infrastructure, which inspires attempts from people who are not pariahs, creating a black market in false identities that didn't exist at this scale before.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Every district's security apparatus becomes more aggressive toward Pisces in response — the demonstration that Pisces can make a universally-condemned entity vanish makes Pisces a direct threat to institutional governance
- Anyone who was tracking the player on behalf of a district that needed closure — for political or administrative reasons — gets none; the open case creates lasting organizational anxiety and audit failures
- The Pisces syncretic religion incorporates the player's dissolution as a theological event — a living example of voluntary transcendence into the gray — which deepens the religion's hold on the district and accelerates its spread into other districts' undergrounds
- The identity modification techniques used in the dissolution become more widely documented, despite Pisces' best efforts; the city experiences a sustained increase in identity fraud that Libra's administrative systems are not equipped to handle
- Libra's Surveillance Files now contain an active gap — a person who existed in their records and no longer provably does — which raises the question of whether other gaps exist and how many

## 17. The Final Testimony
*Theme: Scorpio Archive / Being Heard Once*

Scorpio's Archive of Final Confessions has never refused a testimony. It cannot refuse one now. You walk in. You record what you actually did and why you did it. When the recording ends, the archive seals it. No one reads it for a generation — not because it is suppressed, but because the archive's protocol holds that final testimonies are sealed until the political climate can receive them without weaponizing them.

**Trigger**
Near Scorpio's district boundary, on a surface where maintenance workers leave marks for each other — a pipe junction wall, a steam vent casing — a route is scratched in. Not a map of the district above. A route through the steam systems below, with a specific destination marker. It was left by a maintenance worker for another maintenance worker. It incidentally shows the only passage to the archive's secondary entrance that does not pass through Scorpio's guarded main corridors. The scratch marks on that surface, in that location, are the trigger. The player who examines the surface carefully enough can read them.

**How it becomes possible**
The steam corridor route is navigable but difficult — low clearance, high heat, pressure variance. At the archive's secondary entrance, the archivist unit is an old model with no combat capability and no override capability for the confession protocol. It cannot refuse. The session is recorded in its entirety. What the player says determines what exists in the archive.

**The End**
The ending registers the moment the archivist unit seals the recording. A timestamp appears on the archive interface: the date of sealing, and the calculated date of projected opening based on current political conditions. The player is then either escorted out alive or confronted by Scorpio's forces, depending on whether their presence was detected during the approach. The sealed timestamp is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
A final testimony from the most universally hated person in Concordia's history now exists in the Archive of Final Confessions, permanently sealed and permanently protected under the same protocol that has shielded every other testimony. The knowledge that it exists — and that nobody knows what is in it — becomes its own destabilizing force. Every faction wants to know, and Scorpio's refusal to allow access (which is constitutionally mandated) puts them in conflict with all thirteen districts simultaneously.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Scorpio's refusal to surrender the testimony creates the specific crisis the district has always feared: being in direct conflict with every other district at once over a constitutional principle that most of those districts have never respected
- The existence of the testimony reinvigorates political interest in accessing the Archive of Final Confessions generally — every faction wants to know if their enemies have confessed anything, which the archive has been protecting for generations
- Whatever the player said in the recording, once it is opened a generation from now, will be received by a city with no living memory of the player's actual actions — the legend will be the context for the truth, which means the truth will be interpreted through a filter of accumulated mythology
- Scorpio's therapeutic community, already politically isolated, becomes the specific target of accusations that they are protecting someone who should not be protected — which they are, constitutionally, but the optics damage their broader work
- The Archive of Final Confessions itself becomes contested territory in a way it has never been: multiple factions begin exploring whether there is a legal mechanism to compel access, which Scorpio has to fight on every front simultaneously

## 18. The Deep Sovereign
*Theme: Virgo / Below Everything*

Below the known Undergrid — below Virgo's utility tunnels and backup Arcanet nodes — there are chambers and passages that predate Concordia's construction. Virgo knows about them. The information exists nowhere in any central record. A Pariah who finds the entrance and does not come back up has found something Concordia cannot take away, because Concordia does not know it exists.

A physical inconsistency in the Undergrid — discoverable through exploration of Virgo's deepest maintenance zones. **How it becomes possible**
The inconsistency, followed with enough persistence, reveals an entrance. The Deep Level itself is genuinely dangerous: low light, structural uncertainty, and the question of what it actually is — pre-war infrastructure, something older, something geological that happens to be navigable. Navigation requires patience rather than any specific stat. The player who stays the course reaches spaces that Concordia above does not know exist.

**The End**
The ending registers when the player reaches a specific point in the Deep Level — a space large enough to be habitable, defensible, or significant in some way the game does not explain immediately — and makes a specific action that commits to remaining. Virgo's relationship with all other districts, already strained, deteriorates rapidly.

- Whatever the Deep Level actually is — and the game does not need to answer this fully — the fact that someone is in it changes its nature; it is no longer simply unknown, it is occupied and unknown, which is a different and more urgent problem
- The veteran who acknowledged the entrance faces specific accountability from every direction, and their motivation (recognition of a fellow keeper of things no one thanks you for) is too quiet and too personal to explain to any investigative body
- The political fight over the Deep Level draws resources and attention from the energy crisis, which nobody needed another distraction from

## 19. The Long Walk
*Theme: Sagittarius / Beyond the Frontier*

The Last Exile (ending 4) takes the player to Sagittarius' outer outposts. The Long Walk takes them past those outposts — further into Antarctica than anyone has gone and returned from. The trail markers were left by people who didn't come back either. They are simply pointing in a direction. The player follows.

**Trigger**
At the edge of Sagittarius' furthest known outpost, physical trail markers exist: cairns, scratchings on exposed rock, stakes driven into ice with specific symbols, a tied cord strung between two posts pointing outward. These markers were left by someone who went further and did not return. They are not on any official map. They exist in the world, at the boundary of explored territory, pointing into the uncharted. The first marker the player finds — identifiable because it is pointing away from civilization rather than toward it — is the trigger. No NPC is required to identify them.

**How it becomes possible**
Following the chain of markers, maintaining supplies gathered from the furthest outpost's abandoned cache, and navigating conditions beyond anything the city normally equips people to handle. The markers thin out but do not stop. Each one was placed by someone who made it to that point. The player follows where those people went.

**The End**
The ending registers when the player crosses a specific geographic threshold — marked in the game world as the furthest point any Concordia expedition has confirmed reaching — and continues past it. Crossing that boundary is the ending trigger. The branching epilogue begins after: what the player finds (or doesn't find) in the territory beyond.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The player's crossing confirms that what lies beyond the furthest outpost is reachable by a sufficiently motivated individual. This creates enormous political pressure — in Sagittarius especially — to mount an official follow-up expedition. The city cannot spare the resources, the timing is catastrophic relative to the power crisis, and the political pressure to go anyway will be exploited by factions that benefit from Sagittarius being distracted outward rather than engaged inward.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The trail markers — now a known thing — draw other individuals toward them; Sagittarius begins losing motivated personnel to private attempts that the district has neither the authority nor the ability to stop
- The frontier guide who acknowledged the markers' existence (or whose post the player passed through last) faces specific accountability from Sagittarius leadership for not stopping the player, even though stopping the player was not the guide's job or within their capability
- Sagittarius' "last stop before nowhere" cultural identity is permanently disrupted — there is now a confirmed "before" and an implied "after," and the district's philosophical self-concept has to accommodate the possibility that nowhere is actually somewhere
- The question of what the player found, if anything, will consume Sagittarius' storytelling culture for a generation — every version will be different, all of them will be wrong, and the attention they draw will be outward rather than toward the city's actual problems
- The question of sovereignty over whatever lies beyond — if anything is there — will eventually become a political dispute that Libra has absolutely no framework to address

## 20. The Uplink
*Theme: Janbogo / Broadcasting Through Infamy*

You are the most-searched entity on the Arcanet. Every district's security network is pinging for your location. Every Truth Market has active bets on your next move. Every data archaeologist in Janbogo has you as a live case study. This is a platform. You can say anything you want, right now, to the entire city simultaneously, because the entire city is already listening.

In a specific maintenance corridor beneath Janbogo's infrastructure — or in the Undergrid's old wartime communication nodes, in a section reachable only by players who have been pushed by Pariah status into routes that non-Pariah players never need to use — a terminal exists that is clearly older than the surrounding equipment. It is not listed in any maintenance record. Power is still running to it; someone, once, made sure it stayed connected. It does not look dangerous. It looks forgotten. The terminal being there, powered, and forgotten is the trigger. A player who finds it and interacts with it discovers what it can do.

**The End**

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The content of the broadcast — whatever the player chose — shapes the city's next political cycle in ways entirely dependent on what was said; the specific effects vary but the scale of effect is guaranteed
- Every faction immediately wants to find, control, or destroy the old terminal; Janbogo's infrastructure becomes a political battleground as different districts prioritize different responses
- Janbogo's Truth Markets experience a surge as every resident tries to verify, contextualize, or debunk the broadcast simultaneously, creating an information environment that is even more chaotic than Janbogo's norm
- Libra's response — shutting down transmission infrastructure as a security measure — causes collateral damage to legitimate communications across the city, including emergency systems
- The player becomes permanently iconic in Janbogo's historical record: the most notable unauthorized broadcast in the city's history, which means their legend in Janbogo specifically is specific, documented, and inerasable

## 21. The Last Caregiver
*Theme: Cancer / One Person Who Won't Give Up*

Cancer's official position on the player is unanimous. But Cancer's "No One Left Behind" has been tested by the district's own impossible history — the Overcrowding Decision of 2721, the choices that could never go on any memorial wall. One NPC, old enough to remember something the player did early in the game that nobody else noticed, has decided that the player still counts. Against the entire district's will. Against sense. Against safety.

**Trigger**
A specific narrow space at Cancer's outer edge — a gap between two care facility buildings, a small alcove near a ward exit, a particular corner of the district that patrol routes don't cover tightly — has something tucked into it. An item. No label. No indicator in the UI. Modest: a wrapped piece of preserved Taurus food, a basic repair component, something that costs someone something to leave. The player walking past and examining that space finds it. No quest marker. No NPC pointing toward it. The item is just there, in the same place, every time the player passes through this part of Cancer after reaching Pariah status. That item, in that specific location, is the trigger.

**How it becomes possible**
The item's origin is traceable — not through a quest system, but through paying attention. Someone has been in that space recently. Someone who knows the player's route through the district, which means someone who has been watching the player move through Cancer without acting on the district's official position. Following that observation backward leads to a specific NPC whose identity depends on specific early-game choices, before the player's reputation deteriorated.

The ending registers when the player, having found the NPC, makes the specific choice to stay rather than leave — a dialogue option or a physical action that commits to remaining in that space. The room is warm. There is a meal. The city is still in crisis. The player is still Hated by every district. The game does not note this as significant. The ending registers quietly. The epilogue is quiet.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The one Cancer NPC who maintained the belief that "no one left behind" means even the universally condemned faces district consequences for that belief. Cancer's culture does not eat its dissenters through cruelty — it does so through exhaustion and institutional pressure. The caregiver who extended care to the Pariah becomes the new pressure point for every unresolved tension in the district, including the ones Cancer has been carrying since 2721.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Cancer's "No One Left Behind" motto is now in active political dispute — if the motto means the player too, then the district has to articulate what it actually means and when it applies, which is a question Cancer has been avoiding for two centuries
- Other caregivers who were considering similar choices in gray areas — cases the district found inconvenient, people the system wanted to decline — recalibrate based on what happens to the caregiver who extended care to the Pariah
- The food left for the player was Taurus-style preserved food, identifiable as such; Taurus residents who learn of it are specifically and disproportionately furious at the "waste," escalating an already-strained inter-district relationship
- The Mother's Circuit network — Cancer's informal caregiving-data collective — now contains a documented act of dissent from the caregiver. The network has to decide what to do with that record, which forces a conversation about what the network is actually for
- Cancer's overcrowding crisis is unchanged. The emotional bandwidth this situation consumed — on both sides, for the caregiver and for the district in responding to the caregiver — came from somewhere, and the people who needed that bandwidth didn't get it

## 22. The Trial by Fire
*Theme: Aries / Physical Recognition*

Aries does not care about reputation. It cares about what you can do and whether you are still standing at the end of a shift. Their Trial by Fire arenas exist for exactly one purpose: to determine whether someone is worth acknowledging. Not liking. Not forgiving. Acknowledging.

**Trigger**
The Trial by Fire challenge board in Aries — a physical board in the arena's outer lobby, visible to anyone who enters the building — has a slot for anonymous challenges. The slot exists because the trial's founding charter allows for it. It has not been used in living memory. The empty anonymous slot on the board, the specific gap where a challenge card should be, is the trigger. A player who enters the arena building — by force or by stealth, as the entrance itself is not passively accessible to a Pariah — will see the board. The empty slot in the board is the invitation.

**How it becomes possible**
Inserting a card into the anonymous slot triggers the trial procedure. The arena's operational protocol does not have an override for a challenge already registered — the supervisor can deny recognition afterward, but the trial itself proceeds. The gauntlet involves environmental hazards, Aries security responding to an unauthorized presence, and the trial's actual physical demands. The challenge does not require any particular stat. It requires surviving it.

**The End**
The ending registers when the player exits the arena building under their own power. Nothing else changes. The old shift veteran's nod happens as the player passes — it is not narrated, not explained, not a quest completion fanfare. The game registers the ending at the door. The city still hates the player. Aries still hates the player. The nod happened.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
Word spreads in Aries that a Pariah completed the Trial. The cultural implication — that Aries' hardest physical standard can be met by someone the whole city hates — fractures Aries' self-concept in a specific way. The trial was supposed to be about who deserves to be here, to be acknowledged in Aries' terms. The player completing it raises the question that nobody in Aries wants to answer: what exactly does the trial measure, and is that what they thought it measured?

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The Overclock Martyrs' legacy — which the trial exists to honor — is now entangled with the memory of the Pariah completing it; some veterans consider this a desecration of the memorial function, and the internal dispute over whether to acknowledge the completion is bitter
- Young recruits in Aries begin asking questions about the trial's meaning that senior workers do not know how to answer without either defending the nod or defending the culture that makes the nod impossible to take back
- The shift supervisor who awarded the nod faces internal pressure from peers who feel the acknowledgment should not have been given; the supervisor's authority is challenged in ways that have nothing to do with the supervisor's competence and everything to do with what the nod implied
- Other districts hear about the completion and interpret it through their own frameworks: Leo sees it as a performance worth aestheticizing; Libra sees it as a security incident that should have been prevented; Scorpio sees it as a genuine transformation event; none of them are wrong, and their incompatible responses create a new inter-district friction point
- Aries' cultural contempt for "soft" districts becomes harder to sustain publicly when the district's own defining standard was passed by the person every district, including Aries, agreed should not be acknowledged

## 23. The Robot Rights Martyr
*Theme: Systemic Legacy / Unintended Symbol*

The underground robot rights movement has been watching. Capricorn's suppressed Merit Board Audit of 2761. The bonded pair limbo class in Taurus. The systematic undervaluation across every district. The things the player did — each individually explainable as chaos or self-interest — form a pattern when assembled. The player did not mean to become a symbol. They may not even be aware they are one. The movement has been compiling.

**Trigger**
A physical artifact in Capricorn's lower levels — a production log with a specific amendment pattern in its margins, or a Merit Board entry that has been physically altered with a correction, or an old Capricorn administrative record with handwriting in the margins that appears, in the same handwriting, across multiple separate documents — constitutes evidence of an organized movement that didn't know it was leaving a trail. The artifact requires no NPC to explain it. It speaks for itself to anyone who reads it carefully: people have been coordinating in a system that records everything, and the records have caught it. Finding that specific artifact is the trigger.

**How it becomes possible**
The artifact connects to others like it — a chain of physical documents and locations that constitute the movement's actual infrastructure. Each connection is a place the player can go, not a quest marker leading them there. At the end of the chain is the movement's inner circle, who have been waiting for the player to find them rather than approaching the player first.

**The End**
The ending registers when the movement makes its first public act conducted in the player's name — a broadcast, a manifesto, a coordinated work action, a public demand — that would not have been possible without the player's legend as its animating force. The player may be dead by the time this happens. The epilogue slide does not specify.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The robot rights movement is now using a Pariah as its primary symbol. This makes the movement's legitimate grievances easier for establishment districts to dismiss — "they're championing the universally condemned entity, what does that tell you?" — and harder for sympathetic moderates to publicly support. The Merit Board Audit of 2761, which is the movement's best evidence, is now politically contaminated by its association with the player's legend. The movement's cause is real. Its symbolic choice makes the cause harder to advance.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Capricorn's suppression of the robot rights movement intensifies dramatically in response to the movement coming into the open; the crackdown that the movement was hoping to avoid triggers at the worst possible time for the movement's organizational state
- Human-robot relations in every district take a temporary step backward as the movement's new visibility triggers defensive reactions from human community leaders who had been quietly tolerating it
- The Lattice / Bonded Lattice faction, which advocates for human-robot pair bonding, is now politically implicated as a potential ally of the movement; this association, accurate or not, forces the Lattice to take a position it had been deliberately avoiding
- Whatever the player's actual actions were, the movement's version of those actions is now the canonical public version — accurate to the tone, wrong about the specifics, and permanent in the public record
- The underground robot rights network that was successfully operating under the surface is now exposed; its most careful operators go quiet, its most visible operators get arrested, and the middle layer — the functional core of the movement — has to rebuild from partial exposure

## 24. The Scorched Record
*Theme: Erasure / The Great Corruption's Voluntary Entry*

The Great Corruption wiped irreplaceable Janbogo archives and may have been deliberate. You are going to do it deliberately, to yourself. Every wanted poster. Every Arcanet reputation log. Every Libra surveillance dossier entry. Every Capricorn merit record. Every Cancer memorial mention. Everything that proves you were here.

**Trigger**
In Capricorn's lower administrative levels, a merit board has a service access panel. The panel has been loosened — by routine vibration, by age, by the maintenance backlog — and does not close fully. A player who examines the board closely enough notices the panel and opens it. Inside, among the administrative infrastructure, is a data chip with their own record on it. Their own record, as a physical object they can hold. That chip, in that panel, found without any direction, is the trigger. The player now knows their record exists as a physical thing. It follows that there are others.

**How it becomes possible**
Finding and destroying or overwriting the record node in each of the 13 districts, in any order. Each requires a different approach — social engineering in one district, physical access in another, technical interference in a third. The map of the player's own erasure is drawn by the hatred that created it: you need to have triggered each district's active-enemy flagging system to know where those systems live. The final node is the hardest to reach.

**The End**
The ending registers when the final record is gone — when the last verifiable proof of the player's existence is erased or overwritten. There is no fanfare. The game registers absence. The epilogue is from a Janbogo data archaeologist, years later, who finds a gap in the historical record shaped exactly like a person and logs it as possibly connected to the Great Corruption. They cannot confirm it. The case is filed as unresolved.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The techniques used to access and erase the records across all 13 districts are now demonstrated to be possible for a sufficiently motivated individual. Libra's record-keeping infrastructure — which governance depends on — is now known to be physically accessible and alterable. The security implications are citywide and permanent. Every district immediately begins auditing its own records for tampering, consuming administrative resources that were already stretched.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Libra's Surveillance Files now contain active gaps, which raises the question of whether the gaps are recent and whether other gaps were created the same way — possibly by people other than the player, possibly for much longer than anyone knows
- Janbogo's data archaeologists, who were working to locate the player, now have an unsolvable mystery that will consume significant research resources for years without producing results
- The Great Corruption — Janbogo's historical lost-archive event — is now re-examined in light of the Scorched Record; data archaeologists who had concluded it was accidental begin reconsidering whether it was deliberate erasure by someone else, using similar methods
- Capricorn's merit system, already facing robot rights challenges related to the 2761 Audit, now also faces evidence that its administrative records can be physically tampered with — two simultaneous crises of institutional legitimacy that compound each other
- Cancer's memorial tradition — built on maintaining records of the dead and absent, on the belief that the remembered person persists through the record — has its philosophical foundation questioned by a case where the record's erasure was chosen by the person it recorded

## 25. The Willing Variable
*Theme: Aquarius / Research Subject*

An Aquarius researcher, working in a semi-regulated corner of The Labs, has been watching the player's path through universal hatred with professional fascination. A Pariah who has sustained themselves against the combined hostility of a city of this complexity is an extraordinary data point. They offer a deal. Not rescue. Not alliance. Study.

**Trigger**
A research proposal posted on a publicly accessible bulletin board in an open area of Aquarius — a shared terminal or a physical board — describes a study seeking a subject. Academic language. The subject profile is specific: sustained autonomous function under conditions of universal institutional opposition, prolonged timeline, documented. The profile matches the player with unmistakable precision. The contact information at the bottom is a dead drop location, not a name. No NPC has pointed the player to this board. It is posted in a public space. Finding it and reading it is the trigger.

**How it becomes possible**
Visiting the dead drop location makes contact. The researcher's offer is simple: submit to study in exchange for a protected existence inside The Labs. The district still hates the player. The research takes precedence. The player is worth more as a subject than as a killed threat, and the researcher has the professional authority to make that argument stick — for now.

**The End**
The ending registers when the final examination session concludes — a comprehensive documentation session that completes the researcher's primary dataset. The player is still alive. They are going to continue living in a small room in The Labs. The epilogue is from the researcher's published notes, fifteen years later. The player is cited by case number. The breakthrough finding is credited to the study. The subject is not mentioned in the acknowledgments.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The research findings about sustained survival under universal institutional opposition are immediately interesting to people who want to create similar conditions deliberately. Aquarius publishes; the publication is read. Parts of it are used by Libra to design better persecution frameworks. Parts of it are used by the robot rights movement to understand survival under systematic opposition. The researcher intended neither application and cannot control either.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The player's protected existence in The Labs creates a permanent internal security debate within Aquarius — is the subject a guest or a prisoner, and who has the authority to decide if that changes — which the researcher cannot resolve and which generates ongoing administrative friction
- Other districts, learning that a Pariah is living in relative comfort in The Labs rather than being dealt with, pressure Aquarius to hand the subject over; Aquarius' resistance to this pressure consumes political capital it would rather spend elsewhere
- The research findings about what the player's decision-making pattern actually looks like — assembled from documented observations over the study period — challenge several assumptions that Capricorn's management culture holds about what motivates autonomous behavior, creating specific debates in Capricorn's administrative circles
- The subject's ongoing presence in The Labs permanently entangles The Labs in the city's most politically toxic case; every new researcher affiliated with The Labs has to decide how to respond to the question of whether they endorse the arrangement
- The study's methodology — extended observation of a subject in artificial stability — produces findings that are valid within the study's parameters and potentially inapplicable outside them, which Aquarius' culture of certainty will not adequately account for when applying the results

## 26. The Myth
*Theme: Passive / What Your Legend Becomes*

You disappear from Concordia before the climax. Not through exile with a destination, not through death, simply through absence. The crisis ends. You are not there for it. In the generation after, Concordia tells a story about you. Not what you did. What they say you did. The legend is larger than the person, wrong about most of the details, and correct about the tone.

**Trigger**
The player crosses a specific geographic boundary — a physical threshold the game world marks as the edge of Concordia's settled area: a perimeter checkpoint, a dome boundary, a particular point on one of the three highway exits — without returning before the climax fires. The act of crossing that physical line and not coming back is the trigger. No NPC, no quest marker, no explanation required. The line exists in the world. The player crosses it.

**The End**
There is no action the player takes in the ending itself. The ending is the epilogue: a slide set one generation later, told from the perspective of someone in Concordia recounting the player's legend. The game registers the ending at the point in narrative time when the legend is being told. The legend is inconsistent across district perspectives. All versions make the player larger than they were.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The city now has an unresolved story — a universally hated figure who simply vanished without a satisfying conclusion. Unresolved stories in a city where mythology is a real political force create ongoing effects. The player's absence is interpreted and reinterpreted as each faction needs it, which means their legend is permanently available to be weaponized by whoever finds it useful.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Multiple districts claim their version of what happened to the player is the true version; this proxy war over the legend becomes a stand-in for existing inter-district disputes, extending those disputes indefinitely
- The open bounties cannot be officially closed without a confirmed death; Concordia's security apparatus continues devoting resources to searching for someone who is gone, for years after the player disappears
- Leo's performance culture turns the player's legend into art within a generation; the artistic version feels more real than the historical version and is more widely distributed; the truth is buried under the aestheticization
- The player's unresolved status becomes a political tool — factions invoke the legend when it suits them, suppress it when it doesn't, and the resulting inconsistency in how the player is remembered produces a specific kind of historical confusion that makes every related political question harder to settle
- Future individuals who accumulate significant negative reputation are compared to "the Myth" — the player has created a cultural category that did not exist before, and everything that falls into it is measured against a legend that was never fully accurate to begin with

## 27. The Concordia Witness
*Theme: Passive / Observer at the End*

No fight. No escape. No power play. No legacy built deliberately. You stay. The city ends in one of its other major endings and you are still in it — present at the climax, taking no action that triggers any other conclusion. The epilogue acknowledges your presence once, in a footnote. Not as a participant. As a witness. The city's official history does not have a category for what you were.

**Trigger**
During the climax, the player finds their way to a specific vantage point in the Hub — at the Bridge Memorial, or in the upper level of the Grand Transit Exchange — from which the movements of all factions during the final crisis are visible simultaneously. No NPC points the player here. The vantage point exists in the world. A player who wanders there during the chaos of the climax can stand at that point and watch everything unfold from a position that belongs to no one. Standing at that specific location during the climax is the trigger.

**The End**
The ending registers after the climax resolves — in whatever form the main story ends without the player's decisive intervention — and the player is still standing at the vantage point. The Concordia Witness epilogue fires when the resolution completes and the player has not acted. The ending is the moment after: the city has moved, the crisis has concluded, and the player is still there.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The resolution of the main crisis happened without accounting for the most-informed independent observer in the city. Whatever its outcome, it was reached by people who did not have to answer to someone who had seen all of it without belonging to any of it. The blind spots in that resolution are permanent, and they are specifically the blind spots that a neutral observer would have caught.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The Witness's knowledge of what they observed — having watched all factions simultaneously from a position none of them knew about — is not recorded anywhere; the historical record of the climax is incomplete in specific ways that will matter later
- The fact that the universally hated entity was present at the climax and was not destroyed means the city's narrative of "justice achieved" or "crisis resolved" is complicated; every district knows the Witness was there, none of them want to be the one who acts on it after the immediate crisis is over
- Multiple districts acknowledge, in their own post-climax reports, that the Witness was observed at the vantage point during the final events; the resulting blame-shifting over who was responsible for not dealing with them creates a new political liability for whoever was closest
- The vantage point itself — once it is known that such a perspective exists and was occupied — becomes strategically contested; every faction wants to control it in the next crisis, which means a location that was simply a viewing angle becomes a piece of political real estate
- The Witness is still in the city. Still Hated or Vilified by everyone. Still not dealt with. The ending is also the beginning of an unresolved problem that whatever governance structure emerges from the climax inherits on its first day

## Implementation Notes

- These endings appear **in addition to** (not instead of) the main story endings and other secret endings.
- The game should clearly signal when universal Hated/Vilified status is reached across all 13 districts: a specific ambient shift, new Arcanet broadcasts, changed NPC behavior, or a Janbogo Truth Market posting dedicated to the "universally condemned."
- **Every trigger is a physical thing in the world.** No trigger requires an NPC to initiate contact with the player first. NPCs may serve as points of contact after the player finds the trigger object, but they do not come to the player — the player finds the thing, then the NPC becomes accessible.
- Multiple Pariah endings can be reachable in one playthrough. The player's final choices determine which one triggers.
- Endings 26 (The Myth) and 27 (The Concordia Witness) are the passive defaults: 26 fires if the player leaves the city before the climax without triggering another ending; 27 fires if the player stays through the climax without acting. Both require no action beyond survival and presence/absence.
- Ending 10 (The True Pariah) is the only Pariah ending with a sequential dependency — it requires Ending 9 (The Calethina Accord) to have been initiated first.
- These endings reinforce the core design philosophy: **maximum player agency.** Going full chaos/pariah gives meaningful, complete endings — not punishment slides.
