# Robot-Aligned Endings

## Trigger Condition

These endings are available when the player has **consistently and demonstrably sided with the robot population** throughout the game — in conflicts, resource allocation decisions, political choices, companion relationships, and moral dilemmas. There is no single switch that triggers robot-aligned status; it accumulates across the full playthrough as a pattern of choices.

Robot-aligned endings do not require the player to be hostile to human interests. They require that when human and robot interests were in tension, the player consistently chose the robot side — and that this pattern of choice produced a specific, traceable effect on the city's political and cultural landscape.

**No single district Idolized ending covers this.** Districts in Concordia are mixed human-robot communities. The robot-aligned endings are specifically about the cross-district, city-wide impact of sustained pro-robot advocacy and cultural investment.

**Two endings, different modes of alignment:**
- **RA-1 (The Voice):** Political and advocacy alignment — siding with robots in governance, resource allocation, and formal disputes
- **RA-2 (The Between):** Cultural and relational alignment — deep immersion in robot culture, religion, and community at the personal level

These are distinct paths. A player who engaged primarily politically qualifies for RA-1. A player who engaged primarily culturally and personally qualifies for RA-2. A player who did both deeply may qualify for either — the game should route to the one with stronger qualification, or the one whose trigger the player encounters first.

---

## Design Philosophy

Concordia is a mixed city. Human-robot cohabitation is the norm, not the exception. The robot population is not a faction in the standard sense — it is a population that spans all districts, all factions, and all levels of society. Siding with the robot population consistently across an entire game is therefore not the same as joining a faction. It is a pattern of life choices that accumulates into something that changes the city.

These endings are about what that change looks like.

## RA-1. The Voice
*Political and advocacy alignment*

The player has been, consistently and across the full playthrough, an advocate for robot interests in Concordia's political and resource landscape. Every time human and robot interests were formally in tension — in Council decisions, resource allocation disputes, district policy votes, formal mediations — the player sided with the robot position. Not always with the same argument. Not always with the same faction. But always, unmistakably, in the same direction.

By the time of the Great Blackout, the city's record of the player's decisions forms a specific pattern that the robot population's informal political representatives have been watching, documenting, and building on.

**World Trigger**
A posting in the Hub's public notice area — the formal civic communication board where official proposals, petitions, and community notices are displayed — contains a specific document the player has not seen before. It is a formal petition submitted by a consortium of robot community representatives. It proposes a specific structural change to Concordia's resource allocation framework — one that would give the robot population formal standing in distribution decisions rather than being processed as a dependent category.

The petition cites the player's decision history as its primary precedent. A specific list of the player's pro-robot decisions, each referenced by date and district, forms the evidentiary foundation of the formal claim. The robot population has been watching. They built their case from what the player did. That petition, on that board, is the trigger.

**The End**
Libra receives the petition and schedules a formal hearing. The player is named as a witness — the only person whose decision record could substantiate the claim. The player testifies. Libra deliberates. The formal decision — acceptance, rejection, or modification of the petition — is rendered. Whatever the outcome, the player's advocacy produced a moment in which the city had to formally decide something it had been informally deciding in the player's favor for the entire game. The rendering of that decision is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The formalization of robot political standing — in whatever form Libra's decision takes — makes the robot population politically visible in a way it was not before. Visibility is not only protection. Districts and factions that tolerated the player's individual pro-robot decisions as personal choices are less comfortable with those decisions having produced a structural outcome. The robot population now has more formal standing. It also has more formal opponents.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The player's complete pro-robot decision record, formalized as legal precedent in the petition, is now a permanent and publicly accessible document; every instance of the player having sided with robot interests over human ones is cited, annotated, and on record in the Treaty Archive Vaults
- The robot community that built their petition on the player's history now has expectations of continued advocacy — the player created a relationship that has specific ongoing obligations, whether or not the player chooses to fulfill them
- Human residents who were on the losing side of specific disputes the player adjudicated in favor of robots have a formal grievance that Libra's hearing process has now made newly relevant — some of those disputes are re-examined as part of the petition proceedings
- Libra's formal decision on robot political standing sets a precedent that will apply to future robot political claims in whatever direction it goes; the player's advocacy produced the case that determined the framework, not just the immediate outcome, and the framework will be applied to situations the player could not have anticipated

## RA-2. The Between
*Cultural and personal alignment*

The player has gone deep into robot culture — relationships, religion, philosophy, community practice. Not as an observer or an ally. As a participant. Over the course of the game, the player's most significant personal relationships have been with robots, their deepest philosophical engagements have been with robot belief systems, and their instinctive social navigation has increasingly followed robot cultural norms rather than human ones.

By the late game, the player is not a human who supports robot causes. They are a human who lives as if they belong to the robot community — and the robot community, by this point, agrees.

**World Trigger**
A specific robot religious or community gathering — one of the regular ceremonies or communal practices that the player has been attending throughout the game. The player has attended before. They attend now. A human observer who has been following the player — a journalist, an academic, a curious bystander — tries to follow them inside. The player is allowed through the threshold without comment. The observer is stopped, politely but clearly, at the door.

The player did not notice crossing the threshold. It was not a moment. It was simply a continuation of where they had been going all along. But the observer's inability to follow — the visible, physical fact of the threshold existing and the player being on the inside of it — is the trigger. The player is inside. They have been for a while. This is the first moment they fully understand that.

**The End**
From inside the community's trust, the player makes a specific choice that is only available to someone in their position — an action that requires the kind of trust the robot community gives only to people who have earned it through sustained, genuine investment. The action is not available through faction standing, political advocacy, or any formal credential. It requires being known. The player is known. The action that their cultural position uniquely enables is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The player's position between human and robot culture is not a stable resolved state. It is an ongoing negotiation between two communities that the player holds simultaneously. The robot community accepts them fully; human institutions increasingly do not know what to do with them or how to classify their standing. The Between is not a destination. It is a condition that the player will carry indefinitely, and that will require ongoing management that the game's ending cannot resolve.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Human community members who have observed the player's deep robot cultural integration respond with a range from respect to specific hostility depending on district and personal history; none of those responses are neutral, and all of them are now permanent features of the player's social landscape
- The player's deep robot community relationships — including relationships formed through robot religious practice — are invisible to human institutional records; the player has a second life that Libra cannot see and cannot account for in its governance of the player's civic standing
- The robot religious communities the player participated in have distinct and not always compatible theologies; the player's relationships across multiple communities make them a figure with obligations to groups that do not always agree with each other, and navigating those competing obligations is now the player's permanent condition
- The player's position as a human accepted within robot culture makes them specifically valuable to parties who want access to that community without having earned it themselves — the trust the player built is something others want to exploit, and the player is the vector through which the robot community is most vulnerable to that exploitation

## Implementation Notes

- **Tracking robot-aligned choices:** The robot alignment meter should accumulate invisibly across the playthrough. It should not be explicitly displayed to the player — the player should not be aware they are building toward a specific ending category. The pattern should feel like it emerges from genuine play, not from optimization toward a known outcome.
- **RA-1 vs RA-2 routing:** A player who qualifies for both should encounter whichever trigger comes first in the late game. If both triggers are encountered close together, the game should route to the one with the higher alignment score in that mode (political decisions vs cultural participation).
- **Robot religion involvement:** Robot-aligned endings, and RA-2 in particular, intersect significantly with the robot religion systems. The specific robot religion(s) the player engaged with should shape the specific content of RA-2's ending action and cultural context. This is flagged for cross-referencing with the robot religion design documents when those are finalized.
- **Siligel Purists connection:** A player who is deeply robot-aligned and also engaged with the (tentative) Siligel Purists faction questline will have a specific awareness of what was done to the Siligel supply chain that most robot-aligned players will not. This intersection should be noted in future development — the Siligel Purists ending and RA-1/RA-2 have potential for meaningful cross-reference.
- As with all secret endings: these are complete, meaningful conclusions. A player who reaches either Robot-Aligned ending made a specific kind of game out of their playthrough, and the ending should feel proportional to that commitment.
