# Near-Pariah Endings

## Trigger Condition

These endings are available when the player holds **Hated or Vilified** reputation with **10, 11, or 12** of Concordia's 13 districts — close enough to universal condemnation to lose access to most of the city, but with 1–3 districts still maintaining a working relationship with them.

**This is not the Pariah condition.** The Pariah failsafes require all 13 districts at Hated or Vilified. Near-Pariah endings specifically require at least one remaining relationship — which is both the player's only resource and the defining shape of what is still possible.

**Wild Child does not count toward the hostile tally.** A district at Wild Child status (Idolized + Vilified simultaneously) is not counted among the hostile districts for Near-Pariah purposes. The remaining relationship(s) may be at any non-Hated/non-Vilified tier, including Neutral, Accepted, Liked, or Idolized — but not Wild Child, which creates its own category of complication.

---

## Design Philosophy

The Near-Pariah player is not the Pariah. They still have something. That something is thin, possibly fraying, possibly based on misunderstanding — but it is real. These endings are about what you can do when almost everything is gone but not quite everything.

Unlike the Pariah failsafes (which are about survival and escape with no allies), Near-Pariah endings involve the remaining relationship(s) actively. The surviving districts are resources to work with, not just neutral ground to hide in. The endings reflect what is still possible when you have 1–3 allies in a city of 13 districts where 10–12 want you dead.

The key dramatic tension: the remaining relationships are precious exactly because of how much was destroyed. And the city will pressure those relationships. These endings are not comfortable.

## NP-1. The Redeeming Ember
*One district remaining*

Twelve districts want you gone. One district has not joined the consensus — not because it loves you, not because it forgives you, but because it has a specific cultural or institutional reason it cannot participate in collective condemnation of someone who hasn't directly threatened it. The relationship is not warm. It is, by this point in the game, the only thing left.

**World Trigger**
A distress marker appears in the one remaining district — the kind a community posts when it needs help it knows it cannot get through official channels. Not addressed to the player by name. But its specifics — what is needed, under what conditions, with what risks — describe a job that only someone in the player's position could take. Too dangerous for anyone with a reputation to protect. Too publicly associated with the district for any neutral party to touch without choosing a side. A player in near-Pariah state, reading that marker, understands immediately that it is functionally addressed to them. That marker, on that community board or checkpoint wall, is the trigger.

**How it unfolds**
The job is real and the threat is real. It does not matter which district remains — Cancer, Sagittarius, Virgo, Pisces, any of them — the structure is the same: the district faces something that the consensus arrangement has made it uniquely vulnerable to, and the near-Pariah is uniquely qualified to address it. The quest is short. It does not require rebuilding the player's reputation or explaining their history. It requires doing the one specific thing the marker describes.

**The End**
The specific threat to the last district is resolved. The player, having completed it, makes the explicit choice to remain in the district's territory rather than leave. Not celebrated. Not forgiven. Present. The act of staying rather than walking away is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The district that accepted help from the near-Pariah is now permanently associated with them in the eyes of the other 12. Whatever pressure and political cost were previously directed at the player alone is now redirected — partially or entirely — onto the sheltering district. The player's survival became the district's political liability.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The help was genuine and the district's acceptance was pragmatic — the relationship becomes debt rather than trust, and debt in Concordia always accrues interest
- Other districts interpret the remaining relationship as a formal alliance, which it is not — the misreading produces specific political consequences the district did not intend and cannot easily correct
- Community members within the sheltering district who had wanted to join the consensus face specific internal frustration; the fracture between those who accepted the player's help and those who didn't doesn't heal quickly
- The player's continued presence draws sustained external attention to a district that had previously been somewhat protected by its relative neutrality — the Redeeming Ember's cost is paid by the district, not the player

## NP-2. The Necessary Devil
*Two rival districts remaining — each believes the player is secretly working against the other*

Eleven districts want you gone. Two districts haven't joined the consensus — and their reasons are different, possibly contradictory, and almost certainly based on a shared false assumption: each privately believes the player is working against the other one. Neither has acted against the player because each believes the player is their asset against a rival. The player knows this. The question is what to do with it before the window closes.

**World Trigger**
A specific inter-district transit corridor connects the two remaining friendly districts, passing through territory nominally controlled by a hostile district. Both friendly districts maintain patrol presence in that corridor even though neither fully controls it — each is there partly to keep an eye on what the other is doing. The result is a contested corridor that is, practically, accessible to someone both districts regard as an asset: neither side will stop the player moving through it, because each assumes the player is moving through it on their behalf. That specific corridor — accessible because of the mutual assumption, inaccessible to everyone else in the player's position — is the trigger. A player who maps their remaining relationships and explores the city's administrative geography will find it.

**How it unfolds**
The window is finite. Both districts are in contact with each other even while maintaining their separate relationships with the player — eventually, comparison will reveal the shared assumption. The player can exploit the window (use both relationships without ever clarifying), resolve it honestly (disclose the situation to both districts and attempt to broker something real), or use it to accomplish a specific third objective that neither district knew they were enabling. The choice determines the resolution.

**The End**
The player commits to one of the three approaches and executes it to a definitive conclusion. The moment of irreversible commitment — the action that closes off the other options — is the ending. The corridor that was the trigger becomes the location of the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The two rival districts, once they understand they were both holding the same false assumption, have a crisis of trust — not only with the player, but with each other. The near-Pariah's situation inadvertently damaged the working relationship between the only two districts that weren't hunting them. The city loses a specific inter-district relationship that was functional, however tense.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Whichever district felt more used by the dynamic may reconsider its position — potentially completing the Pariah condition against the player's wishes, or becoming a volatile quasi-ally rather than a stable one
- The corridor that was accessible because of the mutual assumption becomes specifically contested between the two districts once the assumption is gone — both are now focused on controlling it, which disrupts transit for everyone who used it
- The player's accumulated knowledge of both districts' internal workings — gathered during the period when each thought the player was their confidential asset — is now a liability: both districts know the player has it
- Hostile districts observing the disruption between the two formerly-friendly districts move to exploit it, worsening overall stability in the city at an already-critical moment

## NP-3. The Splinter Accord
*Three districts remaining*

Ten districts want you gone. Three districts have not joined the consensus — each for a different reason, with different politics, possibly incompatible values. Together, they represent enough of a functional bloc that something formal between them is conceivable. A near-Pariah with three remaining allies is not a Pariah — they are a badly positioned political operator with a thin but real hand to play.

**World Trigger**
In Concordia's administrative geography, every district's territorial edge meets others at specific junction points — maintenance crossings, transit hubs, utility junctions that were built to service multiple adjacent areas. A player who has navigated near-Pariah conditions across the full city long enough knows this geography well. The specific junction where the territorial edges of all three remaining friendly districts converge — a physical point that is simultaneously in all three districts' administrative sphere — is the trigger. It exists. It is findable through exploration and knowledge of the three remaining relationships. Reaching it and recognizing what it is opens the Splinter Accord path.

**How it unfolds**
Each of the three districts has a specific reason it has not joined the consensus. The Splinter Accord requires understanding all three reasons — not just knowing them, but finding the specific formulation of a mutual agreement that addresses each district's specific concern simultaneously. The player brokers between the three, using the junction as the meeting ground because it is the only space all three can technically access without entering hostile territory. The accord itself is modest: not a new faction, not a claim of authority, just a specific mutual agreement that formalizes what each district was already implicitly doing.

**The End**
Representatives of all three districts confirm the accord at the junction — a signed document, a verbal agreement witnessed by all three parties, or a specific coordinated action that functions as confirmation. The moment all three confirm is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The accord was organized around the player as its implicit center — the three districts' common ground is that they all still deal with the player. When the player is no longer present, the accord loses its organizing principle, and the three districts don't have enough else in common to sustain it independently. The accord was real while it lasted. It does not outlast the player.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The ten hostile districts now have a specific target: the three-district bloc. The accord consolidates opposition against it in ways that make each individual district's situation worse than it was before the formal agreement
- Each district joined for a different reason — and those reasons are not long-term compatible; the first internal dispute about priorities will reveal that the accord was held together by the player, not by shared values
- Libra is not party to the accord and cannot formally recognize it — the bloc exists and functions but has no institutional standing in the city's governance framework, which limits everything it can accomplish
- The player's position within the accord is permanently that of the outsider who brokered it, never a full member — a status that becomes increasingly uncomfortable as the accord develops needs the player cannot meet

## NP-4. The Crisis Pivot
*Any near-Pariah state (10, 11, or 12 hostile districts)*

The Great Blackout fires. Concordia's enforcement mechanisms — the patrol networks, the district communications, the political machinery sustaining the consensus against the player — collapse into crisis management. For the duration of the Blackout, 10–12 hostile districts are too occupied with survival to maintain active pressure on a single near-Pariah. The window is specific, short, and unlike anything the player has had access to since reaching near-Pariah state.

**World Trigger**
The Blackout itself is the environmental trigger — but its specific manifestation for the near-Pariah is a physical change in their immediate environment that makes the window undeniable. A checkpoint that goes dark and unmanned. A patrol that abandons its route for an emergency call. A locked gate that loses power and swings open. Whatever the specific physical change, it is the first moment since reaching near-Pariah state that the player is not actively constrained by enforcement. That specific physical opening — the unmanned checkpoint, the open gate, the empty patrol corridor — is the trigger. It is encountered organically during the Blackout regardless of where the player is.

**How it unfolds**
The near-Pariah's accumulated knowledge of 10–12 hostile districts — their patrol patterns, their vulnerabilities, their internal politics — is now momentarily actionable. The Blackout grants access that the near-Pariah state had closed. The Crisis Pivot is about identifying, during the window, the one specific action that the player's unique combination of near-Pariah knowledge and Blackout-granted access makes possible — and completing it before enforcement resumes. The action is not predetermined; it is chosen from whatever the player has been building toward.

**The End**
The player completes their chosen action before the crisis resolves and district enforcement resumes. The completion of that action — during the window, while the gate is still open — is the ending. What the city looks like when the lights come back on is the epilogue.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The near-Pariah's action during the Blackout was taken without coordination with any of the remaining friendly districts, which had their own crisis plans. Whatever the player accomplished, it disrupted, complicated, or rendered moot something the friendly districts were trying to do during the same window. The player used the crisis. The friendly districts paid part of the cost.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- When enforcement resumes after the Blackout, the player's actions during it are fully documented — every district that was too busy to watch during the crisis has the records afterward, and the window is closed
- The hostile districts that couldn't maintain pressure during the Blackout redirect their attention once the crisis ends; the near-Pariah condition is, if anything, more acute after the window than before it opened
- The remaining friendly districts reassess the value of their relationship with the player after learning what the player did during the crisis — not all of them reach the same conclusion, and at least one reassessment goes badly
- The specific action the player took during the Blackout was correct for the conditions of the Blackout and may not be correct for the city that exists afterward — the window revealed what the player was capable of and what they prioritized, which the remaining districts now know

## NP-5. The Controlled Descent
*Any near-Pariah state (10, 11, or 12 hostile districts)*

The trajectory is visible. 10–12 districts are already hostile. The remaining 1–3 relationships are under sustained pressure. Full Pariah status is coming regardless of what the player does to prevent it. The Controlled Descent is the recognition that choosing when and how the last relationships end is more valuable than trying to preserve them indefinitely — and that entering the full Pariah state with specific preparations in place is better than being pushed into it cornered and unprepared.

**World Trigger**
A specific Libra administrative document appears in a location the player passes through — a wanted poster, a broadcast transcript, or an enforcement notice that lists the remaining friendly districts as "pending" or "under review." It is not addressed to the player. It is the city's administrative record of what is coming next. A player who reads it understands that the consensus is already extending toward the last relationships, and that those relationships are going to end on Libra's schedule rather than the player's unless the player acts first. That document — in a specific corridor, on a specific board, in whatever form it takes — is the trigger.

**How it unfolds**
The Controlled Descent requires the player to identify and complete specific preparations before the last relationships tip: resources cached in locations that will be accessible in full Pariah state, routes mapped through districts that will shortly be hostile, specific information gathered while the remaining relationships still grant access. Each preparation is a physical action in the world — hiding something, finding something, establishing something. The descent itself — the final act that tips the last relationship to Hated or Vilified — is chosen by the player rather than forced on them.

**The End**
The player completes the specific preparatory action they identified as most critical — the thing that makes the full Pariah state navigable rather than terminal — and then commits the final act that completes the descent. The preparatory action's completion is the ending. What follows is the Pariah failsafe system, but entered from a position of preparation rather than desperation. Several Pariah failsafe paths become more accessible as a result of what was prepared during the Controlled Descent.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The city has now achieved unanimous condemnation of a single individual — completed without any district defecting from the consensus. The institutional machinery that accomplished this has been proven to work at full scale. Libra will use it again. The mechanisms built to manage the player become a permanent feature of Concordia's political toolkit.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- The last districts pressured into joining the consensus against the player were the most resistant — the pressure required to move them damaged Libra's relationship with each in ways that create specific resentments the city has to manage going forward
- The unanimous condemnation is a political achievement Libra claims full credit for, which inflates Libra's institutional confidence in coordinated district pressure as a legitimate governance tool — a confidence that will be applied to future targets
- The player's prepared descent means they enter the Pariah state with specific advantages their preparations enabled — but those advantages were assembled from the last remaining relationships, and those former allies know exactly what was taken from those relationships before they ended
- The demonstration that unanimous district condemnation is achievable and manageable creates a specific precedent: the city now knows it can do this, and will consider doing it again when the next individual accumulates sufficient hostility

## NP-6. The Sympathizer Network
*Any near-Pariah state (10, 11, or 12 hostile districts)*

Not every member of every hostile district agreed with joining the consensus against the player. Dissidents, internal critics, pragmatists, people with personal reasons — they exist in every district, and the near-Pariah's position as the focal point of unanimous condemnation makes them specifically visible to everyone who privately disagrees with that condemnation. These individuals cannot openly associate with the player. But they can quietly help: looking away at the right moment, leaving a door unlocked, passing information through channels that don't trace back.

**World Trigger**
A specific anonymous note appears in a location within hostile district territory — slipped under a door in an area the player passes through, tucked into a supply cache the player accesses, folded inside a maintenance log the player examines. It is addressed to no one. It contains specific, accurate information — about a patrol route, a resource location, a timing window — that could only have come from someone inside the hostile district who wanted to help. The information is actionable and costs nothing to use. The note is the trigger. It is evidence that the consensus is not unanimous even within the hostile districts, and that the sympathizer network exists and is already operating without the player having built it.

**How it unfolds**
Following the note's lead reveals additional nodes in the network — not a faction, not an organization, just individuals in separate hostile districts who are each, independently, doing small things that make the player's survival marginally easier. The network can be mapped by finding each node: a Virgo maintenance worker who leaves specific tunnel accesses unlocked, a Cancer caretaker who doesn't report the player's presence in their ward, a Scorpio archivist who provides information through an intermediary. Each node is a physical location the player can find and a physical act the player can observe or interact with. The network can be used or actively supported — the choice shapes the ending.

**The End**
The sympathizer network becomes functional enough that the player can accomplish a specific act — not escape, not survival, but something meaningful that the network enables and that the player could not have accomplished without it. The completion of that specific act is the ending.

**Main Negative Effect on Concordia**
The sympathizer network, once identified by the hostile districts — and it will be, because the player's use of it leaves traces — becomes a specific investigation target. The people who quietly helped are discovered. The consequences fall on them, not on the player. The near-Pariah's survival costs specific individuals within the hostile districts their standing, safety, and in some cases more.

**Minor Negative Effects**
- Each sympathizer who is identified and punished becomes a cautionary example — the network that formed around the player makes future dissent within those districts more costly, suppressing the very capacity for internal criticism that the network demonstrated existed
- Scorpio's therapeutic community faces an influx of people processing the moral weight of having helped or having failed to help — the near-Pariah becomes a city-wide ethical case study with ongoing psychological costs
- The information the sympathizers provided is traceable back to its sources; the investigation of the network follows the information chain, and the most useful nodes are the first ones identified
- The districts that most aggressively identified and punished sympathizers suffer specific internal trust damage — nobody knows who else was quietly helping, and the resulting paranoia affects internal communication and morale in ways that outlast the investigation itself

## Implementation Notes

- Near-Pariah endings are available **in addition to** the main story endings and other secret endings. A player who qualifies should be able to find at least one of these six paths regardless of which specific districts remain in their column.
- The **Controlled Descent** (NP-5) is the only Near-Pariah ending that explicitly bridges into the Pariah failsafe system. Players who take that path enter the full Pariah state prepared, and certain Pariah failsafes become more accessible as a result of preparations made during the Controlled Descent.
- **NP-1 through NP-3** are shaped by how many districts remain. The game should make it clear which count the player is at without explicitly labeling it — ambient dialogue, Janbogo broadcasts, and Libra administrative postings should all reflect whether one, two, or three districts haven't joined the consensus yet.
- **NP-4 through NP-6** are available at any near-Pariah count. They are the catch-all endings for players who don't fit neatly into the count-based categories or who reach near-Pariah state late in the game without a clean count.
- As with all secret endings: these are complete, meaningful conclusions — not punishment slides. A near-Pariah player who finds and pursues one of these paths should feel that their playthrough produced a real, distinct story.
